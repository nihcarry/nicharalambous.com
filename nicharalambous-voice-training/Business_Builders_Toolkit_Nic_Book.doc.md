**Introduction**

Without giving it too much thought in my younger years, I have ended up dedicating my life to building businesses. With each failure (and there have been many), I was honing my craft and with each success, I was gathering the momentum I needed to keep building and learning. Being a lifetime entrepreneur wasn’t a conscious choice that I made. I started my first business when I was 16 so I can’t claim to have been self-aware at that age. I was simply doing what the teachers told me to do in business economics class. So I started my first business and from that day on, I was hooked. 

That business wasn’t anything special. In grade 10 at school for one term, we were asked to start a small business. The premise was fairly simple; take R500 and spend it on starting your business and by the end of the term the person with the most profit would be crowned the winner of the business competition. So I went to the bank (my parents) and asked them for a small loan of R500. It was up to us to figure out how much money we wanted to borrow to then try and make it back over the term. I opted for the mid-range of R500 so that I could give myself a good chance of making back the initial investment and then profit on top of that. Keep in mind, this is 16-year-olds building businesses so we weren’t too fussed about net vs gross profit margins. Just taking a loan and turning it into more money than we borrowed. 

So I took the R500 and purchased Greek worry beads to sell to my fellow students to keep their stress levels low while we were learning in class. The product sold like crazy and by the middle of the term, my business had been banned by the headmaster because too many kids in class were clanging and clacking their worry beads and distracting the teachers. So I was done and had made a small profit. I didn’t win the competition that term but the entrepreneurial bug had bitten me. 

Building businesses is a way of life for me. It’s how I have defined myself, my work, my identity and everything else for two decades. But building businesses is hard and lonely work. It’s often thankless work that has, in recent times, been glorified as the work of the gods. But you and I know better. It’s not the work of the gods done in the heavens, it’s more like the work of the insane done in the trenches, wading through shit looking for a magical diamond that we aren’t even sure exists, can’t see and aren’t convinced we 100% believe in. It’s messy, it’s wonderful, it’s dangerous and difficult, it’s brutal, it’s intense, it’s rewarding and soul-crushing and it’s the only way I know how to exist. You get that, right? You understand where I’m coming from. That’s why I started writing a newsletter in partnership with the *Daily Maverick* back in June 2020; because building businesses is gut-wrenchingly hard but even though we feel like it most of the time, we’re not actually alone. We’re all building things together, around each other, near each other with and for each other all the time without realising it. 

A couple of months after the first official COVID-19 induced lockdown in South Africa started, the *Daily Maverick* CEO, Styli Charalambous (yes, that is his real surname and yes, mine is Haralambous but no, we’re not related despite both of us having impeccably thick beards), and I were having a conversation. In passing, I mentioned that the *Daily Maverick* was producing incredible pandemic-related content but should consider covering more small and medium-sized (SME) business news to help SMEs through the tough COVID-19 fallout they were experiencing. To which Styli replied: “Are you open to writing something too, a regular piece?” and that was that, the SME Toolkit was born.

# **Busting business jargon**

When you start anything new there is a learning curve. Some of these curves are steeper than others but one thing that always holds people back is industry-specific jargon. I hate business jargon. In fact, I hate any language that is specifically used to make people feel dumb or incompetent. Many years ago I worked at a very large mobile network and for the first few months that I attended meetings, I kept hearing this string of letters used in sentences that baffled me. Meeting after meeting I sat and pondered what the hell an MSISDN was (pronounced kind of like “mizdin”). Then someone threw KPI into the mix and I was so frustrated with the acronyms and jargon that I blurted out a desperate request for anyone to please explain what a KPI was and while they were at it, tell me what an MSISDN was. 

As many people will know, KPI stands for Key Performance Indicators but very few people will know that an MSISDN is what mobile networks call a phone number. Why they don’t just say phone number is beyond me. 

The day I blurted out the seemingly stupid question changed me forever. I decided there and then to try and stop using jargon when normal words would work and to ask the dumb questions when no one else would. 

I want to help bust basic business jargon to try and flatten that learning curve for you. It’s hard enough building a profitable business; why do we need to navigate unnecessarily complex words at the same time? No more\!

### Jargon to lose

* **I don’t have the bandwidth** – If you mean you are too busy, say that. A better idea is to try and figure out why you have no "bandwidth" and then structure your to-do list a bit better. 

* **Viral** – What exactly do you mean when you ask for a video to go “viral”? Most of us understand this to mean that a piece of content spreads quickly online but you don’t just “make something go viral” – it’s a complex and often expensive process to get that shareability. 

* **Growth hacking** – A relatively new field of marketing that focuses on growth. Theoretically, all marketing is about growth so I’m not sure it’s entirely necessary to single this one out. 

* **Disruptive** – If you have to describe your product or service as disruptive, it likely isn’t. Disruptive businesses and products speak for themselves. 

* **Make it pop** – You want it to stand out, you want it to be different but making something "pop" means very little to the people working with you. How, *exactly*, do you want this thing to pop? Be more descriptive than that, be more specific than that. Spell out your vision as clearly as you can and don’t be a vague prima donna vague. 

* **Pivot** – In startup-land, the term “pivot” is widely used and well known but recently this term has become popular as many businesses are “pivoting to survive”. Pivoting doesn’t mean throwing out your business, revenue model and existing team. It *can* mean that, but it could also mean a slight shift from what you have been doing to something similar that appeals to a different target market. 

* **Mission-critical** – At this point in our pandemic journey everything and anything is or could be mission-critical. If everything is urgent, nothing is urgent, so please use this term only if one particular thing is truly critical to the survival of your latest mission. 

### Jargon to keep

* **CPA** – CPA stands for Cost Per Acquisition. How much does it cost you to acquire a new customer? This is an important metric to watch if you are spending money on advertising. 

* **CLV** – Customer Lifetime Value refers to how much revenue you generate from a customer over the period that they remain a customer. This is an important number in relation to your CPA.  
     
* **ROI** – The Return On Investment is an important figure to understand, as much as it is an irritating bit of jargon. If you spend R10 acquiring a customer, the ROI should be more than the R10 you spent (CPA) to acquire that customer. This is used in relation to a CLV too. 

* **Cash flow** – The lifeblood of any business, cash flow refers to how much money is going into and out of your business. If you have more money going out than coming in, you have negative cash flow and your business is in trouble.   
    
  ***For a deeper dive into cash flow management, head over to Section 3\.*** 

* **Cash flow statement** – The cash flow statement shows the movements of cash and cash equivalents in and out of the business. The cash flow statement is an important tool for evaluating business health.

* **Net profit vs gross profit** – Gross profit is the total number remaining after subtracting revenue from the cost of goods sold. This does not include operating expenses. Net profit is the gross profit (revenue minus cost of goods sold) minus operating expenses and all other expenses. Zero net profit means no business. 

# **Contracts & agreements**

Businesses are started for myriad reasons. Some people turn their hobbies into businesses, others aim to serve the common good, while still others set out with a specific idea and objective. There is no right or wrong way to get your business started but there are lots and lots of legally binding contracts and agreements that every business owner will encounter on their journey. 

I find contracts overwhelming. I am not particularly good at reading them and I certainly cannot write them. I struggle to sit and ingest the many pages that do matter. I read the words but often they don’t go into my brain, forcing me to read the sentences again and again. 

Agreements are, however, vital to the survival and growth of your business. Whether you are signing a lease agreement, a partnership agreement, an employment contract, a non-disclosure agreement or your company documents you have to get comfortable with the process. 

I think the worst thing that founders and business owners can do is assume that all parties are on the same page without a written and signed agreement. Everyone is on the same page until they are not. Write it down, sign it, digitise it (so you don’t lose it) and keep it safe.

Most business owners see contracts as a drag but you can reframe them as the bedrock of your business or relationship. The aim is for each party to leave feeling that they won something and lost something but that the process was fair. In most cases, if you have to refer to the contract, the relationship is probably in trouble. 

### Memorandum of Incorporation (MOI)

The MOI is the most important document governing a company. This agreement sets out the rights, duties and responsibilities of shareholders, directors and other people involved in the company. Every company registered in South Africa needs to have an MOI. This is the document that you will refer to when things go really bad with your partners, shareholders or directors.

### Shareholders Agreement

Shareholders of a company are able to enter into an agreement with one another concerning just about any matter related to the company. However, it is imperative to understand that the shareholders agreement (SHA) must be consistent with the MOI that exists for the company. If there are any inconsistencies, the MOI takes precedence. Any aspects of the relationship between shareholders (and sometimes directors) not covered in the MOI must be covered in the shareholders agreement.  It’s imperative that once you have created the SHA and MOI all parties must sign these documents. Shareholders must also be issued with actual share certificates which can be handled by your accountants or lawyers. 

### Referral Agreement

If you have a product or service that someone else would like to promote or sell on your behalf, you will need a referral agreement to ensure that both parties are treated the right way. These agreements can cover things like the amount paid to the referrer, the rights given to the referrer in terms of the product, branding and distribution and for how long the referral is valid. 

### Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA)

This is my least favourite agreement for SMEs. NDAs are used to ensure that information, secrets, tools, tricks, tips and any “special sauce” that one company divulges as part of a working relationship is not used or communicated outside the working relationship. The complicated issue with NDAs is proving that the information being disclosed is unique and that the agreement represents the first time that the receiving party has been exposed to it.

In general I refuse to sign NDAs without serious and robust justification, because they can lead to more trouble than they are worth. Presenting an NDA to the right partner in the appropriate context is great. Using them as a generic way to protect your “unique IP” that is neither unique nor intellectual property can come across as condescending and is often unnecessary. 

### Memorandum of Understanding

MOUs are not legally binding. An MOU is an agreement between two parties that is outlined in a formal document that states the terms of the agreement. The terms in the MOU are negotiated and debated in the MOU before entering them into a legally binding agreement. Usually the MOU establishes the intent of two parties planning to do more business together. 

### Service Level Agreement (SLA)

Usually, when two businesses enter into a relationship one of them is providing a service to the other. Sometimes this service is exactly what you pay for but sometimes it is not. The SLA is an agreement that governs the service offered and paid for. If at any point in the contracted relationship the service level drops below what was agreed, the agreement kicks in and each party will use the SLA as the benchmark. If the agreement is a good one, it will outline the measures required to resolve the issue and the penalties that will be incurred if the service does not improve.

## **Finances**

## Some of the basics

### Invoicing

As you start making sales, you can do the invoicing yourself using templates in Microsoft Word or Google Docs. But it can become overwhelming to manage invoicing as your business grows. This is a fantastic problem to have but is a genuine concern when you are paying suppliers late or you forget to send invoices out at all. Something so simple can lead to very real pain for your bottom line.

### Payroll

With fewer than five team members it’s easy to pay salaries and manage leave. However, as your business brings on more people, things like PAYE, UIF, leave allocation and the complexities of managing your team’s finances can wreak havoc. If you can afford it, let someone else do it for you. Remember, there are people in the world who enjoy this kind of work, even if you don’t. 

### Accounting

Budgeting, managing profit and loss, creating annual financial statements, income statements and all the other complex parts of running a business can mark the breaking points for many entrepreneurs, myself included. The more experience I gained, the more I realised that it’s good to know the basics but it’s better to let an expert fine-tune these things. 

### Strategic thinking

An often overlooked part of finance is the strategy behind your business model. Early on, as the founder you simply grow your business in the direction of the sale. As your business evolves it’s important to find brilliant minds that are financially oriented. More often than not, founders battle with the nuance of finance and as your business grows, financial strategic thinking matters as much as any other part of your business. 

## The four basic stages of finance in a business

To begin with, you need to identify which stage of financial management you find yourself in. The four phases below are guidelines. The context matters; not every business is the same. 

### The “do it in a spreadsheet” phase

At this stage, you should be tracking your finances on a weekly or monthly basis using a very simple spreadsheet. Tracking your income and expenses is the most important thing you’ll need but remember that your spreadsheet is really only as good as the data in it.

### The “do it in the cloud” phase

There are many solutions online that you can choose from that will make your accounting and invoicing more automated than the spreadsheet phase. These online tools, or “cloud tools” as they’re known, can plug into your bank account, pull through transactions and even help you with invoicing and hour tracking. The beauty of moving into this phase is that if done right, your outsourced CFO can simply dive into the tool of your choice and get started. 

### The “outsource an accountant” phase

Once you have acquired a few clients, you are invoicing often every month, you have a few key staff members, need to manage payroll and you have more on your plate than you can handle, it’s time to get yourself a real accountant. You can either find an individual who is looking to pick up a few clients and can dedicate some of their time each day to your needs. Or you can turn to various companies that offer this service. A simple search online for an “outsourced CFO” will yield results to investigate. 

### The “hire a CFO” phase

The final phase is the big one – the point at which you can no longer manage with a part-time accountant or outsourced CFO. You have more staff than you can manage yourself, you have a core executive team and need a finance whizz to join your business and think more strategically about how to make more money, where to save it and work strategically on your business model with you. 

### Outsource or build a financial team?

The important part of figuring out whether to hire and build a team or outsource indefinitely is identifying which phase your business is in. Another variable to consider is your core competency as well as that of your team. If you are suited to a more financial role and are happy to take it upon yourself and relinquish control of other aspects of the business, this could be a good move for you. However, if your core skill is sales but you keep dropping the ball on invoicing or paying suppliers, you need to bring in help. 

If your business can afford a full-time salary for a team member to do basic accounting and bookkeeping and you think you want to continue to grow your team, it’s probably a good idea to start to build an internal finance team. However, if you have no real intention to expand your staff (avoiding financial complications that come with HR overhead), you can easily work with an outsourced or part-time accountant.

# **Validating ideas**

Whether you are currently running a business and thinking about launching a new product or side hustle for yourself, or you are thinking about selling and marketing your first idea, you’ll know that new ideas can be scary. 

How do you know if it’s a good idea? What if nobody likes it? What if nobody wants to spend money on the product or service you’re thinking about launching? Where do you even begin to find answers to these and the many more questions you have tumbling through your mind?

Validation is a combination of experience in a specific industry or market, a gut feel that you have and market research but there are many different ways to validate a new product or idea and I’m going to cover a few of them below. 

It’s important to remember that your idea is yours and the validation you are seeking is not intended to dissuade you from building something but to confirm that there is a glint of hope that if you spend time building this new idea, you aren’t wasting your time and money on something that is a non-starter. Don’t take any of the feedback personally, you asked for people to comment so listen when they do but also realise that you may see a gap that nobody else does or you may have a problem to solve that few in your immediate circle share, but the market is still out there. 

### Make a sale

Whether you have a physical version of your product or are ready to actually start selling your service, the best way to validate an idea is to find one customer. Try and find just one person willing to part with money in exchange for the thing you are thinking about launching. 

If you have an existing customer database then you’re halfway there already. Split out a small portion of your most active customers and email them your secret new idea and allow them to pre-order. If you receive orders then you’re in the money\!

If you don’t have an existing database, you’re in luck because you have potential customers all over your social media accounts like Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn and even the WhatsApp groups you’re in. Send a post or message to people and tell them a bit about what you are planning and that if they hurry they can get a pre-order in if they contact you. Gauge the interest and see if there’s demand. 

### Research the market

I am always stunned at how few people have done any actual research when they come to me with a new idea. It’s not particularly hard to do... open up www.google.com and start searching for products, terms, ideas and phrases similar to yours and see what comes up. Allow yourself to dive into the rabbit hole and get lost for a few hours. Save links to similar products or services, find inspiration and even pick up on things you might have missed with your own idea. 

Do the research, it’s very likely that there is someone trying something similar, but that’s not a bad thing. Competition validates a market desire and then you can start to figure out what your unique selling proposition is compared to your competitors. 

***In Section 5 I cover in more detail methods of getting to know your customers and market research methods to help you better understand your target audience.*** 

### Competitor analysis

This is one of the most traditional ways to validate an idea, product or business. Start trying to find competitors and when you do (because you will), compare them and figure out what the gaps are that you can fill. 

Are your competitors too expensive? Is their service delivery poor? Have they stagnated and lack new designs or updated versions that people are begging for? Can you do what they do but simpler? Better? Faster? More often?

This is a fantastic way to figure out if customers actually exist and if you can dive in and eat your competitor’s lunch. 

### Anecdotal conversations

Your gut reaction when you have an idea is likely to hold on to it very tightly and protect it from the world until it’s ready to launch. That’s a huge mistake. You’re going to hold onto it so tightly that you suffocate the life out of it and bury it before it’s lived any kind of life. 

This validation method is more of a slow-burn that you can carefully execute over weeks and even months. In standard conversation with people in your life, slowly start talking about the problem you are solving or the gap that you are filling with your new idea. Engage with colleagues in those awkward few minutes before everyone joins the zoom call or talk to your friends and family once in a while about it and listen carefully to their responses. If they share your problem and are looking for a solution you might be onto something. 

### Talk to actual customers

It’s important to identify a very specific customer profile that you have in mind for this new idea. Once you’ve done that, go out and find them and talk to them about your idea and their needs. In the post-Covid world, video calls are common, so post on your social media that you want help validating an idea and the first ten people who offer to help on a call will receive a discount when you launch. 

# **Exposing business lies**

It has become trendy to want to “be your own boss” and to “own your own time,” but these little quips trick most people into believing that being an entrepreneur is fun and easy and give you freedom beyond your wildest dreams. 

Pfffft. That’s a lie. 

Being an entrepreneur and being a parent are relatively similar callings. You bring a child (business) into the world, you nurture it, you tell the world about it, you pay school fees to learn some hard lessons and you are proud of the thing you have created. 

But there’s another similarity that I’ve noticed recently. Parents like to tell people without children how amazing it is to have children and that it’s the best thing in the world. Then you have a child and those same parents turn around and reveal their cruel trick – parenting is hard, raising kids is hard, operating on very little sleep is hard, maintaining a successful relationship while trying to keep your child alive is also hard. It’s hard to be a parent. 

It’s also hard to be an entrepreneur and I see the same trends emerging: entrepreneurs who shout from the digital hilltops that being self-employed is amazing, running your own business is the best thing you can do for your life and other lies that trick people into thinking everyone should be an entrepreneur and run their own business. This kind of entrepreneur is trying desperately to validate their own life decisions by hooking other people in and dragging them down to a similar level so that you can all suffer together. 

Then when you start your business, those same confident entrepreneurs who convinced you to join their ranks suddenly appear to be burned out, broken and disillusioned. Misery loves company so make sure your decisions are being made for the right reasons and not someone else’s reasons. 

### More free time

“Be your own boss, you can take leave whenever you want.” Wow, that’s one helluva lie that we tell as entrepreneurs. 

The truth is that since I became an entrepreneur I have taken significantly less leave than I ever would have if I stayed employed full time at a large company. When you own the business, when you have skin in the game, when your next paycheck depends on your output you suddenly feel like you have to work more, not less. 

Your weekends are consumed by thinking about work, thinking about ways to make more money, to evolve your product, to meet new clients and on and on. It’s never-ending. 

But it has to end. You need that free time to work at your best so don’t ignore the needs of your mind and body. Take a break, you and your team will be better off. 

For a deeper dive into your mental health, burnout and digital fatigue, jump to Section 09\. 

### Funding solves everything 

You don’t need funding, you need sales. 

There is a fairly large portion of entrepreneurs who seem to believe that they are owed some form of funding. Whether it’s a loan from a bank, venture capital at a good price or a government handout we seem to believe that it is our right to raise money and that’s how businesses are built. 

Funding does not solve problems, it highlights them.

What is the best form of funding? Customers. When your customer pays you for something that you are selling, it's the best form of funding because that form leads to profit and shows traction. 

You need to focus more on finding the right customer and selling to them and forget about raising funding, getting a loan or having someone bail you out. Funding is not a silver bullet to success. 

As a rule of thumb, if you are trying to raise funding, you need to allocate at least 50% of your time to finding the funding, negotiating the deal and securing the partners. It’s not a part-time job. I know many founders who have shifted their entire focus to fundraising because they have a support structure that allows them to do so. Even then, many fail to raise funds and their business collapses because they weren’t out making sales. 

### A small part of a big pie

This is one of my favourite business lies. “If I just capture 2% of a billion-dollar market, I’ll be rich.” Said every startup founder ever. 

It’s a lie. It’s a trap. It’s rubbish. Why do we want to scale our businesses to the point where we own 5-10% of our own business but it’s valued at R100m? Why do we think this is better than owning 90-100% of a R10m business? 

I’ll tell you. Ego is the reason. We scale because we think we must scale. We want a bigger valuation because people will think we are amazing. 

Do you know what I think is amazing? A reasonable, profitable and sustainable business that won't put me in hospital after severe burnout. I want R1m profit per year and I want to own the entire business. Less admin, less headache, less investor documentation and less complexity. 

Sometimes you don’t need a big pie, you just need to own all of your own pie. 

### Only I can do XYZ

Entrepreneurs are nothing if not confident. Business leaders and managers whether it’s real or not will often ooze confidence that baffles their teams. This confidence is often just arrogance that renders the leader in question unable to delegate and systematise a business. 

It’s easy to just do it yourself but that doesn’t scale. It’s easy to justify holding on to tasks when you know you can do it faster, better, more effectively than anyone else in the business. But that doesn’t scale. 

The misguided view that only you can do something in your business stems from one of two places; either you are the best and most effective human in the history of the world and you literally can do everything better than anyone … or your hiring practices are bad and your systems are terrible. 

You pick. 

*“Strong opinions loosely held”* 

# 02\. PLANNING & STRATEGY

The number of business plans written versus business built must be staggeringly out of balance. Most people love to plan. Planning is essential, of course. But plans are mostly irrelevant. Why? Well, as Prussian Field Marshal Helmuth von Moltke the Elder so efficiently put it, “no plan survives first contact with the enemy.” This rings true in life and in business. You can plan until you’ve run out of cloud hosting to store the documents and diagrams but until you are building, launching and engaging with your customers you have no idea if your plan will work (and most often it doesn’t work at first). 

That is not to say that planning and strategy are not relevant to a business. Without a doubt, it’s important to know in what direction you are moving and what your strategy is for when you arrive at your destination. 

Agility and resilience are fast becoming the most defining traits of successful modem businesses.

# PLANNING FOR UNCERTAINTY

In a world filled with uncertainty and doubt, a world that is more upside down than it is right-side-up, is there any value in laying out a plan for your business? I think so.

One of my Nicisms is *strong opinions, loosely* *held*. This applies directly to the requirement for your business to survive in the world that now exists. It is the act of planning and the motion it creates that is imperative. 

Standing and remaining still in a fast-moving global economy is the kiss of death. Planning helps you stay on your toes. Planning gives you and your team the first step towards the next phase of your business. Plans must be malleable. Plans must act as a guide and not a blueprint. Plans must be started but relentlessly challenged, questioned and re-evaluated, not protected at all costs.

Here are some things you can and should be doing to create better plans and give yourself a better chance at building the kind of business you deserve. 

### Be focused

It’s hard to write an entirely new plan for every aspect of your business. So once you have a clear idea of what you did well and what you did badly, consider the following areas in your planning and focus on the ones you know can move the needle for your business:

* Marketing  
* Branding  
* Sales  
* Operations  
* Customer service  
* Team Retention and Upliftment

There is no way that a small team can dramatically improve upon every aspect of the business, so choose your focus, order and prioritise them and then get to work. 

### Review your year

Reviews are not meant to point fingers or lay blame. It’s important to look back on the insanity of the past 12 months and figure out what you liked, where you excelled and what you sucked at. Ask yourself and your team the following four questions and then sit down together and review each person’s answers: 

* What did we do extremely well?  
* What did we do terribly?  
* What would we like to do more of?  
* What would we like to do less of?

Before the pandemic descended on the planet, many businesses wrote plans for how they wanted 2020 to play out. A lot of these businesses didn’t update their plans or adapt to the dramatic shift that occurred that year. If you still have that plan somewhere it’s a good idea to bring it out and see how far off you were and how well you adapted to the crazy world you were presented with. 

Reviews don’t have to happen at the end of a calendar or financial year. You can (and absolutely should) review your plan on a monthly basis these days. Look at the high-level points, review the direction in which you are heading and the one you thought you would be heading in and adjust accordingly. Bring your team in and make them part of the process. They see things you won't. 

### Action your review

Once you have discussed the answers to the simple annual review questions you can start to see very clearly where you came short and where you and your team thrived. Include this feedback in your plans for the next year. What did you suck at? How can you improve this failing and where did the mistakes occur? Now you are starting to see a plan emerge by answering four simple questions.  

### Don’t focus on the negative

It’s easy to be critical of ourselves and our businesses. If you focus exclusively on the things you missed, the mistakes you made and the massive deviation from your original plan, you will force yourself and your team into a depression. Remember, plans are meant to be agile and you can’t control what happens in the world. Teams should be judged on their ability to adapt, not on how close to the plan they remained. If the facts change, change your plan. If the world changes, change with it. 

### Avoid detail

The best plans set out a big hairy audacious goal (BHAG) and allow the smart and talented people in the team to figure out the most effective and efficient way to get there. But don’t put too much detail into your plan upfront, or people will be nervous and unwilling to deviate from it, no matter how much damage it may be causing. 

If you focus on the high level, on the BHAG, and everyone agrees on where they want to end up, it should matter less how you get there. 

### Gain perspective

It’s impossible to read the label from inside the bottle and for this reason, it can be a good idea to bring someone into your planning session who is familiar with your business but not attached to it. This person can help you with perspective and keep you driving towards action while not feeling guilting or personally attacked for any particular mistakes made. 

### Know that new is not better

Planning doesn’t mean new. Just because it’s a new year doesn’t mean you need an entirely new plan. Your strategy for the new year might be to continue doing what you did in the year that just passed but to scale up operations. That’s great. Now the nuance of the plan is focused on how you scale, not what you do next to shake things up. 

Each business has unique challenges to uncover and work through and each individual in a team sees these challenges in different ways. You don’t need to build something new to improve your business or grow. You don’t need new people to bring new ideas. Often you need space to let ideas flow and to let people feel comfortable, to be honest. 

### Drop the ego

Planning is not about winning. Planning is not about figuring out who made the best decisions and earned the most money. Planning should be an ego-free task that requires the entire team to come together, be honest and focus on the next period of growth or survival for the good of the business – not the good of the individual. 

Everything is simpler without ego and plans are trusted if everyone is involved in their formation.

# PLANNING FOR FAILURE

The word “hope” has been used more since 2020 than at any time I can recall. We must have hope that our economy will improve. We must have hope that the politicians will do what is right. We must have hope that our businesses will survive this crisis and hope that this pandemic will finally come to an end. 

I understand our requirement for hope in our lives. Hope inspires us to push forward and keep going. We are moved to action and avoid negativity when we hope. I get it. 

But **hope is not a strategy** and sometimes things go wrong. Perhaps it was the wrong product, perhaps the marketing planning and execution lacked rigour, perhaps the market just wasn’t there?

The fact remains, businesses can and do fail and no amount of hope can prevent this. 

So once this happens and hope is gone, what to do? Despair looms. The sense of failure is intense – your employees are distressed and your customers disappointed. 

This is a time for pragmatism and not despair, as understandable as the emotion is. When you feel despair you believe that nothing you can do will make a difference. It’s difficult at that point to see that your actions can actually have a massive impact on the outcome, as tough as it is. 

This section is focused on what to do when you think your business might not make it. It’s a strange place to begin but I like to plan for divorce (or failure) when thinking about getting married. 

If no one has said this to you before, let me be the first: you are not a failure because your business failed. Businesses fail all the time and since 2020 this feels more common than in the previous two decades. Denying your struggle will only make it worse, so keep reading, be as practical as you can and make the hard decisions when you must. 

It’s easy to feel overwhelmed when things look bad. Your gut reaction is to hide and ignore the difficult decisions that need to be made. Don’t do that. Be practical, plan ahead and think of the best strategy you can to save money, downscale, survive and recover. 

### Business rescue

As soon as you realise that your business is in financial distress it’s time to consider business rescue. This is a way for directors and shareholders to attempt to rescue and recover their business in partnership with business rescue practitioners.

Once you have applied for business rescue with the CIPC, a business rescue practitioner will be appointed (or you would have selected one) to work with the directors of the business on a plan to save it. 

Business rescue can be initiated by:

* The board of directors;  
* By an application to court when the business is financially distressed;  
* Various affected persons by application to the court (including shareholders, creditors, registered trade unions and employees).

To file for business rescue, print and complete form [CoR 123.1](http://www.cipc.co.za/index.php/download_file/view/116/975/) document, scan and email the completed and signed documents together with supporting information to businessrescue@cipc.co.za and submit the required documentation. For more details, visit the CIPC website. 

For further information, visit cobra.org.za, a pro-bono service for South African businesses in distress that provides plenty of advice on how to coordinate bank, government and stakeholder support through a structured business-rescue process.

### Liquidation

Liquidation is a scary term for any business owner. This becomes a possibility the moment that your liabilities (what you owe) are greater than your assets (what you have). 

At this point you are technically insolvent and your business must cease trading. This is a difficult moment because you may feel you are on the verge of becoming solvent again. 

There are two types of liquidation.

* Involuntary – when a creditor (someone you owe money to) applies for your liquidation.   
* Voluntary – when you elect to go into liquidation. In this instance you retain control of the process, which is always preferable. 

The resolution to liquidate must be filed with the CIPC by filing the CoR40.1 with supporting documents. For more detailed information visit the CIPC website. 

### Rental remission

If you have a store or office space and believe that you cannot pay rent in the coming months, you should immediately and aggressively communicate with your landlord. 

It is highly unlikely that landlords will completely waive your rent but that’s a good place to start negotiations. The more plausible outcome is that your landlord will provide you with what is called a rental remission. An important concept to grasp when it comes to rental remissions is *force majeure –* a superior power or force that cannot be resisted or controlled.

\<Sidebar\>  
**What I learned**   
When I was running my fashion company, Nic Harry, one of our stores was under severe pressure because the newly renovated Menlyn Park mall was tanking. There was no foot traffic; there were dead zones all over the mall; near our store we had what is known as mall-rot – empty stores that lead to lower foot traffic which leads to closing stores and more empty hallways for people to avoid. It was (still is) the zombie apocalypse for retail malls.   
I approached the landlord and was rebuffed. Low store turnover was *our* problem, not theirs. Lack of sales was a sales problem, not a mall problem.   
I flew up to Pretoria once a month for six months, trying to meet the landlord, who avoided and ignored me at every turn.   
Eventually, I threatened them with media exposure, investigative journalists and other dramatic methods – and that got their attention. We were never given rental remission and the landlord never took ownership for the terrible mall performance but I did manage to find a way out of my lease by finding a bigger retailer who could take on the rent and survive, because they wanted the location for the long term.   
The point is this: cut quick and cut deep. If you see a problem in your stores, in your office rentals or your warehouses, bring them up quickly and be forceful with your view. Landlords don’t care about tenants.   
\<end of sidebar\>

### Retrenchments & HR

One of the more complicated parts of winding up a business is how you go about retrenching staff. This is not as simple as giving them notice that you are liquidating. 

As an employer, you must act in both a procedurally and substantively fair manner and this presumes that you have taken time to consult with your team and consider alternatives, and ultimately, should retrenchments occur, that a fair severance package is offered.

Usually, the minimum retrenchment pay is one week for every year worked. It’s key to have an open and transparent conversation with your team but be sure to understand your role and the rule of law as it applies to your particular situation. 

If you are a good person (and I’m sure you are if you’re reading my book), you are probably kept awake at night worrying about salaries and your team. I understand. You’ve hired great people and you want to treat them well and give them the best you can. But that’s not always possible and sometimes you have to make shitty calls with shitty outcomes that lead to people being fired or retrenched. 

Here’s how I look at this: it’s them or it’s me and my business. I know that’s rough. I know that’s intense and I know that’s selfish but my business needs to survive and to do so, someone needs to go. It’s either them or it’s me. I choose me. 

The reason I choose me is not selfishness. The reason I choose me is because I can rebuild my business. I can grow it back to a place where it can employ people and pay taxes and help contribute to society. Don’t suffer from the sunk cost fallacy here. Don’t think that because you hired people you have to keep them on forever. You don’t. That’s life, that’s work, that’s business. People come and people go. 

It’s your job to make sure you give them every legal and ethical opportunity to prove themselves, to help you save the business and to do good work. But sometimes that’s not enough. So again, when the time comes, cut quick and cut deep. One more month isn’t going to magically turn your sales around and save your business. 

### Shareholder agreements

If your business has multiple shareholders, your shareholder agreement becomes integral to the winding up of the business. 

You should have a signed and initialled copy of your shareholder agreement on hand to use as a guide for how you proceed and engage with each shareholder. When you begin proceedings to liquidate or go into business rescue each shareholder will need to understand their role and what they stand to gain or lose. 

### Secured and unsecured loans

At some point, you may have needed to take out a loan to grow your business or stay alive in tough times. There are generally two kinds of loans: secured and unsecured. Secured loans are more accessible and tied to an asset. Perhaps you offered up your business vehicle or office as security that you will pay back the loan. An unsecured loan is not attached to an asset and here the lender has taken on more risk. 

It is important to understand what kind of loan you have taken out as this will impact your business rescue or liquidation proceedings. It’s also extremely important to understand if you have personal surety and a secured loan attached to, let’s say, your house. If this is the case, the bank could take your house as payment of your debt. 

### Personal surety 

This is one of the most important concepts to understand as the owner of a business and someone who may take on loans or debt. Signing personal surety means that if your business is unable to pay off a loan or repay any form of debt, you as an individual are liable for that debt. 

It is imperative to review every contract, every loan agreement or fundraising document you have ever signed to make sure you did not sign a personal surety clause. If you have personal surety in your contracts, unfortunately, you will likely be responsible for paying back whatever debt remains in the company. 

I have never signed personal surety, thankfully. I have had decent advisors and board members who helped me avoid this particular mess. Unfortunately, I don’t have a good story to tell you about any founders I know who have signed personal surety and managed to get out of it. The banks, malls and lawyers will tell you that it’s a pretty standard term to include in your lease or loan or contract but that doesn’t make it any less brutal. Once those hooks are in you it’s very difficult to remove them, so my advice is to never ever sign personal surety, even if it means losing the deal you’re fighting for. We think everything is going to go our way when we’re just starting out and about to sign on that dotted line but in my career, I’ve learned one thing about contracts that has held true in every situation: plan for divorce, not marriage. Plan for failure, not success. 

### Cut expenses & sell everything

I am always surprised by the way that business owners can distort reality when times get tough. They want to hold on to certain expenses and material items. If you think your business is in financial distress, the first thing you should do is print out your bank statements and highlight every non-core expense. Then cut them out immediately. 

Before you take the step to enter liquidation or business rescue you should make an inventory of everything in your business: stock, office equipment, computers, everything. It’s very possible that you could cover your debt by selling whatever you can as you wind up. Don’t take anything for granted – every cent counts when you are considering the future of your company.

# REFRAMING FAILURE

Failure is such an integral part of building a business but it’s also the scariest part. Humans don’t like to fail. We always strive for success and are taught from a young age that if we fail we are failures. That’s just not true. If you fail, you are not a failure. You have successfully figured out how *not* to do something. 

I’ve never met a successful entrepreneur who is afraid of failure. Avoiding failure is not a viable or smart business strategy. Your small business needs to figure out how to embrace failure, mitigate the impact of failure and integrate the lessons learned through failure into your core DNA. You cannot avoid failure on the path to success. That’s like asking your child to walk without learning how to crawl. The one thing leads to the other. 

It’s time to get comfortable with failure. We’re going to work through ways to embrace these issues and use them to your advantage. 

Remember, failure is not fatal and if it is, you’re doing it wrong. 

## WHY BUSINESSES FAIL

There are lots and lots of reasons that businesses fail. Below are a few of the more common issues that small businesses face and some suggestions on how to deal with each one. 

### No sales

This may sound stupidly obvious, but if you are struggling to pay bills every month, you have a sales problem. Many entrepreneurs think that launching a new feature is a solution to slow sales. It’s not. New features, new products or anything new is like sending Eskom more coal and asking them to keep the lights on. Adding more things to sell on top of a bad sales funnel won't bring in more revenue. 

Bite the bullet and do the hard thing. Get out there and sell. Talk to new customers, find new sales channels and start the outreach needed to improve your sales pipeline. There is no silver bullet here. To make more sales you need to get out there and sell more. 

### Cash flow management

I want to let you in on a very loosely held secret of mine (and of most great businesses, regardless of their scale): I’m obsessed with cash flow management. This is, without a doubt, one of the biggest reasons that small businesses fail. You think you have money in your bank account when you actually have invoices that haven’t been paid and bills that are due. 

If you don’t believe that “numbers are your thing”, you need to make numbers your thing. Focus on learning about your business’s cash flow, when you receive payment, when you need to pay suppliers and how much money you have on hand to juggle with. 

In short, cash flow management has to do with how much money you owe right now vs how much money you have coming into the business right now. If you owe someone R10 000 next week, you don’t have that cash in the bank right now and next week you’re only receiving R2 000 into your bank account, you are technically insolvent, because you can’t pay your bills. This is not a great place to be as a business and can lead to suppliers putting you into bankruptcy. 

When you are starting out it’s easy to default to the hand-to-mouth cash management system, which means that you’re spending everything you earn. This is trouble waiting to happen. It’s imperative to work out how much money you can set aside early on to manage your cash flow and to act as if you don’t actually own that money going forward. Set aside ±10% of your earnings to begin with and over a period of a few months gauge if that is too much or too little. It’s really important not to screw this up. If you don’t build up a cash reserve, you’ll be one mistake away from going under. 

### The wrong customer

If you have a great product, a great sales team and fantastic marketing but are still struggling to make the kind of sales you need, you are probably talking to the wrong customer. (I made this mistake for years: assuming who my customer *should* be instead of doing the hard work to figure out exactly who my customer was.) This leads to wasted time selling to the wrong company or people and wasted budget and effort trying to convert a “maybe” to a “yes”. 

Spend more time working out who your ideal customer is, where they are and what they need from you. Then go to them and sell them your solution. If your customer thinks your price is too high, they are the wrong customer or your price *is* too high. Figure out which problem you are dealing with and solve accordingly. 

***Head over to Section 5 for more on finding the right customer.*** 

### Big experiments

Too often I coach startups that have spent months, if not years, building the “perfect” product, ploughing every last cent into the launch of the product and then being destroyed because they missed the mark. 

One big experiment more often than not leads to one big failure. 

Reframe experiments as small opportunities to learn about your business and your customer. Take smaller risks more often, record the results and then iterate with your next experiment. 

Building a culture of experimentation into your business helps your team understand that they can work on big problems but solve in small ways that provide frequent feedback loops and mitigate the risk of one expensive experiment. 

A fantastic way to avoid destruction of your business by one big experiment is to embrace the lean startup methodology of building minimum viable products (MVPs). Eric Ries, the author of *The Lean Startup*, defined an MVP as that version of a new product that allows a team to collect the maximum amount of validated learning about customers with the least effort. 

Maximum validated learning, least effort. 

Not maximum effort, least learning. 

Make sure you aren’t spending all of your time and money on a single experiment that could teach you nothing and take you nowhere. Small, iterative product evolutions that include customer feedback will leave you in a much better position.

### Too much, too fast, too often

As the founder of a small business, you are a talented and high-energy individual. You also have a bit of an ego because you are actually good at a lot of things. This ego and your abilities lead you to believe that you don’t need help and you can do everything better than anyone else. 

You can’t. I sure as shit can’t either. I need help all the time and I do my best to find the best people and bring them into my creative process when I need them. Asking for help is a sign of strength, not a sign of weakness, get that into your founder brain right now and your life will be infinitely simpler and better. 

You are doing too much, too often. Pick your priorities and focus on what you are the best at. Everything else can be the next priority or handed off to someone else to focus on. Delegation is one of the biggest issues that founders face in a small business. Your team wants to be involved more and if you prevent that from happening, you build a resentful culture. Let people in and let them help you grow. 

### Post-traumatic growth

Reframing failure is hard. Entrepreneurs tend to frame failure as an end-point when in fact, failure is a through-point that helps you get to your next destination. 

To help you reframe failure let me introduce you to Post-Traumatic Growth (PTG). Post-traumatic growth (or benefit-finding) is positive psychological change experienced as a result of adversity and other challenges in order to rise to a higher level of functioning. If you reframe failure as an opportunity for you and your business to grow, you will start to thrive in situations that present a failure. Understand that failure is trauma but your psychology and that of your team and business will evolve for the better after a failure. 

If you avoid failure you will never improve. If you never experience failure you will never grow. 

*“Failure is not fatal and if it is, you’re doing it wrong.”*

# 03\. CULTURE, CONFLICT & CRISIS

What should you be optimising for in your business right now? Sure, there are myriad answers to this question, but I think three keywords come to mind in a post-pandemic world: uncertainty, adaptability and learning.

Our businesses need to be adaptable, they need to acknowledge uncertainty as a variable that isn’t going away, and we need to enable a constant learning culture for ourselves and our teams. I’d argue that you should be optimising your own life and your mind for these things too.

The world is not more or less uncertain now than it was 400 years ago. Uncertainty is certain. Change is inevitable. Entropy is constant and insistent. The more you are able to learn, the more resilient you will become and the more adaptable you can be.   
   
How do you become adaptable?

Be sure of what you know and go forward with that knowledge, but if the facts change, change your opinion, adapt, move and evolve. If you are an events company specialising in real-life events for thousands of people and one fine day a global pandemic shuts down the world, you can either sit tight and hope for things to go back to “normal” or you have to consider your options and rethink your basic business model. The facts have changed and your business needs to change in accordance with those facts.   
   
You are not a tree, you do not have roots, you can move and move you should. Consider that being adaptable, holding strong opinions weakly, is an acceptable strategy for your business. How do you then build a culture that supports this way of thinking? How do you create a learning organisation that is constantly becoming something better as it learns new things or more efficient methods of doing certain things?  
   
What are you optimising for in your business? Is it adaptability or is it an outdated concept that no longer applies and is holding you back?

This section will help you understand the fundamentals of creating a curious culture of learning and provide you with tools to manage crisis when crisis eventually presents itself. 

# BUILDING A LEARNING ORGANISATION

Organisations thrive when they are learning, curious and evolving. Small business is much the same and stagnation is often an early-warning sign of impending death. But it can be difficult to convince yourself and your team to push through the daily grind of survival and to then expect them to learn something new, challenge themselves and remain curious about their work. 

Change is difficult – it’s meant to be – but it is an imperative variable to embrace if you want to stay on top of your game. 

It is a mistake to think that promoting a culture of learning is not useful to your bottom line. Learning organisations by their very nature are set up to be agile in thinking and in execution. A learning organisation is one that is skilled at creating and transferring new knowledge while being able to execute these changes effectively and modifying behaviour to move towards the best results. 

Learning organisations don’t just appear, it takes dedication and focus to ensure that everyone in the business is on board to grow, learn and develop their own thinking and that of the organisation. 

### Small experiments more often

Many organisations (and people) are afraid of failure. This fear of failure often occurs because the stakes are too high. We give ourselves one shot to make it or everything blows up. The key to embracing learning is to lower the stakes. One way to do this is to make your experiments as a company more frequent and smaller. Instead of putting an entire year’s marketing budget into a campaign, rather spend a few weeks of budget on a smaller version of the campaign and then test and refine using the results. Help your team become comfortable with the concept of small, manageable experiments and they’ll start to experiment on their own and bring you results to review. 

### Promote curiosity

Curiosity is not some ethereal concept with intangible results that businesses should fear. We shouldn’t shy away from our curiosity or that of our colleagues. Promoting curiosity in an organisation leads to fewer decision-making errors, more innovative thinking, reduced group conflict, more open communication and better team performance. It can help people and organisations avoid confirmation bias (see Section 9). In a recent study of 3 000 employees across multiple businesses and industries, 70% said they felt clear barriers at work to being curious. 

Allowing your team to engage in their curiosity (obviously relevant to their work) can unleash agility, ideas and products you haven’t even considered. If you have the best people on your team, you should trust them to explore and make relevant use of their time for the betterment of your business. 

### Make it informal

Learning is not owned by HR. Learning is something that each person in the company should take ownership of. The more you democratise learning inside of your business, the more likely that the best ideas and freshest thinking emerge. Learning is everyone’s responsibility and should be part of everyone’s job description. 

### Bring in experts

Your business can’t be expected to know everything about everything all the time. You need to bring in experts to address your team, to educate them and engage them in new and different ways of thinking. 

If you have clients who are specialists in specific areas of business then partner with them and perhaps even offer up your expertise to them in a trade exchange. Hire keynote speakers from specific areas that you believe your company is lacking and help to upskill your team in a session once a month. You can also consider highlighting your internal expertise by allowing one of your own specialists to present their work, passion or curiosity to the team in a monthly session. This shows your team the true quality of their colleagues and allows the specialist to feel recognised while educating and upskilling her colleagues. 

### Allow mistakes

Working in fear for your job is a terrible way to be the best at what you do. If you want to build a learning organisation and truly want to engage the best minds that you work with then you need to remove the fear of mistakes. 

Mistakes happen all the time. They are a normal part of building and sustaining a business but many businesses punish any mistakes swiftly and brutally. If your brightest minds are too scared to try anything new, think in ways that may differ from the organisational consensus and are too nervous to speak up for fear of reproach, your organisation will never learn anything new. People will be too afraid to challenge the status quo because if they make a mistake they’ll be fired like John was last week. Remove the fear of mistakes, make experiments smaller and the risk lower and people will likely begin to thrive. 

### Hire for learning

I’m not interested in experts from yesteryear. I’m not interested in people who are not adept at learning and evolving. I’m interested in working with smart people with an obsession for learning and teaching others. Finding these people isn’t as difficult as you might think. It all starts at the hiring process. Are you hiring people who are single-minded and effective at doing one thing very well but aren’t interested in new ways to do that thing, new discoveries around that thing or alternative views about the thing that they do every day? Or are you hiring someone who wants to do the best work regardless of how or who has helped them along the way?

One very simple and very practical way to hire for curiosity is to ask a series of simple questions in the interview process. 

* What are you learning right now that has nothing to do with your work?  
* What is the most recent thing that you taught someone?  
* What hobbies do you have outside of work?

The questions above and variations of these questions can help you discover hidden talent. 

The difficulty with hiring for curiosity is that you must have already created a culture of learning for these people to flourish. If they are hired and are then hamstrung and unable to learn and evolve they will become disgruntled and leave.  

# CULTURE AND STAFF RETENTION

It is significantly more costly to replace a brilliant team member than to retain them. Some studies indicate that it could cost up to R350 000 to recruit, train and integrate a new senior manager.

One of the most complicated things about being an entrepreneur is managing your relationships. It’s not only your suppliers, your customers and your investors who can put you under pressure but your team can do so too. 

Some of my most difficult times as an entrepreneur have come about because of my team. 

They want too much from me or not enough. Either I've hired the wrong person or the right person doesn’t fit with our company culture. Either my expectations are too high or their performance is too low. It’s a never-ending tightrope. Sometimes you hire someone who turns out to be an absolute boon to your bottom line but they decide they don’t fit with *your* business or culture and they leave. 

People make everything more complicated and that’s both a good and a bad thing. 

One of the startups that I joined was run by a seagull CEO: someone who swoops in, shits on everything and then flies away. I was the COO and was left to pick up the shit, wipe it off the shoulders of a team that was working 18-hour days to fulfil the CEO’s vision in spite of the CEO being absent, misguided and unable to stick to a decision for longer than a week or two. 

One of the corporate phrases that I hate the most is “headcount”. I always imagine some douchebag manager waltzing through his open-plan office with his arm outstretched, pointing-finger elongated and squinting with one eye as he counts the heads of the human beings sitting in the office. He proudly goes back to his desk and reports that he has 35 heads under his management. 

I hate it. I hate everything about thinking of people as heads in a machine that uses them until they aren’t worth anything more and then discards them and replaces them with a new head. Gross. 

It’s easy to think that all people are looking for is a job that pays well. But retaining staff has very little to do with money and everything to do with job satisfaction, problem-solving and team cohesion. If people are paid enough, do you think they’ll stay in a job that abuses them, burns them out or allows them to be assaulted and ridiculed? Doubtful. Money isn't everything, it’s one of the things but it isn’t *the* thing.

### Be flexible

It’s difficult to create a one-size-fits-all business that is also flexible. These two concepts don’t work well together because flexibility is different for everyone. If someone on your team has pets that can only be walked in the afternoons, try not to have a meeting with that person in the afternoons, for example. This means a lot more one-on-one attention for each of your team members. But it’s imperative to let them know that you want to work with them to find a solution that is flexible but beneficial for them and the company. 

### Fire the assholes

A successful team is the sum of its parts, not the result of a single rockstar salesperson who brings in a chunk of your revenue but causes your best people to resign. As the leader of your business you need to recognise when one bad apple is rotting the basket. Removing the bad apple is more sane than losing two or three people. This applies only if you believe that your hiring processes are working well and you have a team around you that you are proud of. If the one bad apple is being a jerk because the people around them are all C-players, you should probably rethink your hiring processes, keep the person who's bringing in the revenue and restructure your team. Tough decisions are often the right ones. 

### Try uncommon sense

The common sense that prevailed in 2019 is very different to common sense of 2022\. It’s common sense right now that working from home is difficult and draining. It’s common sense that outcomes trump hours worked. Common sense has changed and it’s important that everyone on your team understands what common sense means in your business. 

Ask your team what issues they’re facing that were once common sense but have now become completely uncommon. Engage with them frequently and figure out what the new and uncommon sense may be. If it fits your culture and it will make them happier, then adopt some uncommon sense and forge a new path for your team. 

A few years ago I was running a business that had staff travelling to our offices in the Cape Town CBD every day. This was before the pandemic made work from home a truly acceptable alternative to in-office days. Traffic wasn’t always a problem in Cape Town but in 2018 it had reached peak insanity. I am an early bird and a few days in a row one of the team was getting into the office before me pretty consistently, so I asked her how that happened. She told me that to beat traffic, go to gym and still see her child she needed to get up at 4am and be in the gym before 6.30am. This really isn’t the kind of environment I wanted to cultivate so I implemented flexitime for her and anyone else facing a brutal schedule due to crazy traffic increases. 

The change in attitude and happiness level was immediate and those with a longer commute were finally able to keep normal hours – because I'd asked a simple question. 

### Be transparent

At one of my startups, many years ago, we were going through a difficult time financially. I hadn’t paid myself a salary in a while and everyone could sense that something was going on. I put on a smile and hid the truth from my team. I struggled with the balance to be transparent or protect my team from the stress of not having money to pay their salaries in a few months. 

When the truth eventually came out they unanimously agreed that more information would be better. As often as you can manage, err on the side of sharing, not hiding. Tell people if you may be unable to pay them in a month because it gives them the information they deserve to make decisions to protect themselves and their financial futures. 

The pleasant upside of being transparent is that most people want to help, they want to keep their jobs and they want to make the company better. By sharing tough times you may provoke some creative solutions that lead to more revenue. Conversely it’s also motivating to share the successes and celebrate them when they happen.

### Encourage productivity 

People thrive when they feel valued and they feel valued when they are being productive. If you have found this not to be true with your team then it’s likely you employ B and C players. You need to find the A players because those team members don’t want to sit idly by and rake in a salary for doing nothing. Productivity is also very closely aligned to engagement. The more engaged you are with your team members, the more likely they will be productive and engaged in the success of your business. This requires more effort from you as their manager or business owner but the benefits outweigh the extra effort it will require from you. 

### Value human beings

The number of business owners that I have spoken to who don’t care about their team’s personal issues is shocking to me. If you want to retain your best talent then you have to treat them like people who have lives outside of work because guess what? They do have lives outside of work\!

I’m not asking you to invite your entire team around for dinner every week but it’s good to know their child is sick, they don’t sleep very much, suffer from anxiety or have car problems. These little gestures of care matter to most people and increase the ties between you, your company and their attachment to your collective success. If their job is just about a paycheck then they’re going to find a bigger salary eventually. Then what?

# ONBOARDING STAFF 

Hiring people and building the right culture for your business is difficult in the best conditions. In a remote world, hiring and onboarding new people can be overwhelming, complicated and detrimental to your business. 

It doesn’t really matter what business you are in or how you make money, if your hiring and onboarding processes are terrible, the people you hire will make things incredibly complicated and eventually ruin your business. 

Many small businesses take the onboarding process for granted and just assume that because their business is small, onboarding is simple and organic. It’s not. Usually, in a small business environment, the processes are all buried inside the brain of a couple of key people. The onboarding process needs to benefit existing team members as well as the new people. 

Let’s start by understanding what “onboarding” a new team member means. Onboarding is a series of steps and activities that new team members should experience to give them the best opportunity to integrate into your company. Without proper onboarding processes, the new team member will feel isolated, lost and insecure about their work and unsure about where they fit into the culture of your organisation. 

A good onboarding process is even more important when the team is distributed around the world or isolated from a traditional physical office environment. 

### Onboarding goals

Do you have clearly laid-out goals for onboarding a new team member? What do you want them to know by the end of their first week with you?

Figure out what this looks like and then create a document that you can share with the new person as they arrive on their first day (or even the day before, so they feel semi-prepared). 

These goals can include basic HR documentation, contact information, access to folders and documents, communication tools, setting up their email and file-sharing accounts and introducing them to their team members one by one. 

### The team

It is important that new hires understand the team they are joining. Who does what? Who can help with which parts of the company? Who do they talk to if they are missing something important to their work? Who is their immediate boss? Who are their colleagues working with them on their first project and what does everyone actually do at the company?

Don’t rush through this step and assume that the new person will just figure it out. It’s hard enough starting a new job. Expecting someone to introduce themselves and learn everyone’s roles and responsibilities without help piles on the stress. It’s also a good idea to share the organisational structure with a new hire in the clearest possible way, so that they understand what the team looks like and who is responsible for what.

### How we work

When I work with a team I share with them my “How I Work” document. This is a basic document explaining how I work with my team and how my team can and should work with me. This document covers communication, file sharing, urgent matters and various other basic information that means people don't have to guess about things. 

Your company should have a similar document. What you don't want is a new hire to spot a problem that is critical but to have no idea how, when and where to raise the issue, so it is left unsaid because they are the new person. If you haven't created this kind of document, your first step is to create one so that everyone knows how they are expected to engage at work. 

### Communication is key

There is always lots happening at a small business and everyone is busy. This leads to a sink-or-swim mentality when onboarding new people. It’s OK to give your new team members a sense of responsibility but under-communication can rapidly lead to a sense of isolation. 

Aim to over-communicate with new hires for the first few weeks. Check in a few times a day to make sure they aren’t stuck with anything that they are too shy to mention. Make sure that they are hooked into the communication channels like Slack, Discord, WhatsApp or wherever you communicate and ask your team members to also check in once or twice in the first week. 

I also suggest having a virtual coffee or afternoon drinks hangout. This allows the team to talk about topics that are not work-related and get to know the new hire as a person, not a resource. 

### Be patient

Of course, it is important that your new team member integrates and starts working as soon as possible. However, it’s more important that they integrate properly and do the best work they can, so be patient. Give them enough time to learn the virtual ropes and engage with the team so they can figure out where they fit in. The more pressure you pile on, the more panic they will feel and the more they will pull away from your team and isolate themselves. 

Isolation is the kiss of death for a new person trying to integrate successfully into a remote team with pre-existing relationships and work routines.

# MANAGING CONFLICT

Conflict is a standard part of life. But since the COVID-19 pandemic began, it feels as if conflict is a larger part of the daily grind than usual. At home under lockdown, familial conflict feels inevitable. At work, pressure is mounting as the economy contracts and disagreements erupt. 

Managing this conflict is part of life and of business. But not all conflict is created equal. There is conflict that leads to better decisions, stronger relationships and a more cohesive team; and there is conflict that is destructive, unnecessary and harmful to your team and the business you are trying to build. It’s up to you and the people you work with to identify the conflict you are facing and work through it with as little emotion as possible.

I never shy away from conflict but over the years I have learned that there are times to put up a fight and times to walk away. Good leaders recognise this and their businesses thrive on robust conversation and debate that is structured and inclusive. This results in resilient and capable teams who trust themselves and know management has their back and businesses that are adaptive and innovative.

I’ve worked with people – and organisations – that are highly conflict-averse. It’s as if a single debate or argument that went the wrong way would break them. This fragility is unhealthy. On the other hand, there are those that seem to thrive on conflict, where the outcome is binary – victory or loss.

Extremes of any kind are bad and this holds true with conflict. Too much and great leaps forward never happen. Too little and there is no clear passion for the work being done or the business being built. 

Managing conflict is tricky at the best of times and in a global pandemic that never seems to end, it’s only going to get worse, so you better make conflict management a top priority in your leadership skills plan. For now, here are some tips to handle conflict.

### Don't shy away from negative feedback

Humans have a tendency to avoid dishing out negative feedback. While easier, it is not what most employees seek: improvement and validation. Don’t avoid the negative stuff. If your team thinks they are great just because you are afraid to be upfront, you are in for a storm when you one day do provide a frank assessment of their performance. Feedback, even if it is negative, will be well received if it is honest, constructive and fair. 

### Embrace conflict

In twenty years of building businesses and working alongside some really smart people, I have never encountered a conflict-free business. You cannot avoid it, so don’t; embrace it. It’s a smarter strategy. 

Tension is natural and necessary, especially if you are hiring smart – and diverse – people who think independently. Strong ideas and firm opinions are to be celebrated. Don’t stifle the debate but steer it in a constructive direction – you don't want it becoming personal. 

### Know that it’s good to be wrong

Conflict isn’t always about a winner and a loser. Sometimes it's the debate and the thinking that flows from this that is more valuable than the outcome. Your team should be able to debate pertinent issues and in the process grow together. 

If the conflict you are dealing with is a personal dispute between individuals, it’s important to understand the facts without emotion as much as possible and then work towards an outcome. In these scenarios, there may be the need for someone to be right and someone to be wrong but here again, the result should, as far as possible, not isolate or ostracise an individual. Of course, if anything illegal or against company rules has taken place, the situation must be dealt with accordingly. 

### Apologise

One of the things that I have had to practise throughout my career is to apologise. I confess that I have struggled with this. However, the art of genuine apology is a powerful tool for a team leader. 

It took a few years of maturing, a few years with my psychologist, plus a partner with extraordinary patience to help me understand that apologising is a show of strength, not weakness. Most of us don’t want to admit we’re wrong (apologise) because we think people will see this as a sign of weakness or that we weren’t at the top of our game because we messed up. This is not correct. It’s the complete opposite. Every time I have managed to effectively apologise to anyone they have left the conversation feeling empowered, feeling like I understood their perspective and that they helped me grow. This is leadership in its truest form. Recognising that you can make a mistake and that the people that you hired to help you are actually able to do so. If you’re the smartest person in the room and you never apologise, you’re hiring the wrong people and doing the wrong job. 

Master it and you will unlock a new level of trust with your team. Of course this means taking your (and my) ego out of the equation. Sometimes it’s worth apologising even if you weren’t entirely to blame. It’s important that your colleagues understand that as a leader you have the self-awareness to know when to take a bullet for the team and the strength to apologise without being asked to. 

### Embrace strong opinions, loosely held

I’ve said this already – it’s OK to have a strong opinion about something, but if the facts change, it’s also OK to change your opinion. This is at the heart of many conflicts: one party believes something while another has a different set of beliefs but neither party is willing to look at the facts and change their opinion. 

This is the time to step in and guide the conversation towards a resolution. Facts, not ego, are key to arriving at an outcome that everyone can get behind. 

### Don’t text

In a world where remote work is becoming more prominent and communication is becoming brief and precise, it’s important to remember that it’s often easier to resolve conflict with a video call or an in-person meeting than over email or via text message. 

I’m not the only person in the world who has misread tone over text and felt more aggression over email than the sender intended. If you work in a remote team, the quickest way to resolve this kind of misunderstanding is with a video call. Don’t underestimate the value of engaging with someone on a human-to-human level and laying all the cards on the table.

# SIDE-HUSTLE CULTURE

When I was a younger startup founder I would do everything in my power to prevent my team from becoming distracted. No side projects, no extra work, no freelancing, no private work, no clients outside of the business. Focus every ounce of your efforts into my business, my idea, my startup. 

That works for some businesses and it worked for a period. But I don’t think it works anymore. 

I now believe that people can’t be held back from earning money, creating their own wealth and flexing their minds and skills to build something of their own. 

Most business owners misunderstand side hustles. Most founders, bosses and leaders think that their staff will start a side hustle and in a short period will resign from their job and take all the business’s clients with them. 

That is just not true. It’s hard to build a side hustle and it takes time to turn a profit. I firmly believe that the war for top talent is going to be won by the businesses that embrace intellectual freedom, side hustles and performance-based incentives. Not dictatorships that demand loyalty. 

Here are a few reasons why side hustles are good for your business, good for you, and good for your team. 

### The best people

Different people perform at different levels in different environments. Painting everyone in your company with the same brush is naive and insulting to those who show up, smash targets and perform all day every day. 

It’s completely OK for you to give some of your team the leeway required to build a side hustle because they perform exceptionally well at their full-time job. Rewarding these team members with the freedom to build in their personal time is a gift they will thank you for.

### New perspectives

I believe that allowing people to follow their curiosity, research their interests and build side hustles helps them explore things that you potentially don’t want to pay for as a business owner. If you are a software development company and you prevent your developers from building their own side projects, you are limiting their experiences to the work in front of them. 

Every developer I know has a side project and is constantly building new things in new languages that do new and different things. They’re doing it with or without your knowledge. So why not embrace their desire to constantly level up their skills and build things into your business for the benefit of your team and income streams? Why force them to hide their voracious need to learn more and build more? Rather shift your perspective and help them build things that could improve your business while giving them upside with equity or profit share in the new product or project they launch. 

### People don’t want to quit their jobs

This is possibly the biggest mistake that business owners make – thinking that your team wants to walk away from a stable and well-paying job for a side hustle. 

Here’s the thing that you should understand better than most: It’s very, very difficult to build a project and turn a profit. It takes time, focus and a lot of effort. What your team more than likely wants is the freedom and trust to explore while doing their job and hitting their targets. 

If your staff is performing then give them the freedom to experiment because most people just want to play around, experiment and have something small of their own. A side hustle mostly adds income to an already existing income stream (salary). It’s not a replacement. 

### Your team is hustling anyway…

You should not be able to restrict what your employees do in their time away from work. Business leaders and managers seem to believe that a salary buys every minute of an employee’s time. It does not. 

If you pay someone for 40 hours a week, that’s what you get from them. You should not be telling them what they can work on during their time away from the company. 

Now, of course, there are obvious limits to this freedom; people should not build competitors to your core business without telling someone. They shouldn’t use your unique IP to make money without you knowing it. They shouldn’t poach your clients and they shouldn’t use your database for their personal benefit. Of course they shouldn't; don’t be silly\! But why would you not allow your team to work on a completely different idea that has nothing to do with your business? Why are you preventing them from diversifying their income streams? 

Here’s a little secret: you are not preventing them from anything. They’re hustling on the side whether you know it or not, so why not embrace it and become part of the solution, not the problem for them. Buy their loyalty with the freedom to earn extra income. 

### Two side-hustle systems

Here’s are two really simple ways to embrace side hustles in your business: 

**1\. The Hackathon**  
Hackathons are really mini side-hustle days. You give your team a set amount of time to solve a problem they may have. You can define the rules and terms and then they go away and build something. If their projects prove to be valuable, you allow them time to build it out and integrate it into the broader business. Quick, simple and affordable. 

**2\. The Level-Up Method**  
Think of side hustles as internal startups. Allow anyone in your company to request a small budget (±R5 000) to test an idea out. Together you define the project and expected outcomes and then you allow that person to go out and, on their own, build the side hustle and test their thinking. 

If the results are positive, allow them a slightly bigger budget to expand their side project. And on and on until they realise they don’t want to continue, or they grow their side hustle into a serious business unit that they can take ownership of and benefit from.

# 04\. LEADERSHIP & PEOPLE

Whoever started the rumour that being a leader is fun, fulfilling and something that everyone should aspire to was one of the great liars of our time. 

Being a leader can be lonely and complex. You can feel isolated, confused yet successful and productive all at the same time. You can have one department think you are the best thing since the automatic out of office email response was invented while another group of employees absolutely despise your instructions and are constantly finding ways to undermine you. 

Whether you are a manager, a founder or a CEO, or run a team of two or two thousand, leadership is constantly changing and is always going to look and feel different to everyone. That’s because we’re all different and we all have different skills to lean on and use in our day-to-day running of our business. 

This section is filled with insights and anecdotes to help you find your leadership style and enhance your best traits. Whether you are struggling with communication, battling with shyness, are not very self-aware or have the worst sense of humour in the world there is a way to overcome whichever obstacle is holding you back. 

One of the key traits of good leaders in this decade is the ability to read and understand emotions. Your own emotions and other people’s really do matter in business. We’re not robots, we feel things and we feel them often and intensely and ignoring those things is ridiculous and outdated. Of course, not every feeling needs to be felt in public (I really wish more people understood this and held back from tweeting every thought that pops into their brains) but on the whole humans are not able to push their feelings down forever and still sustain a productive track record.

# LEADERSHIP TOOLS

It takes hard work and a set of tools to become a better leader. Here are some thoughts, skills and tools to help you get started on the path to being the kind of leader that your team needs. 

### Collaborate

Often one of the more difficult things to do as a leader is to involve people in your role as a leader. You shouldn’t be outsourcing your job to your team, of course, but you should absolutely be including them in key decisions that could impact their jobs and livelihoods. 

If you have hired the best people and they have been given the freedom to do their best work then collaborating with them makes the most business sense. You want (and need) the best minds solving the hardest problems.  

### Use radical candour

Open and transparent communication is not a new concept but it is one that many people in leadership roles struggle with. There are definitely some things that many of your team won't want to know but on the whole people operate better when the goals and problems are clearly defined. “We have six months until we can’t pay salaries,” is a great problem to give your team; ask them to help you work towards extending that runway. Being honest about problems when it’s too late to solve them is very demoralising. 

I like to practise radical candour with my teams as a default method of communication. This is a management philosophy based on caring personally while challenging directly. 

Kim Scott, the co-founder of Candor, Inc., recalls a specific moment after giving a successful presentation to Google executives when her former boss, Sheryl Sandberg, asked her to take a walk. Sandberg told Scott about what went well during the presentation and then started to discuss areas that she thought Scott could improve upon. Finally, Sandberg said, "You said 'um' a lot in there, were you aware of it?"

Scott made a brush-off gesture and responded, "Yeah, no, it's a verbal tic, no big deal really." At that moment, Scott describes how Sandberg stopped, looked right at her, and moved on to the "directly challenging" bit.

"When you say 'um' every third word, it makes you sound stupid,” said Sandberg. Scott said Sandberg's blunt feedback was the "kindest thing she could have possibly done" for her career. Because of this feedback, Scott trained with a speech coach and essentially booted the "ums" out of her vocabulary. 

That's what makes radical candour so radical: the elements of personal investment and directly challenging the receiver. 

### Practise self-care

Taking care of yourself is not selfish. Self-care is not selfish. This is such an important point that I want to say it again: It’s OK to look after yourself, in fact, it’s mandatory for good leadership that as a leader you are taking care of yourself. Later we’ll dive more deeply into mental health and fitness (Section xx) because it’s such an important topic but right now, understand that burnout trickles down from the top. If you aren’t taking holidays, your team won't feel comfortable to do so either. If you are calling them at midnight on a Friday, they’ll do the same to their team and their suppliers and clients. If you are not putting yourself at the top of your priority list, you’re in trouble.

I call this the Sacrifice Fallacy – the idea that you need to sacrifice your mental and physical health to build a business, manage a team or do a job. It’s a toxic belief that we as managers and leaders need to give up. You have to put yourself and your health at the top of your priority list before you can lead anyone. 

### Be consistent

Without fail, every bad leader I have ever worked with has lacked consistency. Every day you engage with a different version of that person. If they had a bad morning at home they take it out on the team at work. If they had a good meeting after lunch they return full of the joys, despite having lambasted you for something irrelevant in the morning. 

They say that children react well to routine; well, your team reacts well to consistency. Consistency of strategy, consistency of methodology, consistency of measurement and feedback. Consistency is key to surviving and thriving as a leader. 

### Lose the ego

Be in the trenches with your team. You can’t ask people to do things that you don’t want to do in a tough spot. This doesn’t mean that you should be doing all the work, but if you are retrenching and restructuring your business, everyone can and should do everything possible to take the business forward. 

This means that as a leader you need to drop your ego as much as possible. You can’t take offence if someone is upset, you can’t be upset if people provide feedback when you ask for it and you can’t treat people as if there is no global pandemic affecting their lives. It’s *not* about you more often than it *is* about you. Remember that and it’ll go a long way with the people around you. 

### Use humour

This may feel like a strange addition to the list of tools that good leaders should have but I believe it’s an important one. If you are able to laugh with your team, engage in a joke or two, be self-deprecating and self-aware, you’re going to win them over and create a relationship that isn’t just about them delivering work to you. 

Humour also helps people understand the way you think and see the world a bit better and will give your team much-needed respite from the doom and gloom of the world, the news and the always outraged social media that we’re all so addicted to. 

### Develop self-awareness

It’s important to understand the term “self-awareness” before taking steps to become more self-aware. Self-awareness is the ability to monitor and understand yourself, your abilities, your emotions and your reactions to things that happen in your business and life. If you are not self-aware, you might believe that you are the best singer in the world when in truth, you couldn’t carry a tune if someone offered you a million rand. A self-aware leader understands what they do best, what they suck at and have little ego when talking about these things. Self-aware people attract the best team members and retain them for longer. 

### Let the best do their best

Entrepreneurs like to do everything themselves. We like to take on as much as we think we can handle and then throw in 50% more work for ourselves. It’s the way we’ve always done it so it must be the right way, right? Nope. 

If you have taken the time to bring together a team of people who you believe are excellent at their work, the best thing you can do is let them be excellent at their work. Realising that you are not the best at everything is liberating, it gives you permission to focus on the work that you love the most and are the best at. 

Hire the best people and then for the love of all things good, let them do their best work. Remember, if they make mistakes you should practise radical candour and challenge their work, not their worth. 

### Invite feedback

It’s impossible to read the label from inside the bottle, so at some point you may want to invite your must trusted team members, friends and even family to provide you with constructive feedback. It’s important to note that this is not a bitching session where you are the target. The feedback must be specific and come with suggested ways to alter behaviour for the better. You want to understand that these are meant to be your most trusted allies and they are not out to get you, they are there to help you. 

Don’t become a different person overnight, that won't work. Start small and see if you can listen to and enact the suggested changes to build towards a more emotionally intelligent and self-aware work environment for yourself and for your team. 

### Highlight company values

Do your values line up with your business values? Does your company claim to hold honesty, integrity and transparency as core values yet you never give clear feedback without emotion? Are you always shouting at people without justification? Would you ever really be transparent about company performance or more importantly, transparent about your own performance? 

If your company values and your personal values don’t line up, you have a deeper and more systemic issue. Either you are lying about your company values or your own. Which is it? Which needs to change?

### Respect the value of curiosity

A surefire way to remain humble is to promote curiosity in your organisation. To do this you need to allow smart people to experiment without fear of punishment for failures they might incur. You need to promote deep work and wide research. You need to stop reacting and start proactively solving problems before they arise. 

Curiosity is a superpower in an organisation and it grows from the ground up if you let it. You have gone to great pains to build a team, now let them loose on your business. Let them ask the tough questions of you and your organisation and bring them in to help you formulate the answers. If you make your team part of the process of discovery, they will take ownership of the solution. Remember, curiosity creates change in an organisation. 

### Set boundaries

Being a leader means being *the* leader. Sometimes this means saying no even if your team disagrees with your decision. Sometimes it means you aren’t able to be their best buddy because down the line you’re going to have to be the leader. You must be serious about your work and your passions and invoke the same level of enthusiasm, passion and seriousness in your team. If you are always goofing off, expect them to do the same. 

Set boundaries, have opinions and enforce them with strength but if the facts change, a self-aware and adaptable leader is happy to change their opinion. 

# RECEIVING FEEDBACK

There was a time when the boss was untouchable. Criticism was never an option and ideas trickled from the top down, never the other way around. Those days are long gone. If you have done a good job hiring your team, your best thinkers are working in your business. 

It can be difficult to give constructive feedback. Leaders can let ego get in the way and always want to be right, sound right or find the right answer themselves. As a leader, it is part of your job to ensure that you provide an environment that promotes critical thinking and feedback from everyone in the organisation, including you. 

Leaders, as much as anyone in a business, need to receive constructive feedback that will help them evolve and grow into better bosses. 

Below I present a few ways to help your team provide you with feedback that is useful and constructive and can help you build a better business and team culture.

Remember to check your ego at the door and look for valuable feedback that can improve your company. This isn’t personal. If you feel it is, there are bigger issues at play that need to be resolved. 

### Prepare a question

Asking the right question is often the most important starting point to receiving useful feedback. Have a go-to question at the ready if you want to solicit relevant insights from your team. 

Some suggestions: 

* “Give me some advice.”  
* “How can I support you on this project?”  
* “What’s bothering you?”  
* “What’s the one thing you’ve been wanting to tell me but have been holding back?”

### General feedback suggestions

Promote the use of facts and data, don’t rely on feelings and emotions.   
Be direct and precise with your team and allow them to be direct and precise with you.  
Avoid accusatory language and broad, sweeping statements like “you always” or “we never”.  
Criticism and feedback work best when they are accompanied by a fix. Encourage your team to suggest ways to improve the issues they raise with you. 

### Remove your ego and emotions

It’s important to understand the difference between a moan and constructive feedback. There is a place for both but what we’re after is solid, constructive feedback that can help you improve and help your team members feel heard. 

Emotions can get in the way of constructive feedback; removing your ego is a signal to your team that it’s safe for them to speak openly in a respectful way. 

Resist the urge to defend yourself. Resist the urge to respond. Resist the urge to justify your decisions. Just listen and take notes. 

### Reward criticism and criticise yourself

It takes a lot for team members to openly criticise the work environment and their boss. It’s imperative, once they’ve taken the leap and made the effort, that you reward this effort by making a change of some kind. 

Thank them for doing something difficult and then take time to consider ways to improve before actually making a change. The act of making a visible change will reward their efforts. 

Another way to show your team that it’s OK to criticise you is to do it yourself. If you were involved in a project with your team, take the time to highlight places where you went wrong and ways in which you can improve. This will show your team that you are open to criticism and are looking to improve. 

### Being wrong is a superpower

I love being wrong and the people I work with relish proving me wrong. I don’t get snotty when I’m wrong and I don’t punish people for proving me wrong. Being wrong means that we’ve on the way to find the right way to do something. 

As the entrepreneur or owner of your small business, it’s very likely that you are the risk-taker and the fire-starter. Sometimes you are going to take a risk you shouldn’t or start a fire you shouldn’t and it’s up to your team to show you a better way forward.

If you are unable to take criticism, you are going to move very slowly towards the right answers. 

# **DELEGATE AND DO WELL**

Historically, I have sucked at delegating. 

Throughout the early years of my entrepreneurial career, I believed that I was the apex human in my business. If you worked for me, you were simply in my business to do the tasks that I didn’t want to do. 

How young and stupid I was. 

This belief is, however, very common among leaders and especially among business owners. For some reason, we believe that the most efficient way to do something is to do it ourselves no matter how much we have on our plate. Founders and owners think that team members are simply there to pick up the slack. It’s easy to look around at your team and believe that you are the most capable, the most intelligent, the best suited to do the work and generally the only one who truly knows how to get shit done. 

That leads you to hoard the work, to take on everything yourself and then to lash out at your team when you feel overwhelmed and burnt out. 

Learning to delegate is a skill that involves more than just learning how to do it. You have to set your business and your team up for success. 

When you are in the moment and looking for someone to take over a critical task it can feel like you are all alone and nobody understands your position as a leader doing all the work. The truth is that you are likely to blame for not being able to delegate to your team members. So let’s look at some reasons why you may be unable to delegate:

### You are working with the wrong people

If you look around your team and you realise that you’ve hired people based on how affordable their salary is versus how capable they are, you have a deep human resources issue. More often than not, leaders are unable to delegate because their team is sub-par. So the first thing you need to do is take a good look at who you trust in the organisation and who may need to leave or be given less responsibility. Not everyone is meant to be a manager, leader or key player. Thinking more strategically about your team’s structure will help you know when to delegate and to whom. 

### Your ego is the enemy

Almost every time I coach a leader about delegation I find that the problem sits squarely with their own inflated sense of self. They believe they are the only ones who can do a job well. They believe that they work harder than everyone and they believe that their quality of work surpasses their team’s by an order of magnitude. The brutal truth is that because you feel this way your team responds accordingly. They don’t try to help because they know you’ll knock them for their efforts. And so the cycle continues until you end up firing everyone and shutting down. 

If you have the right people in the right positions, use them and get over your ego. The best leaders help their teams achieve and they don’t hold them back. 

### There is too much work

Business is about survival, and survival means working hard and securing sales and new leads. We are all told to keep our pipelines full of new clients so that we never fall behind. The challenge that you face as a leader is balancing your pipeline with the resources you have to complete the work and keep up with demand. 

It’s your job to find this balance. It’s your job to make sure that your team’s mental and physical health comes first, not the client. If you are giving your team too much work and then expecting them to perform at 100% while never taking leave and working into the early hours of the next day, you will end up with average work and disgruntled staff. 

### Unrealistic expectations

You can do it quickly, you can do it right or you can do it cheaply but you can’t have all three. This is the problem that many small business owners face. You want all three. 

In an ideal world, you get all three and that’s why you end up doing it yourself. Your own time is free (in your head), you can do things fast (or so you believe) and you know how to do things right (and nobody else does). 

The variables at play here are simple to understand but complex to unravel. If you want things done right, give people enough time  to complete tasks to your standards. If you can’t do that, it’s your fault. If you want things done cheaply, get an average resource to do something quickly. You need to figure out which one of these variables is worth sacrificing. If none, pony up the cash to get the best resources to do the best work in an appropriate amount of time. 

### Tips and tricks to try

**1\. Choose the right person**  
Don’t simply delegate to the person standing closest to you (or the last person to email you). Choose the right person for the particular task. This involves taking the time to get to know your team and what their core skills are. 

**2\. Be clear about the objective**  
Giving clear instructions and laying out the success trigger for the task is imperative. Be as painstakingly clear as possible so that the person doing the work understands what you expect of them and what success looks like. 

**3\. Be consistent**  
Don’t flip-flop. Don’t forget. Don’t change your mind. If you do change your mind, be open about it, don’t blame other people because the facts changed; own the shift in direction. A rudderless ship is worse than a captain changing their mind. 

**4\. Know yourself**  
Understanding your own capabilities is a key part of being a leader. Being self-aware helps you and helps your team. If you know that you are terrible at client pitches, delegate the final pitch to someone who thrives in front of clients. Understand what you are great at and delegate the rest. 

**5\. Automate**  
There is so much that happens in a business today that can be automated that it’s a travesty to see small businesses plugging away at tasks that should be done by online tools. Automate whatever tasks you know do not require a human and let the humans do the things that machines can’t. Don’t save in the short term to suffer in the long term. Sure, tools cost money but they save time. 

# ENHANCING YOUR EMOTIONAL INTELLIGENCE

A question I am asked all the time by leaders of businesses big and small is: “How do you make people listen?”

The answer to this question is firmly planted in emotional intelligence. Empathy is a fairly overused word today because we’re all trying to find our feet in a world filled with wrong turns, wrong statements, cancel culture and a history of humans harming humans. We’re all trying to have empathy. For the purposes of this section, I want to use the following definition of empathy for leaders: the intellectual identification of the thoughts, feelings or state of another person. 

To really help people see your point of view, to “make them listen”, you need to have an intellectual understanding of their thoughts, feelings and state of existence. This is a superpower (along with your ability to welcome being wrong) that has been overlooked by leaders in the past. 

Leaders of the future do not focus on being smarter or more powerful than other people, they focus on how people feel and how those feelings are converted into action. 

Here a few ways to develop more empathy and enhance your emotional intelligence. 

### EQ vs IQ

Simply put, intelligence quotient (IQ) measures a person’s intelligence while emotional quotient (EQ) measures a person’s ability to identify, assess and control the emotions of oneself, of others and of groups.

There is, of course, no predetermined combination of IQ and EQ that is standard for everyone. It’s contextual and specific to each person but it’s very difficult to lead effectively based purely on IQ. Emotional intelligence isn’t a soft and ethereal concept for leaders to indulge, it’s a real-time method of gathering information about a situation, a person or a group and using that information in combination with one’s IQ to make the most effective decisions possible. 

So let’s figure out how to improve your EQ.

### Take five minutes

It’s easy to be the smartest person in the room (or at least to assume you are) but that doesn’t mean you should always be the first person to act, speak or respond. 

I try to take a few moments, minutes or beats, depending on the situation, before I respond to a question or situation. Here’s why taking a few minutes is helpful: 

* You can analyse your gut reaction before blurting it out.  
* You give other people in the room the chance to answer before you do.  
* You can assess the feeling in the room before you state your opinion.

Before you berate someone or provide an answer to an email, just take five minutes to let it sink in and try to put yourself in someone else’s position. This is a good practice to adopt in life generally. 

### Analyse your EQ

It’s easy to assume that you are an amazingly empathetic person who listens to the opinions of others and engages in a meaningful way. More often than not, your team won't see it the same way. 

Now, stick with me on this one, the best way to find out how you’re doing on the emotional front is to ask. Talk to your team about it. Of course, talking to the most recent or most junior employee isn’t going to yield the most honest answers, but if you talk to your leadership team and engage with them one on one to ask about your performance in meetings, your reaction to urgent situations and your general emotional state around your team, you are bound to get a few nuggets of gold to improve upon or to note for future engagements. 

Talk to one of the leaders in your team and ask them for honest feedback about your performance with your team. Listen; don’t talk back or justify your actions. Just listen. 

### Eat, sleep, exercise

It is extremely difficult to be emotionally aware when we are emotionally, mentally and physically drained. So put yourself first for a short while. Stop working 18-hour days, get 8 hours of sleep, do a bit of exercise every day and start eating slightly more healthy food every day. 

This might sound like silly and simple advice but if you are well rested, physically fit and feeling healthy, you feel capable enough to take on the emotions of your team. 

### Ask for help

I worked at a startup with a CEO who had close to zero EQ. He would call at all hours of the night, he would aggressively insist on things that made no sense and when they didn’t work out he would blame anyone but himself. He would forget important personal occasions and farm out any uncomfortable conversations, taking no responsibility for the mental and emotional state of his team. He defined the company culture purely by IQ and dollar signs but never the emotional state of our team. 

Can you guess what happened?

People left the company. They left quickly and in big numbers and his response was to belittle and malign them. It was never his fault, they were just too soft or didn’t like to work hard, or didn’t understand the vision or didn’t care. 

He never asked for help and he never got it. 

It’s OK if you struggle with EQ, lots of people do, but the greatest leaders do the hard work to improve. Ask for help from someone you know who has great EQ. Be open, be vulnerable and drop that ego. 

### Listen vs waiting to talk

I have sat in meetings with some very powerful and smart people and watched them while they listen to other people talk. Emotionally intelligent people are actively listening they are not waiting to talk. 

We have all experienced the difference between actively listening and waiting to talk. You know when your friend is telling you a story and you immediately realise you have a great additional story to tell and you stop really listening but start formulating what you’ll say as soon as they shut up? That is waiting to talk and not active listening. 

Your team knows when you are waiting to talk and that forces them to speak faster and finish up without feeling heard. Stop doing that and start listening to them. If they aren’t worth listening to, why did you hire them in the first place?

### Take criticism

Nobody likes to be told that they suck. But an emotionally intelligent leader understands that criticism can help them improve the state of their business and the relationships with their team members. Without criticism and an open and transparent culture, how do you expect to improve?

If people are too afraid to give you constructive criticism because they know you’ll fire them for it, you are never going to improve and you will stop attracting talent. 

# LEADERS KNOW HOW TO SAY NO

The early years of my entrepreneurial career were defined by one simple word; “Yes”. I said yes to a lot of things. I would dive headfirst into every possible opportunity that came my way. I would jump in, test it out, spend time on it and commit myself to it fully. 

I’m a loyal founder and I like to see things through. So I would say yes and then give it my all. I wouldn’t look up, I wouldn’t reevaluate and I wouldn’t reconsider my decision. I’d just build and build and build until it blew up, grew up or ended. 

Let me tell you right now that this is not the way to create value and build a successful entrepreneurial career. For me, the need to say yes to everything stems from a place of scarcity, not abundance; if I say no to this opportunity, there won’t be another one. This is patently untrue. Opportunity is everywhere and possibility is everywhere, but the right opportunity is hard to find and you don’t find it by saying yes to the wrong things. 

I want to push you to evaluate what you are working on, who you are working with, which clients you work for and why you are building the business you have chosen to build. 

It’s OK to say no, it’s OK to rethink, reframe and reconsider things and the insights below will help you build a framework for saying no more often and getting to the HELL YES faster. 

### Default No

A few years ago I decided to shift my default answer from yes to no. 

“Can we meet next week to discuss an opportunity?”  
My new reply: “No.”  
“Would you like to work with us on this new shiny thing?”  
My new reply: “No.”

The more you say no, the easier it gets. But here’s the trick: you can only say no to potentially interesting opportunities if you are working on something that you believe in. The thing you spend the most time on must be the thing you want to build, you have chosen to build and you are proud to own. 

I have learned that saying yes to everything is a second-order effect of not truly believing that you are working on the right thing. 

Answer these two questions for yourself: 

* Are you working on the right thing for now?   
* Can you see yourself working on this thing for the next five years?

If you say yes to both, you are lucky and need to keep focus and say no to everything else. 

### No is a full sentence

One of the most difficult things when saying no is finding a good reason to decline an opportunity. Most people think that if you are declining something it must come with some form of justification. That’s not true at all. 

You don’t owe anyone a reason for anything you do. You didn’t ask this person to present you with an opportunity. You didn’t ask your second in command to push you in this direction. You didn’t force your employee to be a horrible person. 

You are able to say no and only say no. You don’t always need to tell people why. Answering with a simple: “No, thank you,” is sufficient. Or saying: “No, don’t do that.” is appropriate. 

Learn to say no without following up with a justification and a world of liberated freedom awaits you.

### Letting go is saying No

Your business will be better off when you let go of harmful events, people, clients and suppliers. Just because you have always worked with someone doesn’t mean you always have to. 

The number of founders I work with who hang on to abusive clients or dismissive suppliers is astounding but unsurprising. Founders are not equipped to say no. We’re YES people. We like to make money and we have bills to pay. 

To keep the lights on you have to keep that shitty client who treats your team badly. The logic kind of makes sense but not if you pick at it. So let’s pick. 

If you retain a bad client who treats your team badly, your team becomes disgruntled. If your team is disgruntled, their work will suffer, they will show up but begrudgingly do the minimum required to get the client off their back. When they do the minimum required, the client is going to push back harder because the work isn’t of the best quality, which means the person has to do more work for the bad client. And on and on the cycle goes. 

So how do you manage this? You say no. You fire the bad clients and you prioritise your team. Why? Because you can (and should) go out and sell more to get the right kind of client. 

Remember, you don’t need all the clients, you need the right client and you should spend as much time as needed to figure out who that client is and where you can find them. Then go find them and sell to them. 

Say no to abusive client and supplier relationships. Say no to the staff that destroy your company culture. Say no to the executive who abuses your team members. It’s OK to let go and say no.

\<Sidebar\>  
**Own the No**  
Years ago I adopted a mantra from a very smart guy named Derek Sivers. The mantra is really simple and is summed up in one phrase: Hell Yeah, or No. 

To cope with all that life and business throws at me I ask myself if the work I’m about to agree to makes me say “HELL YEAH”. If it doesn’t, it’s a no. Say no to more things and focus. This one action will save you more mental anguish than just about anything else. Say no more often.   
What is it that you do? Yes, I’m actually asking you to think about the answer to this question. Because without a clear answer it’s very likely that you’ll say yes to anything.

Part of saying no to things is understanding what you say yes to. 

Finish this sentence: “I don’t do…”

What is it that you don’t do? You don’t do consulting. You don’t do design work. You don’t do group coaching. You don’t do free gigs. What don’t you do?

Define the rules. Write them down somewhere accessible and then stick to them. 

Focus is the key to succeeding and if you’re saying yes to everything then you are focused on nothing.   
\<Sidebar ends\>

### Useful ways to say No

**Establish boundaries upfront**  
When you have clearly set out the rules of engagement and the boundaries for people to observe, saying no becomes infinitely easier. Without clear boundaries, you will be forced to be mean in someone else’s eyes. For example, I have a very clear lateness policy that is widely understood – if you are 10 minutes late, I leave. It’s easy to say no to someone who wants to take advantage of that, because the rules are clear. 

**Two positives and a negative**  
To soften the blow of a hard no you can sandwich that two-letter word between two positives. For example, if someone asks if you’d like to work with them on a new project and you want to provide some feedback, you could say: 

“I am excited by this project and it looks to have great potential, but I am unable to commit the time right now. I am excited to follow your progress and believe you’re going to do very well.”

**Maybe is not an answer**  
I hate a non-committal answer. In fact, I don’t think that a maybe is an answer. So be firm with your no but be fair. If one of your team is asking for leave and you are unable to grant it, say so but tell them why. If there’s no good reason, don’t tell them why … but don’t leave them wondering if you just said yes or you may say yes if they ask again.

# 05\. CUSTOMERS

Figuring who you are selling to is an obvious but underestimated skill in business. Everyone talks about customers: customer acquisition, customer retention, customer profiles, customer-centric design, customer experience. But I don’t think that many leaders actually put the customer at the centre of their work. 

If you talk about being customer-first but can’t describe your exact target audience in great detail, you are not customer-first. If you talk about being customer-centric but don’t understand exactly what problem your target audience is trying to overcome, you are not customer-centric. If you don’t understand who your customer is, how do you know where to go to find them and sell to them? If you don’t know who they are, how do you talk to them? How do you build a community with them and how do you figure out what they want from you?

I made lots of mistakes when I was building my sock company. I opened a new store every three months, which is insanely fast for a first-time retail entrepreneur. One of the biggest mistakes was opening a store in an area where there was a lot of disposable income but not a lot of spending. I had a clear idea of exactly who my customer was meant to be but I did no research into the kind of customer who was visiting the mall where we were about to open our fourth store. It would have been simple enough to visit the mall many times, watch people’s spending habits, talk to them and get some anecdotal evidence, but I was too excited about the very exclusive mall and the very limited store space that I was being gifted. 

Here’s what I learned only once we had moved into this story: there is a high concentration of wealth in Tygervalley in Cape Town, because the people in this area don’t spend their money on expensive socks. They browse stores, the mall has high foot-traffic figures but spending per capita per square metre is insanely low. People in this area just didn’t care for bright, lush, expensive socks. Oops. That’s what you get for skimping on research. Six months and R100 000 later we closed that store and managed to find another tenant to take over the lease. 

Paying lip service to the customer isn’t going to work anymore. The good times have come and gone and now the hard work starts and it starts with the customer. 

This section of the book is more important than many leaders care to admit but without a deep and intricate understanding of your exact customer, your business is in trouble. It’s a difficult thing to admit when you don’t truly understand your customer but rather admit it early on so you can figure it out and get back on track. 

# GET TO KNOW YOUR CUSTOMER

Leaders have to pay attention to a lot of moving parts in the machine that is a business filled with people doing different things at any given time. Sometimes it’s inevitable that things fall through the cracks. Your customers could be one of those things.

When you start a business, one of the first things you do is identify your potential customer. Very often this is done through gut feel, market research and a variety of other potentially useful but sometimes irrelevant methods like the Living Standards Measure which may be outdated or too broad. 

However, your customer profile changes over time as does your business profile. You may offer different services, or you may have a different brand position but are still selling to your existing database. What you need to be doing is broadening your customer base, or finding new customers. Just because you knew your customer once does not mean that you know what they need or want today. 

The importance of knowing your customers cannot be overstated. If you know your customer, your customer will want to know you. If you are trying to sell an edgy high-fashion product to a customer base who likes to wear plain white T-shirts, you'll fail and your customers will be annoyed that you haven’t done your homework. 

Let’s look at a few tools and resources to help you get to know your customers or re-engage with them if it’s been a while. 

### Analytics

Everything you do online is tracked by someone or some business somewhere. Scary, but true. However it can be useful to you as a business owner. 

Your social media platforms provide analytics on who is following you, who engages with your content, who clicked what and where they went once they clicked that link you shared last week. Visit your profile and start gathering that data. Look at which posts received likes, comments and shares from which kind of person and then start creating more of the content to attract your desired target audience. Be proactive. 

For years “big data” has been a buzz word for businesses but very few businesses understand how to gain insights from big data. Insights are more important than big data. Big data implies a lot of data points and a lot of information that you need to sift through. That’s OK if you’re doing the hard work to find the insights. Big data is useless unless you’re able to get the insights, so take time to think about the data and pull out the valuable insights. 

Your website provides fantastic data and if you’ve been around for a while you could have years of information to trawl through. If you don’t have analytics set up, I suggest you head over to Google Analytics and start gathering the data you need so badly. 

### Call your customers

I love that in 2020 video became an acceptable means of communication. It is now completely appropriate to hop on a call with anyone from anywhere and get a really good feel for who they are. This is what you can and should be doing with your customers. 

It can be scary to reach out to people you haven’t met in person but I promise that many of them will appreciate the personal touch. You think you know what your customer wants? What better way to confirm your thinking than to ask them directly? 

If you have an email database, you can send out an email asking for 10 people to hop onto a short call and receive 20% off their order for doing so. Ask them about your service, what you can do better, what you aren’t doing that they might want from you. If you contact five customers a week, every week, you’ll begin to establish a clear picture of who you are selling to and what they need from you. Don’t be shy, they’ll love the personal touch. 

### Surveys

The old methods are often the best ones. Surveys can do wonders for your business. Make sure to ask questions along a specific theme and with clear intent. Don’t make the survey too long and don’t forget to promote the survey to your customer base so that you receive relevant data. You can use Survey Monkey or Google Forms to run surveys with ease. 

Getting the right data and insights out of a survey is more like an art form than a business function. Spend time researching the best questions to ask your target audience and how to phrase these questions to get the most objective answers. It’s also important to remember that people have the combined attention span of a toddler playing with a puppy, so chop out any fields in the survey that are not absolutely imperative. Cut what you don’t need and streamline towards the important bits. 

An alternative to long-form surveys is shorter, punchier social media surveys. Most of the platforms you are on will allow you to ask your customers questions. Do it. Ask them. Don’t be shy about the answers and beg them to be honest with you. 

### Reviews

There are myriad places for your customers to review you and your business. It can be intimidating to do this but you need to find the reviews and start paying attention to them. You are not running a billion-dollar business that’s too big to fail, and even those businesses reach such heights by listening to their customers and making changes where they have to. You may not agree with them all but these reviews are often brutally honest. At the very least they are public, so you should consider responding to the customer and opening a conversation. 

This is not to say that you must head over to Hello Peter and engage with every person who chooses to pick a fight with a business that is doing the best they can. Pick your battles, contact the customer and find out why they loved or hated you and how you can improve. 

Sites to check out: TripAdvisor, Trustpilot, Hello Peter, Google My Business, Facebook, Yelp

### Live chat

Just about every website today has a “live chat” feature when you are browsing. However, very few businesses use this feature to proactively engage with customers who land on the page. In my previous e-commerce business, I would sit on the live chat at least twice a month and talk with customers and potential customers who had landed on my homepage. The insights were surprising and invaluable, and the delight that the customer experienced by talking to the founder of the business was tangible and almost always led to me closing the sale while talking to them. 

Services to check out: Intercom, Crisp, oLark, Zendesk

### Email really works

Finally and most obviously, use email to your advantage. Every time you make a sale your customer should receive an email. Use that transactional email to ask them for some insights and feedback. If you send out a newsletter, use it to your advantage and ask things of your customer that you might not know already. Be curious about their lives, their habits, their concerns. Remember, businesses solve problems for customers. It’s your job to know what problems to solve and whether those problems change over time. 

# CUSTOMER RETENTION TACTICS

The customer is always right… 

This was once the prevailing opinion that businesses needed to take in order to thrive and retain their customers. I don’t think that is necessarily true today. I think today customers have agency, choice, freedom and the ability to associate and align with brands and businesses that they love and that treat them like adults. Showering your customers with attention, discounts and doing everything in your power to make them happy is a good idea if it fits with your business’s core values and profit model. 

There is another great customer saying that I love but is often forgotten because we believe that new is always better: it’s easier to retain customers than to find new ones. 

Finding new customers is one of the most difficult things that a business has to do. You grind day in and day out engaging in communities, finding your perfect target audience, talking to them, educating them, selling to them slowly and carefully. Then when they part with their money, you move on to the next new customer and just hope or assume that you’ve hooked the last customer forever. 

You are wrong. 

Customer retention can matter more than customer acquisition when it comes to profit margins.   
Every customer has what is known as a cost per acquisition (CPA). How much money your business spends to acquire a customer. This figure is incredibly high for first-time customers. The more a customer spends with you, the more profitable your acquisition cost becomes. Your job is, of course, to attract new customers to your business but first and foremost you should focus on retaining your existing customers for as long as possible. This figure is commonly known as lifetime value (LTV). Now we have the makings of a simple mathematical equation to help you understand the value of retention. 

If the CPA for a new customer is R100 and the LTV of a customer is R150 then you are making R50 profit per customer. If you can increase the LTV of your existing customer base to R350 then your profit is R250 per customer. You didn’t spend more money acquiring that extra R200 profit like you would have if the customer was a first time buyer. 

I’m always stunned when I come across entrepreneurs and business leaders who obsessively drive their teams to acquire new customers at an unsustainable rate. Sure, new customers are great but retaining them is probably more crucial to your success. There is no real rule of thumb for how much time you spend seeking new vs retaining old customers but it should reflect your churn rates. If you acquire ten customers per month and nine of them leave within that same month you should be spending majority of your time (80%+) trying to figure out how to retain the customers you have just acquired. If you are only losing three customers out of ten then you should be spending more time acquiring new costumes into your funnel because you are successfully retaining 70% of your newly acquired customers. 

### What is customer retention?

Customer retention involves a variety of activities that your business uses to retain customers, increase repeat purchases and grow your profitability. 

Customer acquisition introduces your business to a customer, while customer retention builds a relationship with them. 

### 100 true fans

To properly set the scene for customer retention I believe it’s useful to refer to an article written by US marketing specialist Kevin Kelly many years ago titled “1 000 True Fans”. The premise is so simple that you’ll relate to it immediately. When you start building your business it’s more important, according to Kelly, to acquire 1 000 fans who truly adore your brand and will be your biggest advocate and marketing machine. I’d like to break this down even further and suggest that, until you have your first 100 truly fanatical customers in your business, you should ignore everything else until you’ve cracked the code of this first 100\. If you have just 100 true fans spending R100 with you every month, you have R10 000 a month in revenue. By keeping these fans, your growth will be significantly easier than if you have to reacquire 100 fans every month. 

Churn hurts your growth. 

The other fantastic thing about your first 100 customers is that they become your early adopters who provide you with the feedback required to iterate and improve your product or service. These are the vocal 100 who want to hear from you, they want to receive your newsletter, want to follow you on Twitter, receive sneaky gift packs that they can talk about on their own social media and want to feel special because they discovered you first. 

It’s like those fans who discover a local band before anyone else does. They obsessively listen to the band live, buy their first single, tell their friends how amazing the music is, how hot the lead singer is, how crazy the drummer acts on stage and shove the merch into their friend’s hands whether the friends want it or not. 

Your first 100 become your loyal 100 and these people are worth 10 times their weight in revenue. 

### When to focus on retention

Not every business has the luxury of shifting focus (somewhat) from acquisition to retention. In the early stages of your business you must focus on acquiring new customers. Once you have your first few customers through the door you should be thinking about retention. Sure, it’s not your focus because you are still trying to grow your customer base but it’s devastating when you gain one customer and lose three.   
Your focus should shift to retention when your business has been growing and scaling for a while and you have a core customer base who knows you. Then it becomes profitable to increase repeat purchases from your existing customer base while consistently bringing in new customers. 

### The basics

What does basic customer retention look like? Good customer service.

What does good customer service look like? Expectation management. 

Let’s unpack this statement. Bad customer service (and therefore low customer retention) happens when your customer’s expectations are higher than your delivery of service. There are two things you can do: increase your customer service levels or lower your customer’s expectations. 

I think a combination of both is required. Don’t promise same-day delivery if you can’t execute on that promise 90% of the time. Don’t promise two-hour support responses if you can only manage responses within 24 hours. Setting the bar at an appropriate level goes a long way to retaining your customers and meeting their expectations. 

### Customer feedback

One of the best ways to retain your customers and increase their repeat purchase habit is to ask them for feedback. Be sincere and don’t be too pushy when you engage with them. Reward them for their feedback because it is helping you to improve your service and invariably improve your profit margins. 

Don't be nervous about including your customer in the building of your business; they’ll take ownership of the journey and your progress. People want your business to survive, so help them help you. 

### Customer inclusion

Gone are the days of hiding the inner workings of your business from your customer. I want to know how the sausage is made. I want behind the scenes. I want to be involved in your success and help you through difficult times. If your customers are loyal and your business is honest, you want to engage with the people spending their hard-earned money with you. 

Give them insights, ask them about pricing, show them upcoming features and get their feedback. The more they feel involved, the more likely they are to spend when you launch something new. 

### Reward loyalty

I have a rule in business: FPFP, Friends Pay Full Price. This applies almost universally to businesses when I launch, first-time customers, family, friends … you name it. But I do like to reward loyalty and so should you. 

Dive into your customer database and find the 20 customers who have spent the most money over the past 12 months. Contact each of them individually and give them a 50% discount on their next order. Make it a big discount so they really feel the value, hell, even send them a little free something with that order. Surprise and delight them. These are your super-fans. These are the people who will tell the world about your product and great service. Reward loyalty. Bake it into your business model. 

When it comes to retention there are three core metrics you should be looking at: 

**Repeat customer rate:** measures the percentage of customers willing to make a second purchase from you.

**Purchase frequency:** how often customers are coming back to buy from your store.

**Average Order Value (AOV):** the amount of money a customer spends in your store on each transaction.

Start looking at these metrics in your business, focus on increasing these figures and watch your profitability climb. 

### After-sale care

Making a sale is incredibly hard. If you are doing it consistently, I applaud you. There are e-commerce tools that can help you engage with your customers post-sale that you should probably be using if you aren’t already. This kind of tool ensures that you stay top of mind for your customers and lets them know that you have new and interesting things to show them, sell them and tell them. Take a look at CM Commerce and Mailchimp automations to get you started. 

### Customer service

Not all customer service is created equal. Some companies provide always-on, 24/7 customer service and this costs them a lot of money but gives their customers a sense of satisfaction and importance. Very rarely does doing it the other way ever work, but a Canadian supplier of cellphone skins and covers, called dbrand, went out of its way to give outrageously bad customer service on social media. Their customer service is so laughably bad that it’s become their calling card. People want the bad service and this approach helps dbrand to retain its customers. It’s almost as if the bad customer service they provided became part of the product they were selling\! It’s an unusual, smart tactic and one that I believe more businesses should deploy. We strive so hard these days to provide 24/7/365 service that we set ourselves up to fail. For most businesses this level of service becomes a noose around the neck of growth because it costs a lot of money to deploy service and employ people to provide the service. Eventually, you have serviced yourself out of decent profit margins. It’s unsustainable to provide always-on top-tier customer service. Even Amazon draws the line somewhere. 

The key thing about customer service is to set expectations upfront and deliver exactly what you promise. If you say 24/7 email support, you better deliver. If you say you have a phone line for customers to call, you'd better answer. The more you deliver the service you are promising, the more likely it will be that your customers will return and spend more with you. 

### Newsletters really work

Every year someone writes an article about the death of email newsletters. This has yet to actually happen. Not only are email newsletters *not* dead but they are thriving. 

The beauty of newsletters is that you own the experience and you own the database. 

If you have a list of customer emails you can start building out a quirky, fun and informative newsletter strategy to help your customers learn more about you, your business, your products and your vision for the future. People buy from people and your SME is no different. 

Send your customers a newsletter once a week, at the most, and give them loads of value that they can share around. They’ll look smart for sharing such great content, you’ll look smart for sending such great content out and it’s very likely that you’ll retain your customers and grow your database.

# CUSTOMER INFORMATION

Big data has been a hot topic for a while now but as a small business there often isn’t very much in the way of “big” data. You more likely have pockets of data gold lying around your office, your cloud storage platforms, your e-commerce website or scrawled in the back of a notebook somewhere. 

Data matters. Customer information is like cloud-based gold that is waiting to be mined, sifted, sorted and turned into profit. 

It’s easy to forget that you have a trove of valuable information at your fingertips that you just need to use to gain insights. Insights are the key here. Data and information are only valuable if you are using them effectively in your business. 

You may have made more than 3 000 sales but are you using those 3 000 email addresses effectively? You may have a loyal customer base but one that you very rarely engage with. You may have high traffic to your e-commerce site but low conversions and have no clue why that is. These are all issues that can be solved by effectively using customer information and other analytics tools to your benefit. 

So, let’s discuss customer information, what to do with it, how to store it and where to get it if you aren’t mining it already. 

### Know the law

South Africa’s Protection of Personal Information Act (POPIA) is one of the most progressive and protective in the world. POPIA focuses greatly on consumers’ right to privacy, requiring the secure and local storage of customer information.

The circumstances under which private data can be processed include these:

* The data subject (the customer or employee) consents to the processing.  
* Processing is necessary to carry out actions for the conclusion or performance of a contract to which the data subject is a party.  
* Processing complies with an obligation imposed by law on the responsible party.  
* Processing protects a legitimate interest of the data subject.  
* Processing is necessary for the proper performance of a public-law duty by a public body.  
* Processing is necessary for pursuing the legitimate interests of the responsible party or of a third party to whom the information is supplied.

In short, don’t abuse your customer’s data. Collect it, store it and ensure that you use it for things relevant to your business and their transactions only and make sure that your customers have opted in to receive communication. 

### Why customer information matters

Whether you know it or not, your customers are talking to you all the time. They are talking to you when they don’t make a purchase. They are talking to you when they visit your website but leave empty-handed. When they share your products on social media. When they go through the checkout process but stop before parting with their money. When they complain, when they email you and all the other times they interact with you and your business. 

This is why customer information matters. This is why it is important for your business to store data and use it to your benefit. There are little nuggets of information strewn throughout your workflow and throughout your business. You need to start reviewing the data available to you and increasing how much data you have about your customers and your business. It’s imperative that you can pin customer actions to their specific profiles within your business and then use the data you receive to improve the customer experience.

### Important information

How do you know what information matters? It depends on your business, of course, but there are some basic questions to ask of your business: 

* What opportunities do you have to ask for information?  
* Are you storing contact information?   
* Do you have their email address and phone number?  
* What are you doing with the information you have?  
* How frequently do you want to communicate with your customers?

### Collecting customer information

There are lots of places that give you an opportunity to ask customers for information that you can use to improve their experience. Here are a few ideas: 

* Online checkout  
* Enquiries via email or social media  
* Complaints  
* Customer reward programmes  
* Customer satisfaction surveys  
* Competitions  
* Your website  
* In-store at checkout

If you have access to these opportunities you should be asking your customers to share their information with you. This is a way for you to start building a relationship with them.

### Storing customer information

The simplest way to get started with storing information is the good old spreadsheet. Using a Google Doc allows your entire team to access the sheet and add to it when necessary. This is a more complex method down the line because the data is in raw format. 

Ideally, you want to use a customer-relationship management tool like Pipedrive. There are businesses built off the back of this problem like Mixpanel. These services require more integration and cost more but if you are starting to scale they can be the difference between recurring purchases and losing a customer. 

However you choose to store the information your customers are giving you I can confidently say that writing it down on a piece of paper is the worst option. Move your customer information, purchase history, browsing data and any other useful information into a place that is digital, cloud-based and usable. 

### How do you use customer information?

Carefully. That’s how you use customer information. It’s not one of those situations where you can simply dive in and go nuts. You have to think through the touch-points with your customer so that you don’t overwhelm them. 

Things you could do with your customer information: 

* Use their phone numbers to call them and find out if they were happy with your service or product.  
* Start building a newsletter that appeals to your customer’s habits and interests.  
* Cross-reference customer information with purchase history to build out trends in your business.  
* Use Google Analytics (or similar services) to find out more about their purchase habits and interests.

It’s easy to forget that customers are the key to unlocking hidden potential in your business. They are talking to you all the time, whether it’s with their mouths, fingers or clicks. You just need to be listening.

# 06\. MARKETING

Marketing is tough business. Your business and brand are personal to you and it’s easy to imagine that no one will do as good a job as you when it comes to talking about your own business. That is true to a point.

It’s time to look at the various options available to you when it comes to marketing and advertising. 

There is no silver bullet to solving all of your marketing problems. It is difficult to find a single agency that can effectively bring you search engine optimisation, AdWords efficiencies, social media marketing, brilliant ideas and execute on all levels. The agencies who claim to do it all rarely can or charge so much that most businesses would go bust just hiring them. 

So that leaves you in a quandary. Never fear, there is always something to learn, a new tool to try, a cool message to experiment with, a community to build and a plan to hatch. 

# Community 

Communities are built by excluding people, not including people. 

That feels strange to type and I’m sure it feels strange to read but it’s true. I don’t want to be part of a community that includes everyone in the world. That sounds altruistic and pleasant, sure but it’s not real. Humans don’t relate to all humans. We relate to humans like us. To be painfully clear here, humans need to respect each other and value eachother but we don’t have to like or relate to eachother at all. 

Your marketing efforts should probably start with a community of people that you’re solving a problem for. Who are they? Where do you find them? How do they speak? What slang do they use? How do they dress? Do you fit in with them? Can you solve a problem for them? Do you relate to them?

If you can figure out the answers to the questions above then you’re on the way to building a fantastic community that will value you and your business. 

Your marketing message, advertising channels, social media tools and everything else will cascade from this core community.

# Marketing vs Advertising

When it comes to the crunch, marketing is really about making a sale. Sales keep your business alive, not funding or charity. Sales. So when we talk about marketing what we’re really talking about is making a sale. Advertising is one form of marketing. It’s important to understand the distinction between the two. 

The difference is important because there are “experts” in advertising who will tell you that only an agency can help your business effectively. This isn’t entirely true or entirely false. It’s a matter of context. You can market your business in a million different ways that may not include spending actual money to advertise somewhere. Great marketing is about great storytelling. Whether you realise it or not your business needs to tell a good story to make sales. 

Let’s look at the different marketing concepts you can consider.

## Target audience

The very first question to ask when it comes to marketing should be “Who is your customer?” If you know exactly who your target audience is then you are on the road to figuring out what kind of marketing you need to do. If your customer is over the age of 55 then you will be looking at vastly different platforms and strategies compared to a customer below the age of 25\. 

So before you think about outsourcing or growing your internal competencies you must figure out where your customer lives and then be there. 

***I recommend reviewing Section 5 for more detailed insights into your target audience and how to niche down.*** 

## Storytelling

Sometimes the best marketing is simply telling a brilliant story. If you have that part of your brand figured out and you understand your customer well enough, all that is left is telling the story on the right platforms. 

Think about what story you are telling, which medium will help you bring the story to life (video, audio, text, short post, long articles, etc) and how often you want to tell this story. Then go out and start talking to your customers. 

When you have done this for a while, you can consider hiring an agency to help you fine-tune your story, recraft it for the appropriate platform or produce content on a consistent basis. 

In the past I’ve struggled to find traction with what I believed were great marketing ideas. One year for father’s day a few years ago when I was still running my fashion company I decided to roll out a more inclusive campaign targeting all different kinds of father figures. We did a photoshoot with family units that had two moms, two dads, no dads, grandparents only, you get the idea. And we added specials on our various socks for different foot sizes. I thought the campaign was brilliant. 

Here’s the thing though, my customers were all traditional dads who didn’t care for my diversity and inclusion tactics. I still stand by my campaign because it sent a positive message at a good time but it didn’t sell any more socks than usual. It was a failure. I told the wrong story to the right customer and suffered for it. It’s important to state that you shouldn’t go around changing your story to fit different potential customers. That’s not marketing, that’s lying or not telling a consistent truth. 

What you really want to do is find the customer who resonates with your story. 

## Social media

For the past few years, social media has been all the rage. At one point you were considered crazy if you weren’t on every platform talking to everyone all the time. That is no longer the case. With so many platforms popping up – Twitter, Facebook, YouTube, TikTok, Snap, Instagram – it is very easy to get started but difficult to gain traction. 

Social media agencies will tell you that they have secret ways and special tricks to help you grow faster but the truth is this: consistency is key. And that’s one of the reasons you may want to consider hiring an agency to help you. If you are unable to post on social media and engage with your target audience effectively every day, you need help. The key to effectively using a social media agency lies in your strategy. What platform do you need help with? How often do you want to post? What happens to customer complaints? Figure this out and an agency can be very effective. However, you are hiring a single resource to do a single job. 

You can also look to hire internally and train someone to speak for your brand. The upside to hiring an internal social media person is that this person can learn more about your business and expand their role. This can be a good value-for-money option. 

## Search Engine Optimisation

Search Engine Optimisation (SEO) is an integral part of marketing your business. It’s difficult to absolutely nail SEO when you are juggling many balls in the business. SEO requires dedicated time and attention and is a slow-burning marketing tactic. The better your search engine optimisation is, the more visible you become to search engines like Google or DuckDuckGo. 

I would love to suggest that you hire an SEO expert to take your business to the next level but there are a lot of charlatans in this space. The difficult thing about SEO is that if you get it wrong it can destroy your search ranking and make your company website completely undiscoverable. 

It’s a good idea to upskill yourself about the general requirements of good SEO and then find a copywriter with SEO experience. I do not suggest hiring an agency to manage your SEO unless they are deeply embedded in your content production schedule and the creation of the content. This is something you should consider outsourcing when you are big enough to afford to take a risk on a resource that may not work out. 

## Digital advertising

Digital advertising is another industry filled with liars, fakes and frauds. There are lots of companies who claim to be good at acquiring customers but all they are after is their 16% commission on top of the budget you are spending with them. Beware of these businesses. 

Conversely, figuring out the nuances of digital advertising can be all-encompassing for an entrepreneur without these skills. You have to know which platform you want to spend money on, what a good cost-per-click is, what a good cost-per-lead might be and what a good cost-per-acquisition is. If you don’t know what these terms mean, Google them\!

If you are seriously considering hiring an agency to manage your advertising online, I recommend asking them for references and talk to previous customers about their performance. It’s easy to blow your budget on digital ads. 

If you want to dive in lightly with Google search ads, I suggest visiting MyAdBot.com and registering an account with them to start with. They provide a great service that is easy for beginners to understand and dive right in. 

# BRANDING

It’s amazing how central a customer is to every single business. Often we believe that our businesses are the focus of the experience when it comes to selling things but the truth is that our customers are the sun and our businesses merely revolve around them. 

This is important because when you are building a brand it’s easy to think that the brand is about you and your business. How your customers *perceive* your business is your brand. You are giving an identity to your business that your customers will either like and embrace or not like and disregard. 

This can take the shape of your logo, the colours you choose, the language you use, the kind of packaging you send your items in and even the level of customer service you choose to uphold. All of these things contribute to how your customers experience your business. Said another way, all of these things create your brand. The tricky part is knowing when to go it alone and build your own brand and branding, and when to hire an agency filled with experts to guide you. 

Let’s look at your branding options and whether to hire an internal resource or outsource your branding to an agency or third party. 

## Brand DNA

In the early stages of your business, it’s very difficult to hire an agency to dictate how your customers will perceive your brand. Small businesses live and die on the brand they create and customer satisfaction with that brand. It’s one of the few things that can separate an SME from a larger corporate: personalised brand and service. 

When I started my sock company, I made the decision to elevate the brand experience with expensive packaging, a clean logo, a custom scent in the box that was matched in our stores nationwide. This was how I chose to create a brand and an experience for my customers. Sure, I could have hired an agency to assist with this but I was talking to my customers every day and had a clear sense of what they wanted from us. 

It’s up to you to define your brand identity at the beginning.

## Expertise

It is important when building your brand to understand what you are good at and what you should be outsourcing. I am not a logo designer. I can’t design packaging. I don’t know how to design a store experience from scratch. But I do understand the DNA of my brand and I define the vision for the company. I like to work on high-level branding and messaging internally and then when it comes to key parts of the brand, I generally find experts to work with who can elevate my thinking to a professional level.

If you are just starting out and need a logo, you could design one yourself which would save you some money. The alternative is to outsource this to a site like Fiverr or Upwork. 

Hiring an agency to do your logo, set up the branding and define the tone of your business is not necessary and very expensive to get done right. This is better left for a later stage in your business as you evolve and grow. 

## Creating a voice

Outsourcing your business’s voice is always going to feel strange. As an SME it’s hard to believe that anyone else could ever take on your brand voice and do it justice. There is going to come a time when, as the founder of the company, your time is better spent elsewhere in the business. This means you will need to hire someone to do the job. If you want more close control, hiring someone full-time in your business is a good option but can be more costly. 

You can consider a blended approach, using an outsourced person who works part-time on your work but isn’t on staff. This gives you flexibility and avoids the massive investment of hiring an agency to do the work for your company. 

## Be consistent

There is definitely value in hiring someone to engage with customers, post on social media and keep the brand identity flowing through everything you do. It’s surprisingly easy to drop off the face of the Earth after you do the hard work upfront, define the language and tone you want to use, set the colours and build the website.

The key to actually building a brand is being consistent with it. You can’t show up on day 1 and then disappear. Remember, brand building is about customer perception; if you aren’t present or consistent, you are not engaging with your customers and cementing your brand. 

A good way to enable your brand to permeate through your business is to have a clear vision and mission statement that your team buys into and believes in. Once you have a team that believes in your mission and vision, it’s your job to constantly remind them what they’re fighting for and what they’re building. 

Imagine if Nando’s decided to do cheeky, smart and risky advertising for a few months and then changed tactics, switched brand identity and moved on to another idea. As a customer, you would be very confused and frustrated. Consistency is key with brands and branding.

# CONTENT CREATION

In a world filled with more content than any single person could consume in a thousand lifetimes, it’s difficult to stand out in the content crowd and add real value. But your business definitely should be producing content to engage your potential customers. The kind of content you need to produce is obviously specific to your industry, your ability, your skillset and where you believe your potential customers are. 

If there is one thing I’ve learned over almost two decades of producing content it’s that you cannot successfully produce content for every available platform. You don’t have to publish where the cool kids play and if you try to do it all you will spread yourself too thin. 

People respond well to authenticity. Create content in your own voice using your own words and doing something that you believe in. If you fake it people will sniff this out and move swiftly past your branded content. Add value with every article, video, tweet or post and you are bound to get a positive reaction from people who come across it. That’s the key right there. Because if it resonates, people will share it. A good idea is to follow the brands that resonate with you, subscribe to their newsletters and start to analyse why you love their branding and communication so much. Is it their tone or the words they use? Short sentences or long descriptive paragraphs? Become obsessive about what you engage in from brands and you’ll start to learn how to construct your own brand personality. 

Most businesses have a website and on said website, most businesses have a blog and on their blog, they have very little content worth anything. This is a massive missed opportunity. Your blog should be populated with industry-specific articles, videos and opinions that help define you and your company as thought leaders. If you can’t do this then it’s probably not worth having a blog at all.  

Content production is difficult and can really take up a lot of time for a business owner but in the age of information, it’s important to contribute meaningfully and add value to your customers. 

Here are my top content production tips for your business:

## Pick a lane

You can’t be everything to everyone and you shouldn’t want to be. Pick your topic of focus and focus on that topic. If you are in the business of producing sustainable clothing, for example, then you should double down on this vertical and produce content that positions you and your business as the thought leaders and the go-to place to find any information about sustainable clothing. 

## Do not rush 

Becoming an authority on a topic takes time and consistency. Most of the businesses I work with want their content to go viral after posting one video on YouTube. Unfortunately that’s just not how it works. You have to do what you do for a long time before it really starts to work. This is true for search engine optimisation, building a Twitter following or growing traffic to your blog. If it’s worth doing, it’s worth being patient. 

## Post more often

It’s better to be prolific than to be perfect. Stop trying to make the lighting perfect in your videos. Stop trying to get the structure of your article just so. Stop trying to figure out how to “go viral” and just produce. Produce more content than you think you should because the world is hungry for information and most algorithms reward consistency and volume. Of course quality matters but every article, video or post you push out can’t be a masterpiece. You also don’t really know what’s going to resonate with your target audience. 

## Define your target audience

This is such an important point that is so often overlooked. Do you have a good idea about who you are talking to and trying to engage with? If you do then figure out where they are and go play there. If you don’t know who your customer is then you better start figuring that out. If you are trying to sell products to 50 year olds but are posting on TikTok then you are in for a horrible and youthful surprise. 

## Choose your platform

Is yours a company that can embrace blogging or tweeting? Do you want to focus on Instagram or YouTube? Are you planning on using social media as a sales channel? It’s extremely important to know which platform has the biggest potential to turn content into sales. The more places you have to engage with the more difficult it’s going to be to engage so be careful not to overcommit.

## Be consistent

This is probably the most obvious, most underrated but most important point of them all. Wherever you choose to post, just do it consistently. If you tell your audience you will send out a weekly newsletter via email then send it out every week at the same time and do that for an entire year and see how you go. Doing it for a few weeks won't give you any good results. Remember, patience and consistency really do count. The book that you are reading right now is a good example of consistently adding value. I wrote a newsletter for 75 weeks in a row providing entrepreneurs, managers and business leaders with tips, tricks, news and insights and it paid off. People respond to my emails every week, they engage with the content and now you’re reading the book. Consistently add value and you’ve won half the battle. 

## Batch-create

If you have time to write one newsletter, you probably have time to write two. If you have time to upload one TikTok, you have time to create two or three. This may sound stupidly obvious but the insane time-saving that this little trick will give you is well worth the effort. 

Think about it: if you produce two videos on Monday, you have content for this week and next week. If you produce two videos the following Monday, you have content stored for three weeks. If you produce two more videos the following Monday, you now have content ready for weeks in advance. And on and on the compounding creating of content goes. Don’t be a chump and produce to publish on the day. Produce to publish and store. 

This is important because life happens and there will very likely always be more important things than that one blog post for this week. But that’s the wrong approach to content. If you are consistent and produce high-quality content, there probably aren’t too many more important things to pay attention to that are low effort and such high impact. However, when the crisis hits, the content stops. Don’t let it. 

## Create then consume

I have a rule for content consumption on social media: 

I don’t consume until I create. 

A big chunk of my work involves creating content that positions me as a thought leader. That means that I have to spend a lot of time on social media engaging with my content and with other people or creators. Sometimes that leads to a rabbit hole of social media and before I know it two hours have passed by. I don’t mind losing two hours if I’ve uploaded two TikTok videos, one YouTube video, a newsletter and a few tweets. 

But if all you’re doing is consuming, you are the product and there’s nothing useful in being a product. 

Create then consume.

# MAYBE, YES & NO

When I was building one of my early startups called Motribe I was relatively inexperienced when it came to the sales process, sales cycles and closing a deal. I pitched to a lot of potential clients for that business and most of them came back to me with some version of “maybe”. Maybe they’ll consider it in the new financial year or maybe they’ll have the budget when their next client signs up. Maybe if we add features A, B or C they’ll consider it for their next integration or maybe if they can get some time with the CEO they’ll get her to take a look. 

I felt like my entire life was one big “maybe” waiting to be turned into a converted sale and that “yes” that I’d been hunting for so long. 

This constant back-footed strategy focused on the “maybe” lead to longer sales cycles, wasted energy and lost opportunity. It was taking me anything from 12 to 18 months from first contact to close what should have been a relatively simple sale. 

It’s natural for founders to be optimistic but my faith in people and blind optimism was destroying my ability to effectively sell my product. That business taught me that there really are only two answers to a sales pitch: YES\! When can we start? Or No. 

“Next month”– means no.  
“Sure, just give us a bit of time to decide" – means no.   
“Come back in a few weeks and we can discuss the potential to partner with you in some form” – is a long-winded way of saying no. 

It’s your job as the founder or leader of the sales department to ignore the distractions and find your focus. Maybes from potential clients are actually nos just waiting to be tossed aside, so get there quicker. 

What you need to realise early on is that the right customer with the right problem will say “YES\!” immediately when the right solution presents itself. I spent too much time early in my career trying desperately to make people like whatever I was selling while I should have been paying attention to the feedback and studying it. If I had been studying the feedback, I would have learned sooner that when you find a willing customer there are no games, there is no real complexity to closing a deal and they won't mess you around. They *want* what you’re selling and you move very quickly to the next phase, which is deal negotiation and price. 

In short, move past the “no” very quickly, ignore anything that smells like a “maybe” and viciously hunt for the “YES\!” clients.  
 

# THINK MORE ABOUT SALES

Let’s look at a few high-level concepts to help you find your footing and rethink the kind of selling you’re doing right now.

## Sell more

It may sound crazy, but “sell more” is the best sales advice I’ve ever received. If you think you’re selling enough, double your efforts. Sales and business development are both mathematical problems. We think of them as soft skills that require finesse and that’s true to a point but really it’s about numbers. How many people or businesses are in your pipeline? Are you actively hunting new leads or are you passively waiting for them to find you?

## Fewer better

I try not to contradict myself, especially so quickly in a single book, but sometimes selling more is *not* the answer. Sometimes you need to do like my friend and fellow entrepreneur Howard Mann advises and focus on "fewer better". 

If you’re unable to make increased volume your sales strategy of choice, the more sensible way is to do fewer things better and figure out exactly who your customer is, exactly where they hang out, exactly what they’re looking for … and then to sell to fewer potential customers but do it better. 

Can your message be tighter? Can your story be more eloquently told? Can your product be shown in a new way to help the right customer realise they need your product? How can you make fewer sales better? Should you focus on fewer sales methods and make them better? Should you focus on fewer social media channels and get better at them?

## Assess existing channels

Every sales channel that you are using right now needs to be evaluated on a revenue basis. Is the channel still serving its purpose and generating income? If you are spending on Instagram, Facebook or Google search ads, are they all converting at the rate you want them to? If you have a salesperson or team, are they converting at the rate you need them to? Would it make a difference if you outsourced sales?

## Your team

Your team are your brand ambassadors. If you look after them and they believe in what they are selling, they will communicate positively about your brand and business at every opportunity. Ensure you have their buy-in – and make it easy for people to find them online. 

What many business leaders fail to understand is that everyone in their business is a sales person whether they know it or not. Everyone is a publisher with Twitter, Instagram, WhatsApp status updates, Facebook stories and all the other places people tell stories. 

If people are able to tell stories, they’re going to be telling stories about their work and your business. So prepare everyone. Help everyone on your team talk effectively on a basic level to a potential customer. 

I like to tell first-time entrepreneurs to focus on their friends and family when trying to make their first sale. Not because I want their friends and family to buy from them, but because each friend has a group of another 20 friends that they may talk to about your business. If they are properly equipped to talk about what they do, why they do and why it may be useful, they immediately become better salespeople for you. 

## Your existing customers

Don’t ignore the people who have been loyal to you. Embrace them and help them as much as you can. Then engage with them and find ways to make them your most vocal brand ambassadors. The time of micro-influence is upon us. You know that customer with 500 followers on Instagram? If they love you, they’re going to post about you and convert their friends. Secure their loyalty with incredible service and kind communication. 

In Section 5 I talk about customer retention and this is where that loop closes. Your existing customers are a sales channel for you. If they’re happy and they love your business, they’ll be talking about it. If they’re unhappy and hate your business, they’ll be talking about it. So best you make sure they love you and are talking about your business in the best way possible. 

Let’s put this into the clearest terms: acquiring a new customer is 6 to 7 times more expensive than retaining an existing customer. Added to that, the probability of a sale from a brand-new customer is only 5-20% whereas the probability of a sale from an existing customer is between 60-70%. You get where I’m going with this. Take care of your existing customers. 

## Different kinds of meetings

The era of a coffee introduction is gone. Sure, we all love coffee but an in-person meeting is inefficient as a first engagement. Embrace the digital meeting, keep it short, send an agenda and get right to the point.

Throughout 2021 I have been pioneering the 10-minute pitch meeting and I urge you to try it out. The basic idea is that I am so certain in my understanding of the problems my target audience is experiencing that if I can just get 10 minutes with a decision-maker, I can probably close a sale. So that’s what I pitch them over email, LinkedIn or cold calls: just give me 10 minutes of your time and if you’re interested to hear more after that, we can set up a time to discuss working together. 

Then I make damn sure that in that 10 minutes I have high energy, I have slides prepared, I have a clear idea of their problem and how I can solve it and I am damn careful not to run over 10 minutes unless the client specifically asks to continue. 

The 10-minute-to-win-it meeting is my go-to strategy and I recommend that you start deploying it as soon as you can. 

## Partnerships

Have you always wanted to work with a specific brand or business? Now is the time to reach out to them and pitch something amazing. The emphasis there is on amazing. Don’t send Gucci an email and ask them to partner with you because you’re cool, they’re cool and you think you could do cool things together. Nope. That won’t work. Gucci is probably not the right brand for you and secondly, they receive tens of thousands of queries just like yours every year. 

Find businesses or brands with a similar customer profile to your own and suggest a joint competition or marketing promotion. Add their products to your newsletters, create a collaborative product that you can jointly promote or get together and do something to help your customers together. But don’t half-ass it. If you’re going to partner up, do it with style and flair, and go all the way. Offer up tens of thousands of rands in prizes or an annual subscription or something that people will tell their friends about. 

## Competitions

Speaking of giving things away, competitions are a fantastic way to grow a database. But it only works if the prize is big enough to bother entering and if the mechanism is simple enough to get people to enter. 

Growing and owning a database of relevant people is an important method of lead nurturing. People love to enter competitions and there are tools like ViralSweep, Gleam and others that help you to grow your entries and reward customers. 

An incredible illustration of this go-big mantra is the YouTuber, Mr Beast. His YouTube videos often cost upwards of $1m to produce because he will do insane things like make 100 people touch a Lamborghini and give it away to the last person standing. He’ll give away houses, randomly give people $10 000 to do something he asks them to, and on and on. Imagine those same YouTube videos if he was offering up $500 or a one night stay at a random AirBnB in some strange place. It just wouldn’t work. 

My own version of this story is how I launched my last book. I wanted to help people start side hustles so I started a fund and gave away R1 000 a day and committed to do that for one year. In 2021 I turned this initiative into a non-profit company that still functions and has funded over 200 businesses so far. In the first six months of this competition, over 12 000 people entered and at writing, we’re closing in on the 25 000 mark. I could have just launched the book with a webinar but that wasn’t going to gain me 25 000 new people in my database and wasn’t going to help start 200 businesses\!

## Add value

Selling your features is a pre-Covid-19 tactic. Today, the best way to find new clients is to add value to the community you are trying to serve. Join Facebook groups, LinkedIn groups, head to Reddit and find a relevant subreddit that your customers might be interacting with, engage with people on Quora and answer questions because you’re an expert.

## Landing page optimisation

This is a more technical suggestion but often we do the hard work to promote our businesses online, spend money on advertising, marketing, content creation, social media marketing and all the other methods under the sun and then we have a terrible landing page that tells a boring story with really average images and no call to action. 

Landing pages are the lifeblood of an online business (any business, really). If you own a restaurant and your website landing page is filled with bad photos of old food and an unreadable font, it’s likely that the person will try and book a table somewhere else. If you’re an activewear brand and your Instagram images are on point but your landing page features old images and outdated lines of clothing and your customers don't understand why they should buy from you, you’re going to trip at the final hurdle and not acquire that new customer. 

Some basic landing page tips: 

**Marketing must match landing.** If your marketing is in bright pink and your landing page is a dark yellow, you are going to confuse your potential customer. If you have different fonts, narratives, keywords and basic images, you are going to confuse your customer. The goal is to have an additive landing page, not a completely new experience. You’ve spent money and time crafting a good advert or piece of content so make sure that your landing page matches up. 

**Fresh content must feature.** Don’t keep old content at the top of your landing page. Make it the most compelling, fresh and interesting content or your lead will leave. 

**Less is definitely more.** Your landing page should not ask people to do 17 different things. In fact, I’d go so far as to suggest that the website navigation shouldn’t even appear on a sales landing page. You should be trying to get people to do one thing and that one thing is buy from you right now. If you’re asking them to sign up to a newsletter, subscribe to your YouTube channel and then finally make a purchase, you’re going to lose them.

It’s time to become more precise about your sales and business development. The most affordable and straightforward way to manage your pipeline has to be Google Sheets or Excel. But Salesflare has a useful free template that you can download to get you started. 

Or, if you are willing to pay a small monthly fee for your sales management and online hosting, there are other options including Pipedrive, Monday.com, Insightly.

If you don’t know how many potential customers you have contacted and you don’t know how many of those have clicked on your advert or how many of those have visited your website and how many of those have made a purchase, how do you know what’s working?

# LEADING VS LAGGING INDICATORS

There is no crystal ball in business to help you see the future. There is no magic 8 ball to help you know which decision is the right one to take. OK, so there is a magic 8 ball but trusting the answers from this tiny ball is as good as throwing pasta on the wall to check if it’s done yet. You won’t really know either way. 

There are, however, leading and lagging indicators to help you figure out what’s working and what’s failing in your business. 

It’s easy to become obsessed with metrics that tell you about the past, something like revenue does that. You track your revenue and believe that it’s a good indicator of how your business is performing. Unfortunately, revenue is not a leading indicator. Revenue tells you what you did in the past that worked, it shows you which parts of your business are ready to scale, it can’t tell you if what you are working on right now is going to lead to any significant uptick in profit or if you’re building towards something good. But revenue is not going to tell you what you should be working on right now to move the needle. 

To get a better idea of where your business is headed you need to track leading indicators. It’s tough to understand how to structure your business to cater for both leading and lagging indicators because both are relevant at different points in your business journey. 

I hate acronyms with a passion. In fact, I hate acronyms as much as I dislike investors with no business experience telling me how to run my company. Sure, sometimes they make life simpler but on the whole, they are confusing and add complexity and panic to an already difficult endeavour. One of my least favourite acronyms is KPIs or key performance indicators. KPIs exist in corporate speak to make people understand that there are indicators that tell the organisation how you are doing, how your project is doing and how the company is doing. 

But it’s never that simple. Not all indicators are made the same. Not all indicators point in the right direction and not all businesses are set up to effectively track, measure and understand the indicators they are looking at. 

KPIs can be broken down into two core areas for your business; leading and lagging. 

Simply put, leading indicators measure change and lagging indicators measure results. 

Leading \= change.   
Lagging \= results. 

These kinds of indicators are imperative to building a successful sales strategy. Are you tracking and monitoring things that you did six months ago by looking at revenue today? Or are you just happy that revenue today is up even if you’re not clear exactly why? Monitoring and tracking the right kind of indicators makes a massive difference to what you work on and understanding exactly what you are doing that is working in your business. 

For a long time, I have told entrepreneurs that sales are actually about maths. Selling is as much an art as it is about sitting down and doing the hard work of actually selling. 

If you make one sales call per day, it is very unlikely that you’ll hit your sales targets for the month. As a general rule, you can assume a 10% response rate to your sales calls or emails. If you make 10 phone calls you can expect one positive response. If you make one phone call, it’s impossible to receive 0.1 phone calls back. 

The number of phone calls you make in a day is called a leading indicator. This leading indicator does a pretty good job of predicting whether you’ll reach your sales figures for the month. 

You may think this is an oversimplification but I urge you to give it a try. If you are trying to close 10 sales this month, set yourself the task of making 100 phone calls in the month (five per day for 20 days) and see how you go. 

Of course, it isn’t all about volume. You also need to make sure that you are telling the right story and figuring out which problem to solve for your customer. The wrong story told to the right customer means no sale. The right story to the right customer solving the wrong problem means no sale. 

## What is a leading indicator?

Leading indicators predict success or failure. They measure actions that generate results. They lead the way for your business. 

If you know that you have to increase sales because you aren’t hitting your targets, you have to do more than look at the sales figures. Sales figures are lagging indicators and can’t help you improve sales. 

To improve sales you’d have to turn to your leading indicators. For example, 

* How many sales calls are being made every day?  
* How many people are registering for your service?  
* Advertising conversion rate

Achieving higher scores on the leading indicators will help you reach your revenue goals (lagging indicator).

### Questions to consider

As you start to integrate leading indicators into your business, you should begin to ask yourself and your team future-focused questions that impact your day to day operations, questions like: 

* What processes can I employ to increase our revenue?  
* What skills can the team improve to better achieve the desired outcome?  
* What steps can be taken to speed up product development?

Just because the leading indicators are future-focused does not mean that they ignore the present. In fact, the questions above should lead you to define clear benchmarks for you and your team to strive towards.

### Why do businesses ignore leading indicators?

Overall, lagging indicators are much easier to track. You’re probably using them right now to monitor the stats of your business – things like revenue or actions taken by your customers that can’t be fixed, changed or improved upon. 

Leading indicators are more difficult to define, track and implement throughout your business. 

You should think of a leading indicator as a signal. Signals can be read, interpreted and used to change things in your business that will ultimately affect your lagging indicators. The difficulty with these signals is that if you receive them, you cannot ignore them. 

For this reason, most businesses choose to bury their heads in the sand and ignore the leading indicator because it usually means a bit of uncomfortable change or work. These signals are often not 100% accurate and open to interpretation, which makes them even more complex to successfully integrate into your business. 

I have recently spent a lot of my time booking speaking events at corporates and conferences around the world. Building a speaking business is much like any other business, it requires sales. There are many different ways to become known as a speaker and to book gigs but one of the most obvious is cold outreach. I don’t mind cold outreach and find that it works fairly well for this kind of business but near the end of the year, I become complacent and bored with the emails I send out and forget to update the content or target specific niches so my emails stop working. I forget about this part of the business until the revenue stops coming in – a lagging indicator. 

When the revenue stops coming in I often become frustrated and wonder why the hell things aren’t moving along as I want them to. You guessed it, I’m waiting for the lagging indicators to tell me something, while if I switched my attention to the leading indicators (the number of sales emails I sent or cold calls I’ve booked) I would very quickly realise that the revenue is going to slow down as a result of me sending fewer emails and booking fewer calls. The antidote? Get back on the bus and send out those cold emails and book those pitch calls\!

### How to use leading indicators

Once you accept that leading indicators are signals you can begin to look out for and interpret the signals to improve your business overall. These indicators are the critical signals that you receive to help you decide which direction to take your business. 

These signals can either confirm your current direction and predict that you’re on the right path to profitability and growth. Or they can tell you that what you are currently doing is going to fall short of your expected targets. 

The results of measuring a leading indicator can and should be visible almost immediately. Daily active users, average revenue per user or number of sales calls made can all be measured and interpreted instantly. If you know that in the past you have required a minimum of 1 000 unique visitors to your website in a week to gain five new customers, you should be able to look at the unique visitors right now and know if you are hitting those numbers or not. 

If you are not set up to track leading indicators, consider some of the examples below and how they might apply to your business. 

### Examples of leading indicators

Here are a few examples of leading indicators per department in your business: 

**Marketing**

* Number of webinar attendees  
* Cost per acquisition  
* Daily active users

**Customer Success**

* Number of calls into customer service  
* User guide downloads  
* Open tickets older than 1 day  
* Backlog of open tickets per customer success agent

**Sales**

* Sales calls per day  
* Sales emails per day

**Finance**

* Average revenue per user

**HR**

* Budget for training  
* Goal alignment

## What is a lagging indicator?

Lagging indicators measure current performance. If you launch a product, lagging indicators will tell you how many people used that product, what they did with it and how much money you generated from the launch. 

The lagging indicators are always triggered by an event that has occurred (hence lagging). 

It’s generally a good idea to use lagging indicators in conjunction with leading indicators to understand trends and figure out if the intended outcomes were met. 

Some examples of lagging indicators: 

* How many downloads of your app  
* How much revenue you generated from a project  
* Software bugs reported  
* Product returns last month

If your business is only using lagging indicators to determine success and find ways to improve and move forward, you are only using half of the tools at your disposal. 

### Lagging indicators lead to these questions

A lagging indicator should encourage you to ask things like: 

* How many people attended an event?  
* How much product was produced?  
* What response did it receive?

### Why do businesses lean heavily on lagging indicators?

In short, we obsess over lagging indicators because they are simple to track. When you ask your team for a dashboard of numbers and figures the easiest ones for them to pull up are the lagging ones. 

This is where you need to step in and dig a little deeper. Remember, lagging indicators look backwards, not forwards. It’s like walking down a street looking behind you as you go; impossible to know where you’re going but easy to see what you’ve avoided along the way.

### How to use lagging indicators

Lagging indicators are usually (almost always) triggered by an event that has occurred already. You spend money on a marketing campaign (event) so you track how many new customers signed up (lagging indicator). You buy a new machine to improve output in your factory (event) and the output improves the following month (lagging indicator). 

Lagging indicators are best used with leading indicators to figure out trends and whether outcomes have been met. In finance, for example, lagging indicators can be used to tell you which areas of your business are performing and which potentially need some attention. 

Ask yourself and your business the following questions to help with lagging indicators: 

* What is my goal and how do I know (measure) if I have achieved it?  
* What are the indicators of my company’s success?

### Examples of lagging indicators

Here are a few examples of leading indicators: 

**Marketing**

* Brand recognition – how many people have become aware of your brand  
* ROI (Return on Investment) – how much money you made vs spent on marketing  
* Customer acquisition costs – how much money it costs you to acquire a new customer

**Customer success**

* Customer renewal rate – % of customers who renew your service  
* Churn rate – % of customers who cancel your service

**Finance**

* Net revenue – Gross profit minus cost of running your business  
* EBITDA – Earnings before interest tax and depreciation  
* ARR – Annual recurring revenue

### Different indicators for different departments

Not all indicators (leading or lagging) apply to every department. 

The difficult thing about tracking the right metrics is doing the setup work properly. It takes a lot of dedication and focus to ensure each part of your business is tracking the right things at the right time for the right goals. 

Finance won't track the same indicators as HR. Marketing won't track the same indicators as Sales, and on and on. It’s up to you to pinpoint your most critical departments and update their indicators to align with your core business metrics. 

### Performance framework

One of the biggest mistakes that small businesses make is not having a performance framework. 

* What are you measuring?   
* Why are you measuring it?   
* How are you measuring it?

Lastly, the most important part of having a performance framework is sharing it with your team so that they know what they should be working on and striving towards. 

If you don’t have a broad performance framework for your business, begin by identifying the most important metrics to your business and then figuring out how each part of your business influences these key metrics.

# DISCOUNTS DONE RIGHT

At the end of every year, businesses across the world prepare for the insane season of shopping and discounting that takes place between November and the end of December. The pressure to join the fray and deeply discount your products or services is immense and I know that you’re probably planning your Black Friday, Cyber Monday, pre-Christmas, end of year and new year sales and discounts. 

It’s an intense time to be in business whether you want to participate in the craziness or not. When I owned physical retail stores around the country the pressure came from the bigger retailers, who used their deep pockets to begin discounting early in the season to bring the shoppers into their stores, regardless of the impact on margins. Often this compromises profitability, but it’s a game you play with your customers to get them in the door and hope they buy things that aren’t on sale or deeply discounted. 

Some businesses will offer in-store discounts but none online. Others will do the opposite and drive shoppers to their online stores and upsell them at checkout. There are differing opinions everywhere you turn and the truth can very rarely be found by looking at someone else’s business. You have to decide what your customers want and what is going to work for your business. You have to figure out what you can afford, what you can upsell, cross sell or any other sell to eke more money out of your customers. 

Truth be told, even typing that previous sentence makes me feel a bit icky. I don’t like the idea of trying to squeeze a bit more money out of my customers. I think there are ways to add value, provide savings and help people out in tough times but you have to plan and you have to give it thought and commit to putting your customer at the centre of your offering. 

### Know your customer

Understanding your customer is the first real step to offering them a discount, promotion or any kind of value. You probably think this is clicheè but it’s worth reflecting on now. Do you really know what your customer wants? If you don’t, perhaps consider asking them in an email. Give them options and tell them you could either give them a discount or you could send them a free gift, surprise or bonus free shipping. The answer may surprise you but assuming you know what they want without any data is probably a bad idea. 

Some questions to consider:   
Is your customer the kind of person who likes to brag about the deal they got from you?  
Does your customer usually qualify for free shipping or can you gift this to them?  
Would they be surprised and delighted with a free gift without knowing what it is?  
Would they prefer a Buy One Get One (BOGO) offer?  
Can you segment your customers and give them exactly what they want rather than broad, vague ideas of what they might like?  
Figure their truest desires and your end-of-year promotions will fly. 

### Types of discounts

In case you think there is only one way to discount, here are a few ideas to get those marketing juices flowing: 

**Free gift**  
Either allow your customer to choose a free gift or give them a secret gift with their order. Either way it’s a gift and it’s free\!

**Bundle offers**  
Instead of offering a simple rand value or percentage discount you can offer a customer a bundle of products that shows greater value than purchasing the products individually but is still profitable for you as the merchant. 

**Percentage discount**  
Straightforward and familiar to everyone, chop off a % from your regular price. The only potential downfall is that customers get used to the discount and believe that you can afford to offer this price forever. 

**Rand value discount**  
If you would rather keep it super-simple, deduct a certain value from the price or total basket that the customer is buying. If they have a basket of over R1 500 you take off R300. If the basket is more than R3 500, take off R500, and so on. 

**Buy One Get One (BOGO)**  
If they buy one item from you, they get another one for free. Simple as that. 

**Free shipping**   
To many business owners free shipping doesn’t seem like a big win but some customers absolutely love to check out knowing they’re saving your standard shipping cost. Give it a try and you might find that your customers are thrilled. 

### Discounting is not marketing 

The easiest way to degrade your brand is to offer a lazy discount and think that it replaces a marketing strategy. Discounting is not a replacement for a clear, clever and effective strategy. 

I can’t remember the last time I actually signed up to someone’s newsletter because they offered me a 10% discount. Can you? Rather provide value and entice people to sign up to your newsletter or visit your store. Engage your customer’s needs beyond just the financial. The brands we remember are often not the ones that save us the most money but the ones that understand us the best and give us the most value. Offering up the biggest discount you can afford can come across as lazy and thoughtless. You still have time to put together an interesting year end marketing plan. Get to work. 

I recently decided to spruce up my home office since I’ve committed to the work-from-home thing. Part of this redo included buying plants for my Zoom background when I give keynotes and conference talks. I decided to use a service online called PatchPlants.com. I really didn’t expect much other than my plants to be delivered to my door. I sorely underestimated this fantastic service. 

Sure, they delivered my plants on time and with a smile but what came after I received the little beauties absolutely blew me away. You kinda think that an indoor plant service can’t be too creative with what they offer and how they engage but that’s where I went wrong too. Patch sent me an email about 15 minutes after I received the delivery. It was packed full of tips for each of the plants I ordered: how to water them, how to know if they’re doing OK, how to gauge their happiness, how to know when they need water, how much sun to give them and on and on. Each plant has a little story to go along with it to help you remember how to treat it and I have to tell you, those emails helped me build a real connection with my plants and want to keep them alive for as long as I can\! Absolute genius in a simple newsletter. 

### Promote your offer

One of the mistakes that I used to make in my retail business was to launch a promotion or discount and expect people to know that it existed. I’d send out one email blast to my database and tell them about it and then hope to all things holy that they’d remember, visit the store in person or online and buy using the promo code. 

If you do decide to discount your products or make your customers an offer, tell them about it. Tell them you’re going to launch it, build up to it on your social media, send them emails, then launch it and remind them. You think they don’t want to hear from you but if you’ve spent time building a relationship with your customers, not only do they want to hear from you but they want to support you too. So don’t forget to tell them about the promotion you’ve launched. 

### Prepare your business

Discounts are a way to engage customers in purchase hype. They get excited visiting your store (online or in person) because they know they are there to buy. At this point you have to be prepared to offer them other products of value, products you have just launched or things you think that they will need or want to buy alongside the product that is on offer. 

If you are simply sending your entire database of customers to a single product that’s on sale, you are missing a trick. 

### Loss leaders

A loss leader is a product or service that is offered up at a loss to the business in order to entice people to visit and buy from your store. The loss leader strategy is usually deployed to attract new customers or to get existing customers to buy additional products that they may not have purchased before. 

It’s easy to look at sales on a short-term basis but the key strategy for the big end-of-year hype should be to acquire new customers in bulk so that you can bring them into your community for the long term. Lifetime value is a key metric in ecommerce, and it should be. One sale means nothing to you in the long term, it’s the value that a customer brings you over their lifetime with your brand that matters. Nike wants to be the shoe you wear forever, not just this season. 

### Worst case success

What does success look like for your discount campaign? Do you want to sell out of a product? What happens if the discount gains steam and you end up with no stock over the festive season? In my opinion, the worst outcome is not that nobody buys your products. It's that your promotion performs so well that you end up with hundreds of customers annoyed that they can’t access your promotion, that their order is delayed because you can’t keep up with fulfilment and they leave, never to return. Larger retailers can handle this because they have sufficient brand equity and stock variety to retain customers regardless. Your business may not survive selling out of stock before the holiday season because you did so well over Black Friday. 

This can be considered a great problem to have but is it one that your business can survive?

### Some ideas

**Promo Partnerships**  
A great way to entice new customers with interesting discounts is to partner with another brand or business and only offer their database the discount. This helps you gain exposure to a new audience and gives your partner a good reason to engage with their database with an offer unique to them. 

**Webinar Discounts**  
Since Covid-19 smothered the world, one unexpected occurrence has been the adoption of webinars by just about everyone. You could host a webinar announcing a new product range, insights, tips and tricks specific to your industry and customer base or any other interesting 30 minutes of content and then offer up a discount or promotion only to people who attend. 

**Loyalty Discounts**  
Right now you can start to prime your customers by segmenting them by number of purchases and letting them know that if they hit 5, 10 or 15 purchases with you before a certain date they will qualify for different discounts. Rewarding your most loyal customers is a great way to build brand equity.

# PRODUCTIVITY TIPS

## Needle movers

Understanding the metrics that matter to your business has never been more important than in a time of crisis. Start by making a note of which metrics you need to move (the needle movers) to make sure that your business stays alive and can start to thrive. How many followers you have isn’t going to pay the bills and the number of likes your latest Instagram photo received won't solve your immediate sales issues. But the number of sales per month, returning customers or increased revenue may keep you in business. 

Once you have the list of needle movers, focus on them exclusively. Everything else can wait. What do you need to do each week to make those needles move?

## Pick your priorities

It’s not possible to have ten priorities. If everything is a priority then logic dictates that nothing is a priority. I promise you don’t have a time problem in your life, you have a priority problem. 

It’s imperative to list the things that need to be done and then rank them top to bottom. If you don’t have a clear sense of what is the most pressing project to work on then this is where you should start. Use the Eisenhower Matrix that I cover below (or a similar system of your own) to ensure you focus on the right things each day and week. 

## Forget the fluff

Making your website prettier, rewriting your Twitter bio, updating your five-year business plan and similar tasks are all fluff if they aren’t moving the needle for your business. As an SME in a difficult economic climate, you should be focused on increasing sales, lowering expenses and keeping your business alive. That’s it. Everything you do each week should directly impact these priorities. If your tasks don’t contribute to these three things, it’s likely that you are working on fluff. Stop and forget about the fluff. Focus on things that move the needle in a real way. 

## Stop multitasking

Multitasking is the death of productivity. There is a Stanford study that shows that multitasking impairs your cognitive control, so do yourself a favour and focus on one task at a time. If this means muting WhatsApp for 45 minutes, do it.

Multitasking is also linked to digital fatigue which leads to burnout. One of the key definitions of burnout is when you lose your sense of accomplishment. Multitasking does this to a person on a daily basis. If you have a task list for the day of five items and you work on each item throughout the day without much intention or focus, you’ll get to the end of the day having only completed about 20% of each of the five tasks.

You end your day having achieved nothing tangible, nothing to completion and nothing to celebrate. Go through enough days like this and you start to feel like you’re never accomplishing anything. If this continues on for months you’ll start to want to jump off a digital cliff and cut off the supply of blood to your typing fingers. There is no joy in multitasking and there is no pride in claiming that you’re good at it. Stop, focus, complete a task and move on. 

## The Eisenhower matrix

If you, like most people, struggle to be productive and sort your tasks out then you should take a look at the Eisenhower Matrix. This simple system forces you to prioritise tasks by urgency and importance. You must place the tasks you have to do in one of four quadrants: Do first, Schedule, Delegate, Don’t Do.  

|  1\. DO FIRST First focus on important tasks to be done the same day.  |  2\. SCHEDULE  Important, but not-so-urgent stuff should be scheduled.  |
| :---- | :---- |
|  **3\. DELEGATE**  Delegate to others what’s urgent but less important. |  **4\. DON’T DO**  What’s neither urgent nor important, don’t do at all.  |

## Exercise

This sounds like the least productive thing to do with your time but it is well understood that exercise improves memory, sharpens focus and increases your energy levels. If you want to be more productive take a 30-minute break and go for a walk, do some pushups or commit to a workout session. 

## Notifications

Turn them off. There, I fixed your phone for you. 

Possibly the worst addition to the mobile phone was notifications. Pile on social media apps, email, the actual phone feature ringing occasionally (who calls people anymore anyways?) and you have a distraction machine optimised for your demise in your pocket all day, every day. It’s brutal, it’s intense but there is a solution and it involves silence. 

Of course, there are situations that require notifications but overall, if you want to be more productive you have to give yourself a chance and that involves one task at a time. Serial work, not parallel work.

To illustrate the power of notifications, as you read this sentence I want you to think about your phone, think about it sitting in your pocket and think about someone trying to contact you. What happens when someone tries to contact you? Your phone vibrates, right? Is your heart rate climbing a little right now in expectation of that vibration? Did you feel the need to take your phone out of your pocket? In the most extreme reaction, some of you might have even felt the phone vibrate right now (if that actually happened, HOLY HELL CAN YOU BELIEVE IT?) but the truth is, what you’re experiencing and have definitely felt in the past is called phantom vibrations. These fake vibrations are triggers telling us that we’re not focused, that we’re addicted to our phones and that we multitask so much we’ve forgotten how to focus on one thing for longer than 10 minutes. 

Let it go. Your job is not to answer “urgent” Slack, WhatsApp, email, Discord, teams or whatever message comes through. Sure, some of you might need to answer those beeps but that’s likely the exception, not the rule. With my teams in the past, I’ve set levels of urgency and methods of communication for urgent matters. For example, a WhatsApp message at 11pm must mean that our website is down, we’re losing money and our customers are publicly shaming us for it. If the issue isn’t that urgent, you better not be sending me late-night messages\!

# HABITS TO STACK

Every day we all wake up and follow a routine. In my case I wake between 5.30 and 6am, exercise for 30-45 minutes, play with the dogs, shower, brush my teeth, make a cup of coffee and then sit down to write for an hour. That takes me to about 9am when everyone else wakes up and starts their day and the barrage of email hits my inbox. 

I avoid email for as long as possible and usually only answer my first email around lunch time. These are habits that help me focus on what’s important. 

Habits are the best and the worst things in our lives and if we are aware of this, we can change them and manipulate them to make our days as productive and positive as possible. 

I was once a 40-a-day smoker. That was not a good habit. I would romanticise smoking and justify the habit as something that founders do. Something that writers do. Something that young people should do once in their lives. Until I was 26 and couldn’t walk up a flight of stairs. 

It’s important to really think about the things you do every day that make your day good or bad. Do you go to bed too late but for no good reason? Do you sleep in and skip exercising and can’t really figure out why? These are all habits that you can control. You can review them and change them. 

Here are some habits to consider stacking on top of your existing habits. You could consider removing some of your own bad habits and substitute any of these for the ones that need discarding. Habit stacking has the potential to turn your morning into a powerhouse of productivity. 

## Regular team check-ins

Everyone is under strain and everyone wants to be connected to the rest of their team. In a world where deadlines are always impending, the pace is always blistering and the sales are always closing you can often forget to stop and check in with your team. This is a simple but extremely effective tool to gain insight into your organisation, its culture and the people who you trust your business with. I like to do bi-monthly one-on-ones with my team to make sure that everyone is OK and on the same page. Schedule these and don’t skip them. 

## Sleep

The best medicine you can take to make yourself more healthy is a full and uninterrupted eight hours of sleep. Study after study has proven that sleeping well improves almost every aspect of your life. I’ve mentioned this before and I’m bringing it up again because I think it’s that important. Get a full night’s sleep and you’ll notice the quality of your work improve, your relationships improve and just about everything else improve too. 

I have a simple basic guideline for my sleep and productivity: 

* 8 hours of sleep \= 8 hours of good work  
* 6 hours of sleep \= 4 hours of good work  
* 4 hours of sleep \= 0-2 hours of good work (if you’re lucky)

I first realised how shit I was at my job without sleep when I was about 25 and furiously building a new tech startup. I had just moved to Cape Town from Johannesburg to raise venture capital with my co-founder. We had some money in the bank, my partner, family and friends were left in Johannesburg and I was on my own and free to burn the midnight (3am) oil. 

So my co-founder and I got to work and night after night, week after week we worked tirelessly, literally, to get the code written, the website designed, the marketing up and ready, the ads going and on and on down our list of to do’s. 

Then I realised that the work I had stayed up to 3am to complete was really, really average. I was redoing most of it the next day after I’d got some sleep. I was moving two steps forward every night but about 10 steps back if you count the exhaustion I was creating. 

This is a vastly different experience to my years at university when I could miss lectures, sleep in all day, drink and party all week and then pull an all-nighter, or more like a two-dayer to get my essay out and pass the semester with no problem. Age, it’s a bastard. 

Do yourself a favour and figure out what amount of sleep works for you. Trust me, the answer is not 4 hours a night, it’s closer to 8\. 

## Delegate

Trying to do everything yourself is going to make your life and business more difficult. If you are unable to delegate, you will become the bottleneck, your quality of work will suffer and you just won't find time to do anything. 

Delegating tasks that someone else can do will free up your time to do things that really count and that only you can do. Get into the habit of delegating or you will suffer. 

There is a very real problem that most leaders experience when trying to delegate: leaders often don’t let go. You have to let go. You have to understand that by giving this particular task away you are empowering the person you are delegating to with responsibility. They want to impress you. If they don’t, you’ve hired badly and you need to reassess your team. 

It’s important to be clear with the person you are delegating to. What does success look like for this project? What’s their budget? What’s their deadline? Give them explicit guidelines to help them achieve the goal and then let them work. If you can’t trust them to do the work, don’t delegate it in the first place. 

## Eat the frog

Mark Twain once referred to the concept of eating the frog and then Brian Tracy wrote the wildly popular *Eat That Frog\!: 21 Great Ways to Stop Procrastinating and Get More Done in Less Time*. It simply means to tackle your biggest and most important task first. If you avoid the most important task of your day, it’ll still be there tomorrow. 

Eat that frog and get it done, then the smaller tasks feel easy in comparison. This is directly correlated to your social media usage, ability to distract yourself and your inability to effectively multitask or prioritise. Remember, you don’t have a time problem, you have a priority problem and your frog should be your first priority. 

## Meditation

I know, everyone tells you to meditate but honestly, it’s really just not for you. Me neither. But then I found a guy who has a company that truly helped me see the value in meditation. Before I came across Sam Harris and his app called Waking Up I thought that meditation was for hippies, yogis and tech bros who thought they were enlightened and had just invented this new thing called meditation. It all felt icky to me. 

The other thing that made meditation overwhelming to me was the apparent time commitment. People said you should sit alone and meditate for anything between 30 minutes and 2 hours. What? That’s an entire morning\! 

Sam Harris went the other way and his app has an introductory course of 10-minute daily meditations. He narrates each one and I managed to do 90 days in a row to get myself into the groove. 

I can’t explain in words effectively enough how much I value the now 20 minutes of meditation that I do every day (as often as I can). The ability to start my day with a calm mind is invaluable and I am noticeably more distracted when I don’t meditate. Do me favour and download Sam’s app and give it a try.

# RUN BETTER MEETINGS

Meetings are where ideas go to die. 

I get cold chills when I think back on my last corporate job, which required me to attend lots of inane meetings. They were a way to look busy, without doing any work. We usually had 10 people or more in a meeting, in a giant room that could accommodate over 30 people. They never started on time, and receiving any kind of agenda was a fantasy.   

Meetings were so important in this company that all senior staff had to make their calendars publicly visible so that anyone could book you into a meeting if you had a free gap. Insanity\!

My boss and I developed a clever plan to book up each other’s available slots, and then we would camp somewhere quiet where we could carry on with our work. 

I don’t have to tell you that this was a crazy way to get work done, and entirely unsustainable. 

Meetings aren’t productive by default. It takes care, attention and significant effort to establish meeting norms that make sense for your business. 

## Shorter meetings

Did you know that you can edit your calendar so that the default meeting length is not 60 minutes? This tiny change made all the difference to the way I run meetings and how people engage with me when they arrive. 

I have set my calendar to a default length of 25 minutes. Two very specific things happen when you only offer 25 minutes to people: 

1\. Nobody wastes any time – small talk is minimised and people get right to the point.  
2\. Agendas kick in – even if it’s informally, people will start the meeting with what they are hoping to achieve or cover and then dive right in. 

## The meeting owner

Who is running the meeting? Who owns the outcomes? Who is driving the meeting forward and making sure people stay on topic, on time and in focus? If there isn’t a clear leader for the meeting you are in then I suggest you stick your hand up and be that person. 

The meeting owner isn’t always the most senior person in the meeting. This person is the conductor and the attendees are the orchestra. It’s important to very clearly and out loud state who this person is so that everyone understands and respects the role. 

This person must take charge and ensure that everyone has a chance to speak, everyone understands the goals for the meeting and at the end is able to allocate tasks. 

## Start on time

There are few things that I hate more than a meeting starting late. It’s disrespectful to everyone who is on time to wait for someone who is late. The meeting owner must take control, start the meeting and move on. If the late arrival is integral to the meeting, the meeting should be rescheduled depending on how late they’re going to be. This is difficult to enforce but you won't have to enforce it for more than a few meetings. Cancel a meeting once, piss everyone off and teach them a lesson. 

## Have an agenda

Calendar invitations are useful things if used correctly. If you don’t know because you’ve never looked, there is a little area in your meeting request that allows for notes. Use that little area for a meeting agenda. If you are inviting everyone to a meeting, take it upon yourself to write a short agenda and ask people to contribute *before the meeting* so that everyone can contribute effectively. 

Without an agenda there should be no meeting. Try it. 

## Pitch, don't invite

Every meeting should be pitched to the attendees. Meetings must justify their existence and if you’re the person trying to get everyone in one room, you need to pitch your reason for the meeting. The pitch should be short but punchy and entice your colleagues to attend rather than force them to attend. The pitch can be in a group chat, email or even in passing. If this is done correctly, sending an agenda, starting on time and being the meeting owner will all be simple. 

## Musk's meeting rules 

Elon Musk famously hates meetings. He created three simple rules to abide by when thinking about meetings: 

1\. No large meetings.  
2\. If you're not adding value, leave.  
3\. No frequent meetings.

Simple. 

Don’t make meetings big. If they're unavoidably big, keep them short.   
If you realise you aren’t adding value, leave the meeting or drop off the call.   
Don’t schedule frequent meetings. If there is an urgent issue to deal with, deal with it and don’t set a recurring meeting. The issue will be resolved but the meeting will remain. Not ideal. 

## Who does what

Every great meeting I have ever attended has ended with the same two questions:

* Who does what?  
* When should they have this done?

If there are no outcomes and no tasks assigned, the meeting was probably a waste of time.

# 09\. YOUR MIND & MENTAL HEALTH 

We’re getting to the point where we can all admit that mental health matters. Well kinda. We all know we should get started on improving our own mental health but it’s very likely that action is noticeably absent. I have become obsessively curious about my own mental health and fitness. I have started to test different things to see what positive or negative effects they have on my overall well-being and mental state. 

Now, more than just about ever in my life, mental health is my top priority and should be at the top of your list too. If there existed an Olympic competition for the destruction of mental health, fear and resentment would surely be the standout competitors for gold medals. Watch out for those two, now operating on pandemic steroids pushing them into the top spots.

I’ve also just about had it with people who fake it on social media and act as if they’re flying high when secretly they’re crashing. These lies contribute to the problem and they need to stop.

It’s OK to have a grumpy day or experience depression but imperative to admit it’s happening and seek help. I do. I see a psychologist to help me keep my mind strong and my focus sharp. I know I can’t do this alone – and that’s OK. 

We can’t ignore our mental fitness because if we do, our businesses will suffer along with us.

Disconnecting is hard and becoming increasingly harder as our offices resemble our kitchens, bedrooms and dining rooms.

It has never been more important to find a balance between working all the time, playing on social media and allowing ourselves time in the real world not staring at a screen. 

Digital fatigue is a real problem for millions of people all across the world and you and I are no different. It’s overwhelming and there is no end in sight with the Metaverse becoming an undeniable trend. 

# MOTIVATION TOOLS

How do you stay motivated and inspired when the world is going through a global trauma? Honestly, I don’t know if anyone has a definitive answer to this question yet. There is nothing that will magically move us from depressed to upbeat. There is no motivation pill, no magic potion, no silver bullet. Staying motivated requires a conscious effort and that has arguably grown more difficult with lockdowns, a constant supply of terrible news, fear of missing out, and social media fakes making you feel shit about your life.

I get it. It’s lonely and difficult being an entrepreneur and running a business. It’s OK to be scared but it’s important to understand that you can still be motivated and afraid. You can still find happiness in difficult times and you can still help your team find their own motivation whether you are working together in an office or all distributed remotely across the globe. 

### Take a step back

There are days when I wake up and know that it’s going to be a bad day for productivity. I feel uninspired, unmotivated and unmoved. I used to stare at my laptop screen and try to push through and find my motivation. This never works for me. I don’t believe that you can push through and find motivation just by willing it to be so. 

What I have started to do when I feel overwhelmed, or disconnected or just not present is to take a step back and check in with myself. This sounds strange but I ask myself if there is something bigger at play, if I can find it in me to do the work I need to do right now or if I need to take a break and try again later. 

Checking in with yourself is a useful habit to get into; the ancient Stoics like Seneca and Epictetus would journal every day to understand themselves and their own motivation better. 

A really simple way to check in with yourself is to ask yourself the big questions more frequently: 

* Why do you do the work you do?  
* Is the work you’re doing making the right impact on the world?  
* Are you happy more than you are unhappy at work?  
* Are the people you work with the best people to be working with?

### Journalling

Take 10 minutes out of your day to write about your day. After a week of journalling, you will start to understand your moods better and the things that affect them. 

What time did you wake up and how did you feel? What did you eat and drink the night before? Do you feel invigorated or sluggish? Write it all down and make a habit of understanding what is going on in your brain. Journalling is also a great habit to instil in your team. Help people understand the importance of keeping their minds sharp and their emotions accessible. 

Truth be told, I struggle with journalling. Perhaps it’s because I wrote so much during my day that I battle to turn my calming time into a writing habit too. With that said, the most success I’ve had with journalling is when I’ve included it in either my early-morning or late-afternoon routines. For example, if you wake up at 6am and then you exercise for 20 minutes, meditate for 20 minutes and walk the dogs for 20 minutes, try to throw in 10 minutes of journalling and keep it super-simple. Jot down what you achieved yesterday and what you missed then include what you want to do more of and do less of. That’s it, 10 minutes and done. 

## Gratitude journal

I want to add a gratitude journal to your note-taking habits. In a world filled with negativity, bad news, Trump and other crazies, a gratitude journal is a way to seek out things to be grateful for. At the end of your day take five minutes to write down a few things that you are grateful for. This can be one big thing or a few smaller things but make sure to focus on the things that you are grateful for. 

## Positive feedback

I have always struggled to verbalise positive feedback. I assume that people do good work because they’re good at their jobs and that’s what is expected of them. This is a mistake. 

People thrive on positive feedback when it’s appropriate. Don’t hold back. Make it a habit to congratulate people for small victories and applaud their achievements. This is what engages a team and lets them know that you are paying attention and you are grateful for the work they’re doing. 

In the past you could get away with ignoring the good and berating the bad but no longer. In a world where remote work is necessary and people are constantly on edge, being nice takes that edge off. I’m not suggesting that you make things up to be positive about but I am suggesting that you give credit where it’s due. One caveat here is not to blend in negative feedback in private with positive feedback in public. Make sure not to berate your team in front of one another when it’s equally as effective and significantly less traumatic for everyone if you deliver the criticism in private. A nifty trick I like to use is to say something positive before you bring in the negative with your team. 

### Rewards

People lose motivation when they feel that the reward for a given behaviour is insufficient. This can manifest in many ways over time and very quickly during stressful times. People may begin to arrive late for meetings or procrastinate, miss deadlines or stop communicating with their team. 

Nip this in the bud with rewards for work well done. Of course rewards have changed in a world of lockdown but you’d be surprised how far a gift voucher for an online store and a public congratulation will go to making your team feel cared about. 

### [![][image1]](https://medium.com/s/story/how-to-get-motivated-43d3d70a95d4)

The cycle of performance, motivation and reward is imperative to the survival of a business and the company culture that you likely want to continue to promote. Ignoring simple things can and will lead to unmotivated and difficult to manage colleagues. This does also raise a confusing position for leaders and managers in business: who rewards the ones who reward others? I believe that leaders should be the most highly criticised people in the business but that doesn’t mean the flipside is true. You should not be the most rewarded person in your business. You should, however, remember to celebrate your victories either with your team as a collective, basking in your unified success as part of the team, or in private with your close confidants, friends and equals. 

### Success and failure triggers

Every business is moving towards what they believe is success but I am astounded at how few businesses have clearly defined and communicated what success and failure look like. 

If you haven’t done so recently, it’s a good idea to communicate your idea of success and failure to your team in explicit terms. You never know what will happen once it’s clearly understood. Some people may realise they are overperforming and their motivation peaks as a result, while others may feel like they need to push harder to meet your expectations because they are flying close to one of our failure triggers. 

### Get to know your team

The one thing that I have loved about lockdown is how authentic people are. When you’re on a Zoom call with someone and their dog is barking in the background, their dishes are unwashed and they are cradling a baby, you really start to understand where that person is coming from. 

Take time to understand your team and their lives and then embrace this new knowledge. There is no longer a separation between work and life. Once you understand what this person’s life is like, give them a break. If they are trying desperately to make lunch for their kids, just let them do it without messaging them every three minutes asking where they are. 

Use your knowledge of their lives for good and help them be more flexible and productive; this will absolutely improve their motivation towards you and their work. 

### Separate work from life

Since the start of lockdown, my partner and I have had a very rigid work/life routine. Every morning we wake up and exercise, then we walk the dogs and once we’re ready for work we go into separate rooms until about 5pm. Then we’re “home from work”. We barely engage through the day because she’s at her work and I’m at mine. Keeping things as separate as possible (even in a small one-bedroom apartment) has been an incredible motivator for me. 

I would not have survived if my work and life had blended into one 12-hour day of work – which is easy to do under work-from-home conditions. There has to be a separation for the sake of sanity and it’s important that colleagues understand this too. Just because I live in the same place I work does not mean I am at work 24/7. 

### Professional help

I am an advocate for mental fitness. If you’ve read anything from me before, you’ll know that. If your company is in the fortunate position to be able to afford it, your team could benefit from having access to a psychologist should they require some guidance, a mental coach or just someone professional to talk to other than their family or colleagues. 

Alternatively, set up a conversation session for your team to talk about anything and everything but work is a good place to start to help them offload and engage with their colleagues in ways that are not stressful and work-related. 

For a long time I didn’t believe in seeking professional mental health. I believed that nobody could possibly know my mind better than myself. Eventually, after suffering through a burst stomach ulcer, kidney stones (twice), insomnia, migraines and many other physical issues brought on by anxiety and stress, I realised that I needed the help of a professional. More than six years later and I don’t know how I ever survived without a psychologist. I call my psychologist my mental coach. Professional athletes are able to receive coaching for diet, strength, cardio and other training, so why are professional business builders and leaders still scared of seeking mental coaching to perform better? It’s ridiculous and it’s time for this stupid stigma to end. 

You can’t read the label from the inside of your own bottle, so hire a professional to read your label for you and give you the perspective you need to improve. 

# **KNOW YOUR COGNITIVE BIASES**

Without realising it we tend to struggle and lash out because we’re under a lot of mental strain. We have stress, pressure and anxiety coming at us from every angle but there are also certain cognitive biases that we are prone to without even realising it. 

Many people scoff at the concept of cognitive bias, especially when you are grinding it out every day trying to keep your business afloat. But it’s important to consider what your mind might be doing without you realising it. Here’s the hard truth; everyone has cognitive biases, even if you don’t agree with me unfortunately that stubbornness stems from a lived experience that you aren’t acknowledging and probably need to review and move past. How you grew up, how you engaged with your siblings, parents, first business, partners, investors, your religion, race, geography, education, research, reading habits, etc all inform your mind and provide you with conscious or unconscious cognitive biases that shape how you make your decisions and live in this world. 

As Roman emperor and philosopher Marcus Aurelius wrote, “You have power over your mind, not outside events.” Your strength lies in your ability to understand the things happening in your mind and control them. Your panic, your stress, your need to please people, your inability to make split-second judgments, your choice paralysis and many more experiences you have every day are often guided by unconscious cognitive bias. 

The more I consider my own mind and the unknown cognitive bias I experience, the more I realise how important it is to understand myself and come to terms with the various biases that I am susceptible to. 

I am fascinated by the mind and especially the mind of an entrepreneur or small business owner. We put ourselves under a lot of strain and often don’t realise the effects our subconscious has on our actions. 

This is why it is important to know how our biases could be guiding our decisions and actions without us even realising it. The mind is probably the most important tool at our disposal when building a business so it is irresponsible not to sharpen the tool, refine our deployment of it and understand how to work with it better. 

It’s easy to ignore these insights because we just don’t believe that our minds are at risk but here’s the thing, you don’t know what you don’t know. 

### Confirmation bias

Confirmation bias is the tendency to search for, interpret, favour and recall information that confirms or supports one's prior beliefs or values. So let’s say that you are thinking about launching an e-commerce store for your business but your team believes this move will not add to the bottom line. Confirmation bias suggests that they will go out and find information and case studies to support their existing view. That’s confirmation bias. 

It’s a good idea to be aware of this cognitive bias so that you can intentionally seek out information that challenges your status quo. 

### Anchoring

If you are anchoring, you depend too heavily on an initial piece of information offered (the "anchor") to make subsequent judgments during decision-making. For example, the World Health Organization initially made the mistake of downplaying the importance of wearing a mask to prevent the spread of Covid-19. Many people are anchoring and hold on to this initial bit of misguided advice and refuse to wear masks. Little do they know, the more we all wear masks, the sooner our economy can open up fully. 

### Declinism

This is the feeling, or the predisposition, possibly due to cognitive bias, such as rosy retrospection, to view the past more favourably and future negatively.

Right now it’s hard not to experience declinism if you live in South Africa. It's depressing to watch businesses close, infections increasing, death rates rising, all the while worrying that the worst is yet to come.

It’s worth noting, and reading more on the subject, that declinism is wrong, more often than not.  
Oh, it’s also worth noting that the rest of the world beyond South Africa is struggling too. It’s not just your country, it’s not just your business, it’s not just you. Life, as they say, is hard. 

### Dunning-Kruger effect

One of my personal favourites, the Dunning-Kruger effect suggests that people with low ability at a task overestimate their ability.

We might have had employees with this trait, or we might be guilty of it ourselves – no one person can be good at everything\! Be wary of the individual who claims they can design a world-class website when all they’ve done is launch a point-and-click blog a decade ago. 

This one catches a lot of people out because you don’t know what you don’t know and then when you do know, you realise how little you know. An important part of forming the best team in your business is coming to terms with what you are all good at, what you suck at, what you’d like to do more of and what you’d like to do less of. These things matter. If you judge a fish by its ability to climb a tree, it will always feel like a failure. Don’t treat your team like fish climbing trees. 

### Sunk-cost fallacy

Let’s look at SAA and the situation it finds itself in right now. The business has been put into business rescue and is likely going to receive a R10-billion bailout. This is a perfect example of the sunk-cost fallacy at play. 

The sunk-cost fallacy is a human behavior pattern in which an individual or group facing increasingly negative outcomes from a decision, action or investment nevertheless continues the behaviour instead of altering course. The actor maintains behaviours that are irrational, but align with previous decisions and actions. Hello SAA. 

### Ostrich effect

You know at the end of the month when your accountant starts asking you for your receipts and invoices and begins to tally things up so you can get a picture of how your business’s finances are looking? That’s around the time the ostrich effect kicks in. 

This particular cognitive bias is the attempt made by investors to avoid negative financial information but the broader use of the term in finance circles has become "avoiding exposure to \[financial\] information that one fears may cause psychological discomfort". If that’s you, time to get your head out of the sand and take a hard look at those finances, good or bad. 

### Zero-sum thinking

Zero-sum thinking perceives situations as zero-sum games, where one person's gain would be another's loss. This kind of thinking is often associated with a person’s subjective view of the situation being observed. 

If you are concerned that your competitors may launch a similar product to the one you are about to launch, zero-sum thinking suggests that you believe that only one new product will survive. If they launch their product, yours won't survive. This is a dangerous way to see the world. It can be helpful in some scenarios but generally there are few zero-sum games in business. This fallacy is disproven across industries and globally; there is more than one car manufacturer. There is more than one TV producer. There is more than one mobile phone producer. There is more than one earphone brand, cereal, pasta, restaurant, shoe brand, T-shirt label, musician … and the list goes on. 

Zero-sum thinking is a lie we’re told and one that we foolishly buy into because we believe other people have to lose for us to win. That’s a lie. Stop perpetuating it. You just need your customers, people who love your brand, product or service. You don’t need to take your competitor’s customers to succeed. 

### The Lindy effect

The longer something has been in existence, the more likely it is to stay in existence. This is the Lindy effect and it’s a great place to start when thinking about your struggles. Remember that if you have survived in your business this long, you are likely to survive for a bit longer and on and on. 

Your goal in the current climate is to make it out alive and the Lindy Effect is a fantastic reminder that if you survive now then you’ll thrive in the coming years. 

### Pessimism bias

It’s natural to sometimes feel like bad things happen to you more than is considered normal but the pessimism bias is the tendency for some people, especially those suffering from depression, to overestimate the likelihood that negative things will happen to them. The truth is more likely somewhere in the middle. 

Negativity is also an addictive substance. I used to be addicted to anxiety (my psychologist helped me to uncover this). When you are anxious your brain releases adrenaline and various other feel-good things to help you recover and work better. My brain and body realised this and when there was nothing to cause anxiety in my life or my work I would make stuff up. I’d manufacture situations so that my brain would release the adrenaline and I could “do my best work”. That is one insane and unsustainable way to work, let me tell you. 

If you are down in the dumps and struggling with the realisation of a hard year behind you and a difficult end of year ahead of you, perhaps take five minutes to reflect on both the ups and downs of the year rather than the generally pessimistic view the world has had since the start of 2020\. For some people, the year we’re in right now has been the best year in their lives. It’s OK to be happy if the world is suffering. You don’t have to expect bad things. 

### Parkinson's Law of Triviality

Parkinson’s law of triviality is the tendency to give disproportionate weight to trivial issues. Also known as bikeshedding. This particular bias explains why your businesses often avoid the big and complex tasks ahead and rather focus on the trivial, simple and achievable tasks instead. 

You believe (and convince yourself) that posting on Twitter will have the same net effect as making your sales calls when in fact the hard things like cold calling are likely to help you more. Ignore the trivial, redefine your priorities and focus on the needle-moving tasks. 

### Semmelweis reflex

The Semmelweis reflex illustrates our tendency to reject new evidence that contradicts a paradigm. You want this year to be terrible. You are convinced that no matter what happens, 2020 was the year that almost killed you. Even if your business is doing OK, your relationships are stronger than ever and you are turning a corner with your mental and physical fitness, you tend to believe that the new evidence is going to break your current paradigm so you must reject it. 

I challenge you to seek new evidence and hold your existing paradigm with less desperation. If 2020 has taught us all anything, it’s that paradigms shift and our core beliefs need to be malleable. 

### Authority bias

Everyone has an authority in their lives. A mother or father figure who you potentially rely on more than you should. The Authority bias is the tendency to attribute greater accuracy to the opinion of an authority figure (unrelated to its content) and be more influenced by that opinion. So when the authority figure in your life or your business gives you an opinion, regardless of their knowledge or experience on that subject, you have a tendency to believe them more than you should. 

Right now it’s imperative to question the opinions of those around you and not take anyone’s opinion as your default view on a subject or decision. You must do your own research, formulate your own opinions and trust your experience as a business owner. You know your business better than most people out there. 

### Illusion of control

**What it is:** The tendency to overestimate one's degree of influence over other external events. 

One of the places that this bias is often at work is your relationships with people. You may have a falling out with someone at work and believe that resolving the situation is completely in your control.

**How to avoid it:** Understand what is truly within your control. You cannot control how someone else feels about you. You can only control your actions towards that person. You cannot control whether someone buys your product or not. You can only control your sales copy, checkout flow and advertising. The final decision is out of your control. 

### Availability heuristic

**What it is:** A mental shortcut that relies on immediate examples that come to a given person's mind when evaluating a specific topic, concept, method or decision.

In short, if something can be recalled then it must be important. When stated so simply it’s easy to see why this can be dangerous. In a world where the most insane news and clickbait headlines catch our attention you can guess what information is recalled first and with ease. 

**How to avoid it:** Learn to question the facts, data and knowledge that you rely on as a default. Just because something is easy to remember doesn’t make it correct. Just because you knew something 5 years ago and the facts are easily recalled, it doesn’t make it appropriate for right now in a particular situation. Question your facts and information. 

### Curse of knowledge

**What it is:** When better-informed people find it extremely difficult to think about problems from the perspective of lesser-informed people.

You know your business intimately and you understand why your product or service costs what it costs. However, your potential customer does not know your business as well as you do. This is why we do research and get to know your customer well. If you assume that everyone knows as much as you do then of course they will pay what you want them to pay. But how do you convert customers who don’t know your business and the finer details of your amazing product?

**How to avoid it:** Engage with your customers frequently and talk to people far removed from your industry. Be open to their feedback and use it as one part of your pricing and product research. 

### Attribution effect

**What it is:** The tendency to believe that what people do reflects who they are. 

Said another way, if you observe a team member being sarcastic at lunch you assume that they are a sarcastic person and you begin to dislike them accordingly. As a leader in your business it’s your job to put aside the attribution effect and see people for who they are not for a one time observation. 

**How to avoid it:** Give people the benefit of the doubt. Don’t be a pushover, of course but try to understand that one act doesn’t define a personality and shouldn’t shape your relationship with someone and how they perform. 

### Bandwagon effect

**What it is:** The tendency to do (or believe) things because many other people do (or believe) the same.

Just because other business owners do something a specific way doesn’t mean you have to do it that way. The bandwagon effect is extremely hard to break from when everyone in your network believes something and acts upon that belief. 

How to avoid it: Do your own research, think for yourself and don’t follow the crowd just because it’s easier to walk in their direction. The simple act of asking “Why?” could change the entire shape of your business and process. Why do you do XYZ? Why don’t we try something new? Why should we just follow them? Ask more questions. 

### The appeal to tradition

Ah, the good ol’ days. Remember them? When things were great, when times were simpler, when work was straightforward and things were affordable? No? Me neither. 

The appeal to tradition is an ancient fallacy that tries to undermine progress by incorrectly suggesting that a certain standpoint, opinion or method is correct because it has “always” been correct.

Your business is likely suffering from the Appeal to Tradition. Make sure that you are considering new methods, new opinions and different perspectives to ensure you are being as effective and efficient as possible. 

### The paralysis of analysis

“I just need more data before I can make an informed decision,” is something that I hear a lot in business. More data, more data, more data\! Here’s the hard truth: there is always going to be more data because in a digital world we never stop producing and consuming. 

The paralysis of analysis is a fallacy that says since *all* data is never in, any conclusion is always provisional, no legitimate decision can ever be made and any action should always be delayed until forced by circumstances.

This logical fallacy will definitely destroy your business and your role as a leader. Learn to make quick decisions and to react to the outcome. Any decision is often better than no decision. 

### Scapegoating

As the leader of your business, the buck stops with you whether you like it or not. Scapegoating is the fallacy that whenever something goes wrong there’s always someone other than oneself to blame. 

Don’t be that person. 

Don’t be the leader who never takes the blame. Don’t be the colleague who is never wrong. Don’t be the team player who celebrates the wins but never takes on board the losses. 

It’s OK to be wrong and it’s OK to fall on your sword if you made a critical error. Being wrong and admitting it is a sign of strength and a signal that you are a mature leader and willing team player. 

### The two-sides fallacy

We like to believe that there are two sides to every story but this is not the case for every story. Sometimes presenting two sides is a wasteful distraction. The two-sides fallacy is the presentation of an issue that makes it seem to have two sides of equal weight or significance, when in fact a consensus or much stronger argument supports just one side. 

For example, the Earth is a sphere. We know it’s a sphere. Scientists the world over (even aliens we haven’t yet met) agree that the Earth is a sphere … but there are always two sides to every argument, right? Sure, there is a minute fraction of humans who believe that the Earth is flat and could be perched on the finger of Zeus. 

These people are wrong. Presenting their argument as viable is a logical fallacy. 

Sometimes in business, you need to present a single argument for a new direction and it doesn’t need the other side of the story to be viable. Take charge and be the leader with a vision and a strategy. Two sides are not always better than one. 

### The Ben Franklin effect

Apparently, a person who has already performed a favour for another person is more likely to do another favour for that person than if they had received a favour from that person.

Think about your business. Are there colleagues, suppliers or clients who have received more than their fair share of favours from you? Have you extended deadlines, delayed invoicing or had to work late for someone else?

The Ben Franklin effect could be the reason you are so nice all the time. 

Carefully consider the power balance in your business relationships. Are you benefiting more than the other party or vice versa? Is someone being taken advantage of or are you happy with the way things shake out?

There may be no need for change but on the off chance that something is off-balance, you should be aware of your willingness to do things for clients, colleagues, friends, suppliers and everyone else. 

### The IKEA effect

The IKEA effect is the tendency for people to place a disproportionately high value on objects that they partially assembled themselves, such as furniture from IKEA, regardless of the quality of the result.

You have built your business with your own two hands, with your own blood, sweat and tears and you love what you’ve created. But is your appreciation for what you have created disproportionately high because you assembled it yourself? Do you have clarity on the value of what you have built? Or is your castle crumbling and you refuse to acknowledge the shoddy nature of the brickwork?

Get an outside perspective. Call on someone you trust implicitly and ask them to give you a firm but fair appraisal of your work, your business, your idea, your strategy or whatever it is you think you may appreciate too much.

### The online disinhibition effect

This refers to the way people behave on the Internet with less restraint than in real-world situations. This is an extremely powerful cognitive phenomenon that is represented by the loosening of social restrictions and inhibitions that would otherwise be present in normal face-to-face interaction.

As much as we all like to believe that we’re the same people in real life vs online, we’re not. We all tend to be more aggressive, more intense, more passionate or more polarising because that’s what the Internet pushes us to be. 

Where might this be problematic for you, your online persona or your business?

Review your social media engagements and try to objectively figure out if you are pushing past your own comfortable socially acceptable levels of anger, frustration and disinformation. Are you too intense about irrelevant things like whether the stupid dress is blue or gold? Do you overreact to customer complaints online while in real life you are calm and calculated? How do you respond to emails from your team or clients compared to your responses when you are sitting in front of them?

### The Pygmalion effect

The Pygmalion effect is the phenomenon in which the greater the expectation placed upon people (often children or students and employees), the better they perform. This can also become a form of self-fulfilling prophecy; a prediction that causes itself to become true. It works both ways, too – people with poor expectations internalise their negative label, while those with positive labels succeed accordingly.

How do you treat the people you work with? How do you treat yourself? When you perform well do you begin to emerge from a funk and perform consistently better? When your best-performing salesperson makes a mistake do you drop your expectations and send them into a downward spiral of failures?

As a leader, it’s important to remember that your words and actions have a meaningful effect on the people you work with. 

Be measured with your words and actions. I know it can be difficult from day to day to always be connected to the things you say and do in the moment, but it is your role to inspire and motivate your team to do and be better. 

If your leadership style is one that motivates through negative reinforcement, perhaps you should reconsider your ways. Sometimes negativity is required but more often than not you inspire people with greater expectations of them and their performance.

# PUT YOURSELF FIRST

It is not illegal to rest. I know, sounds crazy right? I know you feel like you’ll be arrested by the business police or entrepreneur-spies if you don’t work 24/7, 365 but it’s actually not against the law to take a break. Your business has made it through more complicated situations than you taking some leave and recuperating. I get it, your work is important, your business needs you and your team depend on you. All true, but they all need (and expect) the best version of you.

I know celebrity entrepreneurs are all over social media telling you to outwork, outplay, outlast, outthink your competitors and that the harder you work the more likely you will succeed and beat your competitors. 

They’re wrong. Working yourself into oblivion is stupid and you need rest. 

You need time to think. You need space from the daily grind and the survival mode that you have become too familiar with. You need distance from your work so that you can see it more clearly. You are absolutely not the best version of yourself if you’ve had three hours of sleep, are jacked up on caffeine, are wired on anxiety and pumped full of adrenaline that is fast depleting and haven’t exercised or eaten a healthy meal in months. If that sounds like you, it’s time to catch a wake-up. The model of burning the midnight oil is outdated and is driving you towards burnout and horrific mental health issues. Sounds scary? Good, it’s meant to. You are not meant to destroy your brain or body to build your business or career. It’s nonsensical when you say it out loud without the trickery and justifications we sell ourselves. Being unhealthy is unproductive for you and your business. In fact, I’d go so far as to say that burnout is a financial liability that should appear on your profit and loss statements. If you push your team too hard and you insist on long hours, never-ending deadlines, constant project updates and redo’s then you are invariably going to cost your business profit because your team is going to break, do bad work and end up resigning. 

Also, just because you work from home doesn’t mean you need to continue to work while you are at home and meant to be on holiday. Below are insights, tips, tricks and hacks to help you relax, unwind, lower stress, observe your anxiety and work towards rejuvenation before a new year starts. 

## Celebrate your year

Every year ends with a bang and it’s often hard to see what you have accomplished through the smoke and flames of the year-end scramble. 

Set aside a couple of hours and review your year. Go through your calendar, read through your notes and write down what you managed to do in 12 months. It’s OK if things didn’t work out as planned, write them down anyway. It’s too easy to get to the end of the year and feel like you didn’t achieve anything. This is known as recency bias – your mind favours recent events over historic ones – and can really screw with your idea of how you performed over an entire year. 

Celebrate your successes no matter how small and jot down some lessons learned from the few failures that might emerge. 

This task will give you much-needed perspective on time spent vs time wasted and it will definitely surprise you when you see how much you managed to do. 

## Escape negativity 

It’s hard to escape negativity when bad news literally follows us around on the Internet. Taking a break can force a hard stop in the negativity that you and your team might have faced this year. Disconnecting from the social media doom-scrolling, FOMO and screen time will release your mind from negativity and enable you to breathe and focus on the good things, the things you like and the things that feed your mind that are not work-related. Don’t let negativity persist through your holiday. 

## Reduce stress

A growing body of evidence shows that skipping breaks can lead to further stress and exhaustion. Do you feel it? As you read these lines can you feel the weight of constant work pressing upon your chest? I can. 

Don’t be a statistic. Give yourself permission to lay off the email, stop thinking about strategy and wind down for a few days at the very least. Stress is also directly related to physical ailments like lower back pain, migraines and stomach ulcers. Give yourself a break from stress and anxiety, disconnect and regroup. 

Further in this section, I’ll explain what the Stress Response Cycle is, why it’s affecting your life and how to close the cycle using a few simple tools. 

## Be anxiety-aware

It’s almost impossible to change what you aren’t aware of. Anxiety is an insidious bastard that sneaks up on you slowly and overwhelms even the purest and simplest joys in life. That twisted feeling in your stomach, that migraine you can’t quite shake, those sleepless nights, that stinging pain in your neck? All potential symptoms of your stress and anxiety. You know that twitch behind your eye that makes your eyelid wobble? That’s an obvious indication of excessive stress and a lack of sleep. Are you taking note of these symptoms?

It can be helpful to record your bouts of anxiety. Write down when you feel absolutely exhausted, when the migraine hits, when you have a sleepless night and then try to correlate these issues with how things are going at work. If a correlation emerges, it’s time to rethink how you live and work. If you aren’t making notes, you’re never going to notice the correlation. 

## Plan to relax

It sounds silly to lay out a plan for relaxation but intention counts. If you sit on the couch and watch series for 10 days solid, you won't emerge from the holiday rested and content. You’ll feel irritated and physically uncomfortable.

Set an intention for your break. Do you want to disconnect from social media? Do you want to read a book or two? Do you want to get back into regular exercising? Plan out your relaxation and aim to do something each day that you wouldn’t usually do while at work. This down-time accomplishment will give your break purpose as well as help you distance your mind from work. 

I don’t know about you, but I always feel more invigorated when I have experienced something that is different from my routine. A hike, lying on the beach, solving an incredibly difficult puzzle, reading a book that I’ve been trying to finish for months or anything else you want to accomplish. Experiences enable us to feel connected to things that are not work and that makes us feel more like a whole person. 

## Assess self-worth vs output 

As a founder and entrepreneur for more than 20 years I have been programmed to judge my self-worth by my output. When I close a deal I feel worthy. When I sell a company I feel worthy. But what happens when things are going badly? I feel like shit. You know what I’m talking about. You recognise this feeling, right?

I was so deeply immersed in this way of life that it took a few years of work with my psychologist to realise this was even a problem. It’s still something I battle with every day: am I good enough if I haven't sent 10 000 emails today? Am I good enough if I haven't made a bazillion dollars this month? Am I good enough if so-and-so posted that she’s killing it while I’m struggling?

We need to realise that we are more than the things that we achieve and the output of our days. 

One way to come to terms with this is to find a hobby that does not depend on you successfully completing or achieving anything as graded by others. Paint because you love painting, not because you are earning money as a painter. Knit something because you find it therapeutic. Read because you love the words, not because you must learn something to apply to your business. Do things for yourself, not to show others. 

## Do fewer things better

As a business owner or leader, it’s very difficult to focus and it can feel impossible to not multitask. Doing too many things almost always leads to doing nothing well. 

You are gifted at certain things and your business has specific priorities to move the needle. Take time to isolate what you are exceptional at and what your business really needs. The overlap is where you should spend your time. Everything else should be handed over to someone on your team or left until your core priorities are complete. 

Do fewer things better. 

## Remember email is not your job

What happens when you send an email?

You receive another email back shortly after hitting the “send” button. 

This is an endless loop. You send, they reply, you reply, they reply, and so it goes. 

For almost all of us, email is not your job. Email helps you do your job, sure, but it is not what leads to a better business. 

Of course, there are people who rely entirely on email to do their jobs but as the owner of a small business email is very unlikely your entire job. It is too easy to focus on email because inbox zero is satisfying. Email is the low-hanging anxiety fodder that tricks us into doing very little but feeling very good for a short period (until the replies come in). 

I like to check email before 9am in the morning and then after 4pm in the afternoon. Some days I’ll only check email in the morning so that I can dedicate the rest of my time to doing the work that really counts. 

Be honest with your own email habits and consider checking email less and working on things that matter more. 

## Use social media less

I struggle with this one a lot. As much as I know that social media contributes to bad moods and wasted time, their algorithms and dopamine programming beats me much of the time. It’s a constant battle but one that I am trying to win. 

Doomscrolling doesn’t make you feel better. Flipping through perfectly curated lives of people you love (or hate) will only make you feel like a failure, minimise your achievements and make you feel bad about your own life. This contributes directly to burnout and depression. 

Limit your social media time. Focus on spending time on apps that make you feel happy, not anxious and mute or block anyone who makes you feel bad about your own life. Rather see them in person, warts and all. 

## Don't be busy being busy

One of the major mental health triggers for me is being constantly busy. I hate running around like a headless chicken from one thing to the next and getting very little done. 

Being busy is often used to signal to others that you are good at your work, more productive than other people and always have something on the go. This often leads to panic that you have to fill every minute of every day with an activity. That’s not me. 

I like to set clear targets for my week. When they’re done, I slow down and give myself space to breathe. You don’t have to be actively busy to accomplish things. 

Productive is not the same as being busy. 

## Balance the work vs the output

A lot of my anxiety and stress used to stem from the need for people to acknowledge my output as exceptional. This creates innate procrastination which leads to delays in getting work out because it’s just not perfect yet. Here’s the truth: you are not a perfectionist and nor am I. 

Enjoy the practice. Enjoy doing the work and try not to obsess over the output before you’ve even done the work. If you enjoy the practice, you’re likely to practise more often – which will lead to better work in the end. 

## Refocus on learning

For a long time, I was obsessed with being right. I wanted everyone at work and in my personal life to argue, debate and fight with me about any and every point. It was damn exhausting for everyone around me and for my own mental health. 

Recently I have tried to focus on learning. Learning from others, learning from books, shows, podcasts, music and everywhere else that I can. Shifting from being right to being a knowledge sponge has helped me immensely. 

## Cut back on the sugar

This is an extremely personal point to make but sugar is mental madness for me. When I eat sugar I become extremely depressed. When I’m depressed I can’t exercise and when I can’t exercise I don’t sleep very well at all. When I can’t sleep I wake up irritable and angry and because I am a sugar addict (not said in jest, I am genuinely addicted to sugar) I reward my pain with sugar which then sparks the flywheel of despair that I have just described. 

I struggle with this one every day. Some days are better than others and when I have a bad day I acknowledge it, enjoy the doughnut and move on. 

## Stop taking things personally

It sounds silly to finish off with this particular suggestion but so much of my mental energy was once allocated to what other people were thinking of me. If I sent an email and didn’t receive a reply I thought it was a rejection of my entire existence. If someone didn’t reply to a text message I felt offended. It’s exhausting to think that everyone else revolved around you. They don’t. They have busy lives just like you do. 

The minute I gave everyone a pass and stopped taking things personally my entire perspective on life and relationships changed. Try it. 

# **FIGHTING DIGITAL FATIGUE**

Whether you care to admit it or not, you’re losing the war with your phone and the Internet. Oh, you didn’t know you were in a war? Well, let me bloody tell you that there is a war and the fact that most of us don’t even know we’re on the frontline is the problem. 

Big tech companies like Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, Slack, Discord, TikTok, Snap, YouTube et al, have all figured out how to get your attention and viciously keep it. See, there’s this thing called dopamine and it plays a pivotal role in how we feel pleasure. It's a big part of our unique human ability to think and plan. It helps us strive, focus and find things interesting. In short, dopamine is fantastic and your brain knows it. What the big tech companies have figured out is how to trigger your dopamine when you’re using their apps. 

You know that incredibly satisfying “pop” you feel and hear when you refresh your Instagram feed or Twitter feed or Facebook feed? That little popping noise and that feeling trigger your dopamine because it means you’re going to get a hit of new information that may lead to a new like or follow or double tap (heart) or share or some other newfandangled way for these businesses to keep your addicted eyeballs on their tiny little screens. 

This is one of the reasons you have your head buried in your phone for about four hours a day on a good day and up to eight hours a day for some people. It’s hard to fight in this war because we’re not equipped. I’d like to help you out and give you the weapons needed to win this war. Here’s a quote from Chamath Palihapitiya, the guy who basically invented the “Like” button at Facebook: “The short-term, dopamine-driven feedback loops that we have created are destroying how society works.” Even this guy, the billionaire who sent us down this dark road, acknowledges that what has been created is destroying us. 

Context is everything when fighting digital fatigue. Each person must recognise the signals that fatigue is setting in and work to combat them. A level of self-awareness is required in the hyper-connected world of remote work, social media, lockdowns and video meetings. Spend intentional time understanding your mind and body and take note of your personal triggers, feelings and energy levels. 

Digital fatigue is a real thing. I promise and it’s defined as a state of mental exhaustion and disengagement that occurs when people are required to use numerous digital tools and apps concurrently and in an ongoing way. The more tools you use each day, the more likely you are to feel fatigued. The more consistently you use these tools, the more likely you are to feel fatigued. 

Think about your average day in the world while I tell you about mine:

Meditate

Make coffee  
Work  
Have lunch  
Work

Sleep

If you were wondering what digital fatigue looks like, that’s it right there. 

Here are a few things to help you combat digital fatigue. As you read the following list I want you to pay careful attention to how you feel as you read each point. Do you get anxious? Do you start to feel irritable? Are you deeply addicted to your screen time and digital tools? You probably are, and so am I. 

## Activate airplane mode

I know it sounds crazy but you do not need to have your phone on loud and active past 9pm. But Nic, what if someone is trying to get hold of me? Well, they can send you a message or an email or a tweet or a Facebook message or an Instagram direct message or a messenger pigeon if they really want to, but you’ll get it when you’re ready, *not when they are.* 

Sure, there is the odd chance that someone might have died in the hours between 9pm and 6am when I wake up but I’ll be sleeping anyway and there’s very little I can do to bring them back to life at this point. So I’d rather receive bad news after a good night’s sleep, thank you. 

If your boss or work or colleagues feel they need to get hold of you at midnight to solve a problem they’re having, I would respond by suggesting that their bad planning shouldn’t become my bad night of sleep. You’ll be better equipped to solve the problem with eight hours of decent sleep and you won’t be irate that someone woke you up or kept you up. 

## Go silent

It’s been about five years since I took my phone off silent mode. All day, every day with no exceptions I keep my phone on mute. No vibration alert, no sound alert, no nothing. I am not a slave to the “ping” and I refuse to allow my phone to dictate when I pick it up. Answer this: is your phone in control of you or are you in control of your phone? If it plays a sound and you immediately pick it up, unlock it and give it your attention, I'd argue that you’re the slave. 

It’s the same with my dogs. I often wonder if I’ve trained them or if they’ve trained me and I still don’t really have a clear answer. At 5pm every day they sit at the foot of the staircase and tap their feet. I know that it’s 5pm so I get up to go and feed them. They believe that they’ve sparked my action and I believe that I have. Not sure who’s winning. 

I’ve even gone so far as to turn off read receipts and notifications on WhatsApp. Yep, you read that right. I don’t have any way of knowing when someone has sent me a message. There was a short period of about a week when I was stressed out about whether someone had replied to me or not and then the magic happened. I stopped giving a shit if anyone was messaging me. I stopped distracting myself with phantom vibrations in my pocket and I didn’t check WhatsApp at all through the day. Try it, it’s liberating to be in control of your own attention. 

## Digital friction

Just about every smartphone now has a feature to allow you to set time limits on certain apps. Do that. Or better yet, set firm rules for the worst culprits of your digital addiction and delete those apps from your phone. Make a rule that you’ll only browse Twitter on desktop and you won’t do that just to find an argument that you’ll inevitably lose because Twitter is the literal **worst** place in the world to win an argument. 

Digital friction is a simple and somewhat effective tool to lower your screen time but as I have learned, I’m an adult and I know how to turn off these restrictions if I really want to. Much like that sugar addiction that you’ve just learned about, I know that the slice of cheesecake in my fridge is bad for me, but fuck it, I’m an adult, I’ve had a rough day and I deserve that damn cheesecake. In fact, I’ve earned it. 

## A book on the bog

Stop browsing your phone on the shitter. 

Instead, make sure you have an old-school book right there at your feet so that you can read a page or two while you do your business. Think back to the days of magazine racks in toilets. For those of you too young to know what a magazine is, it’s like Buzzfeed, but on glossy paper with the top stories for the month printed and bound for your reading pleasure. WILD\!

By the end of the month, you’ll have read an entire book and significantly cut down on your screen time. 

## A phone-free bedroom

OK, right up front I’ll admit that I suck at this one. I sometimes have my phone in my room but it’s not the last thing I look at before I go to sleep. Ideally, you want to replace your phone with an actual old-school alarm clock so that you don’t go to sleep staring at a screen and switch off your alarm in the morning by unlocking your phone and seeing a hundred unread notifications. 

This is good for your mental health, your sex life and your sleep. The first two are obvious but let’s unpack the last one. I know a lot of people who will claim that when they can’t sleep they simply open up Instagram or TikTok and spend a while scrolling through their feed to make themselves feel sleepy. 

This is scientifically incorrect. 

Bright screens have a direct and negative effect on your melatonin levels. Melatonin is a hormone primarily released by the pineal gland at night and has long been associated with control of the sleep-wake cycle. In short, if your melatonin levels are out of whack, your sleep-wake cycles are out of whack. When you stare at a screen in the dark in bed, you are screwing with your melatonin levels, thus defeating your attempts to fall asleep by scrolling through TikTok memes. 

# BEATING BURNOUT

It’s better to burn out than to fade away. We all know this cliché but I think that in some instances we misuse and misunderstand it. 

In daily life, it’s better to slow down than burn out or fade away. There is an inherent danger in being an entrepreneur or business leader, which arises when we forget that actually we’re human. We need sleep. We need to eat healthy food and exercise regularly. Yet we forget that our lives consist of more than just work. We are complex beings living complex lives, and working ourselves to death (sometimes literally) is not an honourable life.

We forget to be human. 

Burnout culture sucks. I hate it. I hate telling people I am busy. I hate it when people answer “How are you?” with “OMG, I’m so busy, you have no idea.” I haaaate it. 

So let’s stop doing it. Let’s stop participating in the busy bragging and burnout bliss. If someone sends you an email at 2am in the morning, call them out. That is not OK. 

Let’s do things that keep our minds calm and our bodies healthy. Let’s stop striving for more at the risk of our health and mental stability. We need to find a way to work and be humans who enjoy being alive. 

We need to find ways to manage our stress and anxiety and then start doing those things urgently and often. Burnout is a topic that I connect with very personally. In my career as an entrepreneur spanning 20 years I have suffered from Insomnia, chronic stress, kidney stones twice, a burst stomach ulcer that put me in hospital, headaches that lasted for days, depression, severe anxiety that still plagues me today, rage and aggression. That is not a glamorous list and it’s not one that I am proud of. I was probably going to go bald in the long run but over a short period of about 18 months, I went from hair to no hair mostly because of the immense stress of building one of my startups. I paint a lovely picture, don’t I?

So let’s talk about burnout, where it comes from, why it’s so prevalent and how to prevent it in your life. 

Burnout is a condition related to overwhelming stress. Some stress is caused by digital fatigue, as I've mentioned, but there are a lot of things in our lives that stress us out. It’s pretty important to acknowledge that stress in and of itself is not bad. Stress can be very good for us, focus us, drive us forward and motivate us to be better versions of ourselves. The problem really starts when the stress never goes away and it compounds. Compounding, recurring, repeating and never-ending stress is the problem. Not stress itself. The stress only becomes bad when the stress itself outpaces our ability to deal with it. 

## The Stress Response Cycle

Let's unpack what causes the stress that leads to burnout. 

Stress causes reactions in your body and mind. We surely all know that feeling of stress intimately, we feel it in our stomach or chest and it’s a deep, dark feeling that slowly climbs through our organs and overwhelms our senses. 

Stress is caused by ***stressors***, the things that actually make you stressed out. Like a lion running towards you a thousand years ago while you were out hunting for food. This is a deeply ingrained response that we know as the Fight or Flight response. Here’s how it played out back then: you see the lion, your body starts to recognise the threat and pumps you full of adrenaline, endorphins and dopamine to get you the fuck out of there. You turn around and start sprinting back to the safety of your village. 

You make it over the plains and tell your family what happened. They all realise that you narrowly escaped death so you all jump with joy, celebrate, run around and dance with excitement. Said another way, you are telling your body and mind that you are safe. The ***stressor*** is gone. The stress can dissipate and it does. You go back to your normal state. 

That is a complete stress response cycle. You experienced stress. You removed yourself from the stressor and allowed the stress to leave you. 

We don’t do this in 2021\. 

In their brilliant book, *Burnout: the secret to unlocking the stress cycle*, authors (and sisters) Emily and Amelia Nagoski speak about stressors, the stress response cycle and how to break the habits we’re stuck in. In the book, they discuss why we get stuck in the stress response cycle. Here’s the high-level thinking: 

**Chronic stressors** are repeated, consistent stressors like a new project, a bad colleague or a bully. 

**Social appropriateness**, like at work when someone tells an inappropriate joke or treats people badly and you’re not in a position to say anything for fear of being fired.

**It's physically safer** not to deal with the stressor, like when you're out on the street or in a public place where you are not in control or a safe space. 

### Completing the stress response cycle

In order to deal with the stress that leads to burnout, it’s really important to complete the stress response cycle. To tell your body that the stressor is gone and you are now safe and can calm down. If you don’t do this successfully, you end up compounding the stress to the point of burnout. 

Think back to your commute to and from work (before we were forced to work from home): you'd leave the house and almost immediately hit traffic (stressor). Then you’d work your way through the idiots who don’t know how to drive (stressor) and eventually get to work where someone might be in your parking space (stressor). You’d find another parking space and get to work late where your boss shouts at you for being late (stressor). You have meetings with colleagues who don’t recognise your worth (stressor) and talk to clients who are constantly pressing you to do more work at a faster pace for less money (stressor). You eventually leave work and get back into traffic (stressor). 

By my very light recounting of your day you have encountered seven stressors before getting out of your car at the end of the day and greeting your family. 

You don’t close the stress response cycle, so that stress just compounds when you walk through the door and your dog jumps on you, your partner asks you to bath the kids and you still have two hours of work to go before you can “relax”. 

Stress upon stress upon stress upon stress. 

So how do you close the stress response cycle? Let’s unpack the methods below.

### Exercise

I’ve spoken about exercise already: research indicates that just 20 minutes of exercise can help your body process stress better. When you are done with your day do 10 push-ups, 10 jumping jacks or take your dog for a walk around the block. Even that tiny commitment will indicate to your body that the stressor of the day is gone and the stress can leave your body. 

### Laughter

I don’t mean social or posed laughter, the kind you’d post on Instagram because it makes you look affable. I mean the ugly, tears-running-down-your-face belly laughs – deep, impolite and helpless laughter that you share with someone close to you because you feel comfortable enough not to pose. The laughter you share with an old friend over a coffee or glass of wine. The laughter you share with your kids. Simple, unencumbered laughter can help you close the stress response cycle. 

From a genetic perspective, laughter isn’t just letting people know you found their comment funny. Neuroscientist Sophie Scott says that when we laugh we use an “ancient evolutionary system that mammals have evolved to make and maintain social bonds and regulate emotions.” In other words, it's how you release stress, tighten social bonds and regulate your emotions. 

### Affection

By this I'm referring not only to sexual affection, although that can tick off all four of the tips I’m presenting (exercise, laughter, breathing, affection) but to affection from someone you trust and love. Someone who wants to help you and listen without imposing themselves onto your situation. Someone who cares about you as much as you care about them. A long hug will do the trick and the release will be almost instant. You’ll feel the stress leave your body, your shoulders will drop, your brow will uncrease and things will seem OK again. 

Some experts attribute the stress-reducing, health-related benefits of hugging to the release of oxytocin, often called "the bonding hormone" because it promotes attachment in relationships. The hugging and oxytocin release that comes with it can then have trickle-down effects throughout the body, causing a decrease in heart rate and a drop in the stress hormones cortisol and norepinephrine. Covid put this to the test for most of us. Physical affection is such a standard part of our day that we only realise how much we need it when it’s gone. Covid took it away from us and millions of people around the world were worse of for it. 

### Breathing

Breathing is a wondrous (and critical) tool for our existence. 

Deep, slow breaths down-regulate the stress response in humans, especially when the exhalation is long and slow and goes all the way to the end of the breath.

A simple, practical exercise is to breathe in for a slow count of five, hold that breath for five, then exhale for a slow count of 10, and pause for another count of five. Do that three times – just one minute and fifteen seconds of breathing – and see how you feel.

# 10\. TERRIBLE ADVICE

During my more than 20 years as an entrepreneur, I have received advice from everyone and their grandmother: some of it good, much of it harmless and a chunk of it terrible. 

I love receiving terrible advice because I learn more about myself and the person sharing it than if I receive good advice. The problem starts when the person disseminating advice appears to be an expert and the person receiving it is inexperienced, desperate and impressionable. 

As an entrepreneur, you need to remember that the person giving you advice is not on the hook for their words. If you take their advice and it fails, they lose nothing. If you take their advice and it succeeds, they get the credit. It’s a win-win for them but not for you. 

Asking for advice is like asking a lottery winner which numbers they picked. Sure, those numbers worked for that particular lottery winner but they sure as shit aren’t going to work for you. So be careful who you ask for advice and be sure to understand their context as well as your own before implementing their suggestions. 

One of my businesses many years ago was funded by a list of strong characters who all received a seat on my board. Their opinions almost sunk my business. Their advice was often given without regard for my context or the context of the other board members. One person would say I should raise more money; another would tell me to fire more staff and consolidate. The next week they’d tell me to double down on ecommerce or find a way to work in the real world, offline and in person. It was a confusing mess and one that I almost didn’t survive. 

Eventually, I ended up dissolving the board and luckily a couple of years later I was able to sell the business and move on. I’m still close to some of the board members but others fell away and we drifted apart. 

Remember that you know your business and your customer better than most people ever will. It’s good to seek advice, it’s good to take other opinions on board but ultimately the decisions are yours alone. You, not your advisors, will be forced to live with the decisions. Make sure you believe wholeheartedly in the direction you're taking the business, because it's you who have to deal with the fallout. 

This section of the book is an unusual but important one. Everyone is quick to dole out their best bits of advice so I’ve put together some unconventional views on traditionally “good” advice. 

# **Terrible business advice**

## Grow your team

There was a time in business when “headcount” (the number of human beings your business employed) was a badge of honour. Those days are gone. 

Hiring people because growth is cool is a terrible strategy for building a sustainable business. Of course, there are times when human beings can and do solve growth issues in your business, but the default should not be to hire more people just because. 

First, try to figure out if you can solve the problems you have with improved processes or better operational efficiency. If this doesn’t work, consider using part-time staff or freelancers who can plug holes when needed. The last move should be to hire a full-time team member and when you do, make sure that they are able to do the work needed or adapt easily to a shifting environment and grow the business's bottom line. 

## Raise more money and grow

Growth for the sake of growth is stupid. There, I said it. 

Now that we’ve cleared that up, don’t raise money. If you think that raising funding is going to solve your problems, you’re wrong. Before you raise funding you should be looking at your sales, your operational structures, your team, how you generate leads, your content strategy and any other way you can think of to improve the profitability of your business. 

For the sake of simplicity you should look at funding in two clear ways: 

1. Funding to test a thesis – you have an idea and want to see if it can become a business.  
2. Funding for growth – you have a product that has traction, customers and sales, and you want to grow it rapidly.

Raising money to survive won't fix your broken business model. 

## The customer is always right

The customer is unequivocally **not** always right. (See Customer Retention Tactics.) In fact, I’d argue that the customer is most often not right in just about any situation. That doesn’t stop me from fighting for my rights as a consumer but I believe that we’ve gone too far to the other end of the spectrum as business owners. We indulge every possible moronic action, complaint and statement from a customer in case they say something on social media or don’t come back and buy from us again. Enough. 

The fix for this issue is to clearly define who you want your customer to be. Who is the ideal person to buy from you? What do they look like, act like, sound like and how do they treat you and your team? If you don’t have that customer, you need to rethink how you are acquiring leads. 

If the customer is always right, there is no way for you to develop your product offering and engage in a fast-moving world. You’re always waiting for the customer to tell you what they want. The best way to build a business is via a symbiotic relationship with your customer, not a master-slave one. 

## Trust your gut

This is terrible business advice because it is misleading. I firmly believe you should gut-check decisions and follow your instincts if you have a deep understanding of the situation you are in. But that’s just the first step. Once you have heard your gut you need to go out and find data to support your feeling. Don’t proceed on a whim; if you do, understand the risks involved and weigh them up against the rewards. 

The addition of data to your instincts will put you in a much better position than simply listening to your gut and nothing else. 

## Go big or go home

This generic, clichéd advice is doled out by advisors with nothing to lose and no skin in the game. They will tell you to shoot for the stars, dive headfirst into the problem and go big because it’s the only way to go. 

They are wrong. There are many other ways to create a valuable and sustainable business. One of them is to take it slow, scale relative to the market opportunity and feel your way through the ups and downs in front of you. 

Also, it’s OK to go big *and* go home. To have large ambitions, to work hard, to dedicate yourself to your business but also take some time off, go home, see your family and rest. 

## It’s just business, don’t take it personally

This is some of the most insulting advice I’ve ever been given. As small business owners, we pour everything we have into our businesses. It *is* personal. I spend more time working to build my business than with my family, my friends, sleeping, eating, exercising or any other activity. Hell yes, business is personal and I like it that way.

But that doesn’t mean you should take it personally. 

I have learned that everyone is mostly just trying to do their best. Sure there are bad people who do bad things in business. These are the people who you should approach with caution but overall it’s not about you. Someone’s bad decision or dodgy deal is more about them and their issues. You need to focus on what you can control and only work with people who share your values. As much as possible, don’t let your feelings towards someone influence the way you do business. The best revenge is to build well\!

## Protect your idea at all costs

Gone are the days of ideas being useful. They’re not. Not in business. Ideas are only as good as their execution and too many entrepreneurs hold onto their ideas as if they are certifiably worth a billion dollars. 

The opposite is true. Your idea is worthless. It’s also not new and it’s also probably not unique. But it can (and most probably will) still work if you just stop protecting it. Get it out into the world, talk about it, share it with people, get their feedback and listen carefully to how they react when you talk about it. 

Sure, if you hold on to your idea, nobody will steal it but if you hold onto it too tightly, it’ll never see the light of day. 

## Follow your passion

I am passionate about dystopian futuristic novels and love reading about space travel. It’s very unlikely that I’m able to turn either of those passions into a business. Telling you to follow your passion is harmful advice, because there are very few things in life that provide unabated joy and your passions are where that joy usually comes from. 

I believe you should be passionate about your business or idea but you should not turn your passions into businesses. Eventually, you will be running a business, not enjoying the passion that first got you interested. 

Leave your passions for self-care, enjoyment and downtime but be passionate about the business that you are building. 

However, I do believe that it is the intersection of our passions and our work, our personal life and our professional, our family and our friends that makes each of us interesting and unique. It’s the smashing together of unexpected things that make us who we are. You might be an accountant in the day, a keen birdwatcher on the weekends, an illustrator of children’s stories at night and a fantastic baker when you find the time. These things provide you with a unique experience of the world and give you a unique perspective when solving problems or engaging with people at work. Don’t ignore your passions but please, don’t turn every passion into a business. 

## No risk, no reward

Most people believe that entrepreneurs take huge risks at every possible turn and bet the farm on one move, one idea or one decision. This may be true for some entrepreneurs but I believe that most risks need to be small, frequent and balanced with insights gathered over time. 

You don't want to be like the CEO of a tech startup who's standing at a roulette table and placing the entire future of the company on black or red. That kind of risk is foolish and always ends in tears. 

Of course, there is risk involved in building a business, so why pile on more risk with every decision? Sometimes the smart move is the cautious one. Many small and calculated risks will often have a more long-lasting and positive effect on your business than a single massive bet that might destroy what you have built. 

## Innovate or die

Innovation is a word that has been buzzing about for a few years. Prevailing advice will lead you to believe that if you aren’t innovating, you are dying. Here’s the thing, though: for most small businesses innovation is a distraction when you are trying to survive. 

The things that matter can include great customer support, faster service, a slightly better product than your competitors or even something as simple as a slightly cheaper price than your customers can find anywhere else. 

Remember that innovation is not an action and innovation probably can’t pay your bills at the end of the month. Rather stick to consistent improvements to your existing business. 

## Every customer matters

This is true. I actually like this advice but I think it’s been twisted over time and needs to be realigned for small businesses. 

Yes, every customer and every sale really does matter but some customers are bad for your business. We all know these customers and probably have a few of them on our books. They are the customers who insist on discounts, never pay on time, never give you more work but are always the most demanding and most difficult to make happy. 

These customers do more damage than good. It’s OK to fire them. It’s OK to find the perfect customer and double down on them. 

## Move fast and break things

This famous advice popularised by Facebook in its early days is extremely dangerous to small businesses. At the centre of this statement is a concept that makes sense: don’t delay, make quick decisions and react. But it’s the “break things” that is terrible advice for most businesses. 

To manage the breakage a business needs to be resilient, needs growth, needs the best people doing their best work and needs deep pockets to recover. This does not describe most small businesses. 

Most small businesses cannot afford to break things because that will put them under. Most small businesses need to move fast but thoughtfully, not to break things. Take risks but make them calculated and carefully thought out. Do not just move fast, break things and try to fix them later. You will end up with a broken business, broken staff and broken customers. 

## Say yes to every opportunity

As a small business owner, you probably feel the need to seriously consider every opportunity that comes your way. This, however, is a mistake. 

Saying yes to everything means that you have no focus. Saying yes to an opportunity because it may lead to short-term income that eases your cash flow for a month might feel good right now but will leave you off course and out of sorts when you attempt to get back to your core business. 

It might be difficult but focus is really the only way to build a sustainable business. If you say yes to every opportunity, client or job, what will your business become? A PR firm should stick to PR as much as possible or risk becoming a PR firm that does social media, media buying, website design, logo design, TV commercials and anything else that is even remotely related to advertising or marketing. This is not a good place to be if your skillset as a business is PR. 

Say no more often than you say yes and clearly define the kind of work and the kind of customer your business wants.

# Much better advice

At the peak of the first wave of the global COVID-19 pandemic, I stumbled across a quickly overused and then forgotten word, “Coronacoaster”. I latched onto it not only as a human being caught in a global pandemic but also as a long-time entrepreneur. When you own a business life is a rollercoaster. One day you are closing the biggest deal ever, and the next your best employee leaves you and joins your competitor. It all happens so intensely and so quickly that the highs and lows can sometimes blend into each other. 

Throw in the Coronacoaster and you are bound to feel ill and start looking for the nearest exit. 

It’s in the deepest and darkest lows that we react in strange and sometimes questionable ways. But experience has taught us to resist the knee-jerk reactions to insanity, for it’s on the lowest part of the rollercoaster that you need to hold tightest to prepare for the climb to the top and the highs when you get there, even if temporarily.

Don’t make irrational decisions, don’t pivot too far from your existing business if you don’t have to, don’t “go digital” because everyone is telling you to, don’t rush, but at the same time don’t wait too long to act, and don’t be too hard on yourself. 

It’s difficult to know what the right moves are but one thing is for certain, moving is necessary.

There is a surplus of advice about all the things we should be doing to survive and thrive in a time of crisis. I wanted to take some time to think about the things that entrepreneurs and business leaders should not be doing or doing differently.  

## Communication

Try not to overcommunicate with your audience for no apparent reason. Yes you are trying to engage your customers and remind them that you exist and urge them to buy something from you but do they really need to receive a newsletter from you every day? I don’t think so. 

When you do communicate with them, try to make sure the content is relevant and adds some kind of value to their lives. 

## Discounting

The deeper you discount, the more you erode your margins for the long term. Your gut will tell you to discount until people start buying again, but this reactionary move wil in all likelihood do more damage than good in the medium to long term. 

Discounts can work if they are not your de facto method of sales acquisition, so use them sparingly and instead try to focus on new product development, and providing added value on purchases and upsells. 

## Expenses

Cutting too deeply can cause unexpected problems in your business. If you acquire customers through search ads and you cut that budget, you will have to acquire customers elsewhere or lose your revenue. If you retrench too many staff members, who will do the work? Figure out a reasonable budget and stick with it as you reassess your situation on a weekly basis. It makes more sense to create new revenue opportunities while cutting costs so that you can try to ensure your business stays afloat or even grows through this tough period. 

## Sales and marketing

Do not stop selling. You think you are selling aggressively but you probably aren’t. Have you made enough phone calls to new prospects today? Have you communicated with existing customers for referrals? Have you considered a new province or country to sell into? Are you looking at email marketing, social media ads and competitions to discover new clients? YouTube ads, referral programmes and other sales tactics to increase your figures? 

We tend to fall back into old habits when it comes to sales but there is a very big world of opportunity out there if you are obsessed enough to find them. 

## Vanity metrics

Getting lost in "busy work" is one way to sink your business. Vanity metrics are the ones that look good externally (they make you feel good) but actually don’t make any difference to your bottom line. Say you have grown your Instagram account by 100 followers but have no new sales to speak of. Sure, now you have 10 000 followers but how many are buying from you? If this truth hits close to home, you may be focused on the wrong metrics. It’s time to focus on increasing sales. 

## Health

For the first six weeks of lockdown I sat on a bench and worked at a table that I fashioned from a mirror, bedside table and my bed. It was brutal and without realising it I was destroying my back and neck. I forgot to take care of myself. Entrepreneurs think that we should be at the bottom of our own priority list. This is the worst thing you could do for your business. Put yourself at the top of your priority list, stay fit and healthy and everything will seem a little bit easier. 

## Pivot

Pivoting is all the rage. But do not pivot for the sake of pivoting (see Section 01 Starting). The worst thing you can do right now is move away from your experience, your core business model and the business tools at your disposal. Pivoting in the right way, however, is a great idea. 

The key to a reasonable pivot is to assess what your business can do, what tools you have, what skills you have and how much runway you have to experiment. Once all of this is laid out, pivot towards something that can provide short-term revenue and long-term sustainability. Yes, you need to survive in the short term but when the economy opens up what will you be left with if you have pivoted away from everything you knew and have no skills or sales to speak of?

# 11\. WHERE TO FROM HERE?

Do. Not. Die. 

That’s probably some of the best business advice I’ve ever read. I read this brilliantly obvious bit of advice in an essay from one of the most successful and intelligent founders and investors in the world, Paul Graham (paulgraham.com). 

Here’s how Graham put it: 

*“If you can just avoid dying, you get rich. That sounds like a joke, but it's actually a pretty good description of what happens in a typical startup. It certainly describes what happened in Viaweb. We avoided dying till we got rich.*

*It was really close, too. When we were visiting Yahoo to talk about being acquired, we had to interrupt everything and borrow one of their conference rooms to talk down an investor who was about to back out of a new funding round we needed to stay alive. So even in the middle of getting rich we were fighting off the grim reaper.*

*You may have heard that quote about luck consisting of opportunity meeting preparation. You've now done the preparation. The work you've done so far has, in effect, put you in a position to get lucky: you can now get rich by not letting your company die.”*

Graham is the founder of Ycombinator, which is one of the leading startup incubators in the world, having incubated businesses like AirBnB, Dropbox, Doordash, Stripe, Reddit, Flutterwave and hundreds more. He knows a thing or two about not dying. 

So that’s where you’re headed. The world of not dying and not killing yourself to build what you believe is a good business. 

You cannot, must not, suffer from the Sacrifice Fallacy. You cannot build a sustainable business for yourself, your team or your clients and suppliers if you put yourself at the bottom of your priority list. That’s insane and it’s frustrating to me that the narrative around business building is that you have to suffer to build something amazing. 

Sure, there are times when you should work hard and long hours, but there has to be a specific time cap on this kind of work. It can’t be indefinite. There also has to be a damn good reason for it to happen in the first place. A good reason isn’t an extra tiny bit of cash or because your boss said so or because your client asked nicely. Those are terrible reasons to sacrifice your mental and physical health. If you’re working 18-hour days, that’s fine but tell me when those days are going to end and give me a reason for the work. 

The real problem kicks in when hard and long work becomes your accepted normal. This is where digital fatigue and burnout are allowed to creep in. This is when the insane becomes sane. This is when the unusual becomes usual and slowly kills your will to work, your sense of self and your ability to acknowledge success. Sound familiar? Then you probably need to take a break and rethink what normal work looks like. It’s not 12-hour days, I promise. 

Everything in my life can be broken into two main categories – additive or subtractive. I mean everything. Family, friends, work, exercise, sleep, food, video games, books, music, conversations, meals, etc. Everything. 

If it isn’t net additive, it’s net subtractive and that has to change. If my friends mock me more than they support me, they have to change. If my family berates me more than they love me, that has to change. If my business costs me more mentally than it stimulates me mentally, that has to change. I know, I know, you’re reading this and thinking about the outliers in your life – your family, your kids, your dogs, your boss who pays your salary. Yes, it’s hard to think of these things in such binary ways but the truth is, you get to choose. I’ve said it already but I want to repeat myself, you are not a tree, you do not have roots, you can change and replant yourself. You don’t like your husband? Leave him. You don’t like your co-founder? Fire her or leave the business yourself. You can’t live without your chief technical officer but she’s a mean asshole? FIRE HER\!

First, be happy. Find the things that are additive and niche down into them. Figure out how to get more of that in your life. Bottle it and drink it every day. Manufacture that shit and give it to your team and get them on the same high you’re on. Additive, *not* subtractive. 

That’s what our businesses, jobs, colleagues, friends, family, habits, marketing, sales, operations, culture and everything else should be: additive and not subtractive.
