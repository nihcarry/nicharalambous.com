Waiting is a calculated move, use it wisely

My psychologist told me that I have an addiction to anxiety.

When he uttered those words everything changed. I immediately acknowledged that he was absolutely correct. It was like the curtains had been opened in a pitch black room of my mind. I used to love being anxious. I used to thrive on the feeling that anxiety brings with it, on the release of endorphins and adrenaline that helps me focus when there is anxiety afoot. For a long time I struggled to exist and be a functioning person without being anxious.

Then in my next session, he dropped another mind-bomb that shattered my understanding of myself;

Waiting is an action.

This appears to be a very simple statement in and of itself but if you combine my addiction to anxiety with my inability to wait for anything to happen you are left in a puddle of anger and frustration. I found myself creating things to be anxious about, complain about and become irate about.

I was a wreck.

He went on to explain that making plans is good and that executing plans is also good. It was my inability to wait for the plan to work that was destroying any real progress in my life. I am an impatient person by nature so it’s not shock to me that waiting is a skill that I needed to refine but before this moment I had never understood the relevance of waiting.

By reframing waiting as an action, an activity, something I was doing, my psychologist had completely turned my impatience on its head. Once waiting was turned into a move and not a lack of a move I became happy to wait for things to play out. Waiting became an integral part of my strategy and now everywhere I look I see smart people and amazing business waiting for their plans to play out.

Refining my ability to be patient has lowered my levels of anxiety immensely. I am able to let things go, wait for them to work out or not work out and then plan again while I am waiting.

A fantastic example of waiting as a strategy is a chameleon in nature. It waits patiently, positioned where it believes prey will fly or crawl by (strategy). It is hidden in plain sight with a plan that no one else can see or grasp. Then, at the perfect moment, it pounces and puts an attack plan into action. Its prey devoured in a one swift fling of the tongue. Waiting as an action.

There is a fine balance between apathy and waiting. It’s quite difficult to balance all the things you are potentially waiting for but I believe that taking a few minutes to mull things over is an important way to exist in your everyday life, not only in your grand plan.

If you are anxious, I urge you to stop, take two deep breaths and then give it five minutes before you respond. Start there and teach yourself that waiting is, in fact, an action.
