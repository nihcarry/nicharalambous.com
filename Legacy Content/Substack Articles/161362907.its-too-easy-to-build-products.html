Over the past 6 months I’ve been completely engaged in building ideas. I have ideas all the time. My grandfather was an ideas man. He hacked his car alarm system so that only he knew how to unlock and start his car. “It’s a new kind of car alarm!” he exclaimed often.

Idea after idea pops into my mind. Day or night. Awake or asleep. In a meeting or sitting reading a book, ideas just flood into my mind. It’s pretty tiresome for those around me I am sure. Shit, it’s tiresome for me and my poor little brain.

In my life, ideas need to go somewhere. They need to live somewhere. They need to exist even if that means in my idea trap for me to revisit later.

Quick sidenote: the concept of an idea trap was gifted to me by Rich Mulholland. The concept is really simple: I have a single digital notes app that I use exclusively to write down ideas when they come to me. It’s digital so I always have access to it on my phone (unlike pen/paper for most people) and it helps me trap the ideas from floating away. This is the first time I’ve ever shown my idea trap publicly. I bet it’s more confusing than interesting!

Aaaand that’s basically how I’ve been managing with my ideas for decades. I write them down in my idea trap and then forget about them because it’s too complex and costly to build anything, or I need a cofounder, designer or engineer.

With the explosion of the AI revolution, building has become easier and easier. Turning ideas into real working prototypes is basically free ($20pm for Replit ← fyi that’s an affiliate link, I earn Replit credits if you sign up) and instead of writing my ideas down, I now build them.

For the past 6 months that’s what I’ve been doing; building.

As you can tell from the side project updates I post in every newsletter, building has become fun and easy and fast. Every newsletter there’s something new. But I’m not sure that’s a good thing. Everyone is talking about AI enabling people to build their own apps, solve their own problems and run 1-person businesses. This may be true but the question I have is: Will everyone want to?

I don’t think so.

But that’s not the point I’m trying to make here (that’s another newsletter coming soon).

For my next project I am taking my time, planning, thinking, and using AI as my co-founder. It’s helping me plan the project out, scope and spec the stages, select the correct AI to use for the specific task at hand and keep me on track and accountable.

The next thing I am building is a language learning app. Yes, I know there are many, many apps out there but none have helped me actually learn to be conversational.

That’s my focus - become conversational in Dutch by the end of the year using my own app. I’m going to build the first simple version of it, test it and then iterate as customer #1.

The premise is simple — spend 15 minutes a day learning 3-5 words per day and become conversational within 8-10 months. There are some changes I want to make to traditional language learning. I speak English fluently but I know three other languages; Greek (read, write, speak badly), Afrikaans (read, write speak badly) and Dutch (read, write, speak badly).

Notice a trend there? I can read and write in four languages but I am only fluent in one. Learning languages often skips the most important part, the speaking, in lieu of grammatical correctness. I don’t care about being proper, I care about conversing. That’s the app I’m building.

Here’s the plan:

  • Prepare all the content (words, definitions, sentences, dialogues, contexts)

  • Prepare the relevant APIs

  • Prepare the wireframes and designs

  • Create the SRS for learning and integrate AI

  • Build the first version

  • Onboard alpha users

  • Test for a few months

  • Roll out to a wider audience

The goal is to learn Dutch. If I can build an app that teaches me to converse in dutch by the end of the year then I think I’m onto something.

The caution for me is that ideas are easy, execution used to be hard. Today? Ideas are are dime a dozen but now so too is execution. I can almost literally build a dozen things for a dime in 2025. It’s cheap and easy and that’s great if I want to solve my own problem temporarily but if I want to build something meaningful that can scale to thousands of people then I need to have a plan. I need to be methodical and I need to be patient. Just because I can build something in one night doesn’t mean I should.

That feels like good advice for life, just because you can doesn’t mean you should.

Stay curious,
